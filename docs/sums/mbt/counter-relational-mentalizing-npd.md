## Counter-Relational Mentalizing with Narcissistic Patients

Working with narcissistic patients generates predictable counter-responses in therapists. The patient's dismissive strategies, imperious attitude, and apparent lack of compassion can become "tiresome." Managing your own reactions is essential to maintaining effective treatment.

**The core stance: See through the noise and seek the pain**

Your primary task is to remain grounded and in contact with the patient's underlying vulnerabilities. The grandiosity, dismissal, and contempt are defensive structures protecting deep shame and fear. Before you respond from your own irritation or frustration, reconnect with what lies beneath the patient's presentation.

**Staying with the patient's perspective**

Remaining "with" the patient's perspective is essential, though at times nearly impossible. You must:
- Continue to hold their worldview without collapsing into agreement
- Simultaneously maintain awareness of the distortions and defenses at play
- Not withdraw your attention or give up

This requires active, compassionate presence even when the patient is being difficult.

**The not-knowing stance as protection**

Rigorous use of the not-knowing stance helps manage your difficult counter-responses. By genuinely not-knowing why the patient reacted as they did, what their dismissal means, or what they actually feel, you:
- Remain curious rather than judgmental
- Create space for the patient's actual experience rather than your interpretation
- Slow down reactive responses
- Model flexibility and openness

**Challenging from compassion, not irritation**

When you must challenge the rigidity of the patient's mental structures, do so from a motive of compassion. Challenging from irritation, resentment, or the wish to prove them wrong will be destructive. Ask yourself: *Am I challenging because I genuinely want to help them see something, or because I'm frustrated?* If the latter, wait.

The affect focus and presentation of your counter-responsiveness (what you are experiencing in the interaction) is what creates pressure for the patient to become more self-reflective about relationship dynamics. Your authentic experience, shared appropriately, is therapeutic data.
