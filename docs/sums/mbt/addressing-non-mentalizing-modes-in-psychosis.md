## Addressing Non-Mentalizing Modes in Psychosis

Non-mentalizing modes in psychosis differ from those in personality disorders or trauma. In psychosis, these modes can take extreme forms: paranoid delusions in psychic equivalence, sudden delusional "conclusions" from misinterpreted actions in teleological mode, or elaborate, detached reasoning in pretend mode. The clinician's task is to gently maintain alternative perspectives while avoiding interventions that paradoxically deepen the non-mentalizing state.

### The Intervention Framework

The framework depicted in the text maps six key steps, grounded in the initial formulation:

**1. Identify idiosyncratic triggers.** From the outset, you know what types of situations, emotions, or thoughts tend to precede the patient's loss of mentalizing. (Attachment stress, intense affect, social evaluation, cognitive complexity.) Keep these in mind as you work.

**2. Characterize mentalizing impairments.** From initial assessment, you understand the patient's baseline mentalizing capacity—where they are strong and where they fragment easily. This informs how you pitch interventions.

**3. Validate and offer alternative perspective.** When the patient begins to lose mentalizing and become increasingly unable to appreciate other viewpoints, the clinician validates the patient's experience while simultaneously, gently, offering a non-threatening alternative. This is the heart of the work.

*Example:*

- **Patient:** "Everyone on the bus was looking at me as if I was crazy. I had to get off and walk here."
- **Clinician:** "That sounds like a terrible experience, to feel everyone looking at you like that. Did walking here help with that feeling?"
- **Patient:** "Kind of..."
- **Clinician:** "I see. Is it possible that you think we have these same thoughts here?"
- **Patient:** "Yes..."
- **Clinician:** "Well, thanks for sharing this, Johanna. I realize that must not be easy for you at the moment. I just want to tell you that, as far as I can tell from my thoughts, I'm currently focused on understanding how that feels for you, and I'm not really thinking—again, as far as I can tell—that you are crazy. That's not a thought that crossed my mind."

Notice: The clinician validates ("that sounds terrible"), stays curious about the effect ("did it help?"), gently mirrors the patient's fear ("is it possible you think we think that?"), and then explicitly marks their own thought process without being defensive.

**4. Avoid complicated mental-state or hyper-symbolic talk.** As the patient retreats into non-mentalizing, shift away from abstract mentalizing language. Don't ask complex questions about their feelings or interpretations. Don't use metaphor or symbol-heavy language. Keep it concrete and observable: "What are you noticing in your body right now?" rather than "What do you think that reaction means about how you see yourself?"

**5. Contain retreat behavior with a secure environment.** If the patient becomes so dysregulated that they are retreating further or showing signs of psychotic escalation (unusual speech, incoherence, aggressive ideation), the focus shifts. You provide non-threatening feedback, explore options for containing behavior in safe environments, and activate the crisis plan agreed in the formulation.

**6. Provide non-threatening feedback and monitor symptom change.** Track changes in symptoms and severity as outlined in the formulation and crisis plan. If symptoms are worsening, consultation with the prescriber, possible medication adjustment, or increased environmental support may be needed.

### When the Patient Cannot Mentalize

There will be moments when mentalizing is simply not available to the patient. The defensive purpose of non-mentalizing modes is to protect against feelings of fragmentation and meaninglessness. Sometimes you cannot argue the patient out of that protection; you can only:

- Remain present and non-panicked
- Avoid complicated talking
- Offer concrete, grounding support
- Follow the crisis plan
- Document changes for the team
