## Understanding Pre-Mentalizing Modes: Psychic Equivalence and Pretend

Before a child develops a mature capacity to mentalize (around age four), they experience their internal world through two distinct, unintegrated modes: psychic equivalence and pretend mode. In many forms of psychopathology, particularly those linked to early adversity, individuals regress to these earlier modes of functioning, especially under stress. Recognizing these modes is crucial for understanding the client's subjective experience.

### Psychic Equivalence Mode

In this mode, the internal world is equated with the external world. There is no separation between thought and reality; what is in the mind *is* real. The guiding principle is, "How I see it is how it is."

*   **Clinical Presentation:** Clients in this mode experience their thoughts and feelings with absolute certainty and concreteness. A fantasy can be terrifying because it is experienced as a tangible threat (e.g., the "tiger under the bed"). A feeling of badness is not just a feeling; it is proof of being an inherently bad person. Flashbacks in trauma are a powerful example of psychic equivalence, where a memory is not a representation of the past but a terrifying re-experiencing in the present. Reassurance from the therapist is often ineffective because it clashes with the client's perceived reality.

### Pretend Mode

This mode is the developmental complement to psychic equivalence. Here, the internal world is completely decoupled from external reality. Thoughts, feelings, and fantasies are experienced as having no real-world implications or connections.

*   **Clinical Presentation:** This often manifests as dissociation, emotional emptiness, or a sense of meaninglessness. The client may talk about highly distressing events with a notable lack of affect. Their internal state feels unreal, cut off from their life and actions. While this mode protects the individual from the terror of psychic equivalence, it leads to a profound sense of disconnection and emptiness. A client might describe "driving myself mad" with compulsive thinking, a reaction against the void created by the pretend mode.

Healthy development involves integrating these two modes into a mature **mentalizing mode**, where mental states are understood as *representations* of reality—linked to it, but not identical to it. In therapy, clients often oscillate between the terror of psychic equivalence and the emptiness of pretend mode. The therapist's goal is to help the client build the capacity to hold their thoughts and feelings as mental states, bridging the gap between these two extremes.
