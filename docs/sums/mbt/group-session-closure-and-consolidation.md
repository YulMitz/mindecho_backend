## Session Closure: Consolidation and Translation

The closing phase is not an afterthought. It consolidates learning, identifies emerging themes, and bridges group experience to real-world relationships. Without closure, insights evaporate.

### The Closure Process (10–15 minutes)

**1. Clinician summary**: Reflect back the main themes or tensions that emerged. Be concrete: "What I noticed today was a lot of uncertainty about who gets to be in control of a relationship, and how that anxiety shows up differently for each of you."

**2. Member reflection**: Invite members to notice what the themes mean for them personally. "Does this resonate with anything in your own relationships?"

**3. Identify unresolved threads**: "Stanley, we talked about feeling small when your girlfriend asserts independence. That's worth exploring more next week. Can you think about it?"

**4. Note new theme or question**: Write it on a board or in the group summary for next week. Example: "Who am I when I am with someone else?"

### Creating the Group Summary

After closure, write a brief summary capturing:
- Main themes discussed
- Protagonists and their problems
- Key insights or shifts in mentalizing
- Questions to revisit
- Anything left unresolved

Email or print this for members before the next session. This:
- Encourages reflection during the week
- Creates continuity and accountability
- Signals that the clinician was truly listening
- Normalizes ongoing thinking about relationships outside sessions

### Watch for Pretend Mode in Closure

If the group suddenly organizes around a general principle (e.g., "Men are naturally in charge"), gently challenge: "That's convenient, but let's not lose sight of what we were actually exploring about each of you. Stanley, you said you feel small; Sarah, you said you don't know what you want. Let's not abstract away from that."
