## Reducing the "Inner Eye": Shifting from Self to Other Mentalizing

One of the most important therapeutic priorities in AvPD is **reducing the relentless focus on the self as the primary source of explanation for social outcomes**. The client's "inner eye" is turned inward—constantly monitoring and judging themselves—when it should be trained outward toward genuinely understanding others.

### Why Egocentricity Matters

Egocentricity bias generates as much or more psychological pain as the avoidance of others' mental states. The client suffers doubly: they avoid social situations out of shame, and they miss the opportunity to discover that others' perspectives might not match their negative self-image. Breaking this cycle requires **persistent, compassionate, and curious exploration** rather than confrontation.

### The Two-Part Intervention

**Part 1: Emergence of a Genuine Other**

Help the client gradually see the other person from multiple angles, not just through their own shame-colored lens. Use a systematic approach:

- **Their own perspective**: "What do you think was going on in their mind? What were they thinking or feeling?" Start here to honor the client's attempt at mentalizing, but gently probe for evidence.

- **Their imagination vs. certainty**: "How come you're so certain about that? What did you actually observe, and what did you add in your own mind?" This naming of the distinction between observed fact and imagined interpretation is crucial.

- **An observer's perspective**: "If I had been there, not knowing you or caring about what you think of yourself, what would I have noticed?" Offer your own alternative reading: "I might have thought they were just preoccupied, or curious about your work, not judging you at all."

- **Other perspectives in the scene**: "How did that other person see the colleague? What did their reaction tell you about what they understood?" This triangulation helps break the lock of self-focused interpretation.

**Part 2: Inhibiting Egocentricity**

Egocentricity cannot be overcome by demolishing it (confrontation backfires). Instead, **build an attractive alternative** by identifying and amplifying positive cognitions about the other person: admiration, affection, concern, interest, or even love. This intrinsic reward can override avoidance.

The persistent request is: "Move beyond egocentricity." Not as a demand, but as an invitation into a richer, less painful way of understanding the social world.

### Clinical Example: From Self to Other

**Client**: "They looked at me in a funny way. They were seeing me as weird."

**Therapist (curious, not challenging)**: "Help me understand. What did you pick up—was it their facial expression, their tone of voice, something else?"

**Client**: "The way they looked at me. It was weird."

**Therapist**: "So you saw something in their face. What else were you thinking or imagining in that moment?"

**Client**: "I just know they think I'm strange."

**Therapist**: "I hear that you're certain about that. What do you think they could have been thinking instead? Not what you believe, but what might another explanation be?"

**Client**: "I don't know... maybe they were just thinking about something else?"

**Therapist**: "That's possible. If they were preoccupied, what would that tell you about what they were experiencing in that moment? What might have been on their mind?"

This gradual movement—from observation to interpretation to alternative reading to the other's interior world—is the core contrary move in AvPD work.
