## Exploring "Me-Mode": How Others See You

"Me-mode" refers to the client's representation of themselves *as experienced by others*—the self-as-object. This is inherently unstable because it depends on interpersonal context, but it is crucial terrain in narcissistic work. Many narcissistic clients are acutely sensitive to (real or imagined) negative judgments from others, yet simultaneously avoid genuine curiosity about how they are actually perceived.

### Why Me-Mode is Threatening

A client with grandiose narcissism has constructed a protective bubble of self-importance that insulates them from others' perspectives. A client with vulnerable narcissism may be hyperalert to any sign of criticism or rejection and organize their life around preventing perceived judgment.

In both cases, genuine exploration of how others see them creates existential risk: *What if their negative sense is true?* The "me" is context-dependent—it changes with circumstance—making self-experiences rooted in others' perceptions inherently unstable and threatening to an already fragile core.

### Gentle Inquiry into Other-Perspective

Begin by asking the client how they think others see them, using curiosity rather than challenge:

- "How do you imagine your colleagues see you?"
- "What impression do you think your partner has of you?"
- "When you say people don't appreciate you, what do you think they see instead?"

Pay close attention to:

**Disquiet, frustration, or anger.** If the question triggers defensiveness rather than reflection, the client is not yet ready for deep me-mode work. Return to I-mode validation.

**Black-and-white thinking.** "Everyone hates me" or "Nobody understands my true value." This signals that the client is experiencing others' perceptions as absolute truth (psychic equivalence) rather than as subjective interpretations that might change.

**Lack of curiosity.** If the client is convinced they already know what others think and shows no genuine interest in exploring alternatives, note this: "You sound quite certain about how they see you. I'm curious whether that's always the case, or whether there might be times when people's views of you surprise you."

### Affective Narratives and Shame Contact

As me-mode becomes safer, the clinician can use detailed narratives of specific relational events to help the client access the emotional reality of being perceived negatively.

**Example:** A client describes an incident where they ended a relationship by ghosting their partner. Rather than analyze the client's justifications, explore: "When you imagine how Amanda experienced that—the phone call, then being blocked, unable to even have a conversation—what do you imagine that was like for her? And how do you feel now, thinking about it from her side?"

This is not about inducing guilt. It is about expanding the client's capacity to hold two perspectives simultaneously (their own and another's) without collapsing into shame or retreating into defensive superiority.

### The "Me-Mode" Vulnerability

Many narcissistic presentations are organized around a core fear of being seen as inadequate, failing, or unlovable. The grandiose armor ("I'm better than everyone") and the vulnerable collapse ("Everyone hates me, I'm a failure") are two sides of the same coin: intolerance of being perceived as ordinary or flawed.

As the client gains stability in I-mode, they can begin to tolerate the idea that:
- Others may see them differently than they see themselves
- This difference does not make them fundamentally damaged or worthless
- People's perceptions change and are not absolute
- Being seen as imperfect is survivable
