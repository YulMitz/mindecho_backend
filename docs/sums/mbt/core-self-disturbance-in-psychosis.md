## Core Self-Disturbance and Non-Mentalizing Cycles in Psychosis

In MBT, "disturbances in core self" refers to moments when patients lose their stable sense of themselves and shift into non-mentalizing modes—ways of experiencing reality that bypass reflective thought about minds. These disturbances are not incidental; they are central to psychotic processes.

### What Happens During Core Self-Disturbance

When a person with psychosis experiences a disturbance in core self, their sensory and cognitive processes become decalibrated—disconnected. They may notice shifts in body posture, gaze, or speech patterns before any obvious change in mood. The individual loses the ability to integrate incoming information into a coherent sense of self. This is different from the intense affective experiences seen in personality disorders; in psychosis, the disconnect can be sudden and may not be accompanied by obvious emotional arousal.

In response to this fragmentation, the patient attempts to regain some agency by shifting into non-mentalizing modes: psychic equivalence (where thoughts feel absolutely real), teleological mode (where only actions seem to matter), or pretend mode (where thinking becomes detached from reality). At the extreme, these modes can manifest as fixed delusions, hyper-reflectivity, or bizarre interpretations of ordinary events.

### The Cycle of Non-Mentalizing

The text describes a six-point cycle that explains how core self-disturbance triggers and maintains non-mentalizing:

1. **Impaired mentalizing** results from the threat to self-integrity itself. Just as threats shut down mentalizing in any individual, psychotic patients lose capacity to reflect on minds.

2. **Loss of perspective-taking** follows. The patient becomes rigid, jumping to conclusions and unable to consider contrary evidence or alternative viewpoints.

3. **Non-mentalizing modes take over** as a protective attempt to maintain minimal agency over selfhood when the mentalizing part of the mind is offline.

4. **Verbal/symbolic challenge worsens the disconnect.** Cognitive interventions, direct interpretations, or reality-testing questions can actually widen the gap between what the patient feels and what they intellectually "know," triggering further retreat.

5. **Retreat into subjective viewpoint** occurs as the patient withdraws to reduce external stimulation and preserve internal coherence—a protective but isolating response.

6. **Symptom maintenance and exacerbation** result. Over time, reliance on non-mentalizing modes reinforces psychotic symptoms and entrenches delusional narratives as the primary way the patient understands self, others, and world.

### Idiosyncratic Triggers

Three broad domains activate disturbances in core self:
- **Intense affective experiences** (strong emotions, arousal)
- **Social evaluation** (being judged, scrutinized, or excluded)
- **Cognitive complexity** (tasks requiring multiple steps, ambiguity, rapid processing)

Identifying a patient's personal triggers is essential for case formulation and anticipating when mentalizing will collapse.

### Clinical Implications

Understanding this cycle explains why direct confrontation of delusions is often counterproductive. It also clarifies why mentalizing work is only appropriate for patients in stage 2 or 3 of psychotic illness (those managing in outpatient care), not for fully decompensated patients in acute crisis. In decompensation, the patient's mind is too fragmented for mentalizing; what they need is environmental containment and kind, non-dehumanizing care from people who maintain their own mentalizing capacity.
