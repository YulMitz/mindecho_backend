## Engaging ASPD Patients: Acceptance and Reframing

Engagement with antisocial patients is fundamentally different from engagement with other personality disorder presentations. Where patients with BPD often seek therapy to relieve distress, ASPD patients enter "therapy" with a practical external goal—usually related to social systems: custody access, housing restoration, child protection involvement, or legal status.

**The critical distinction: Their goal vs. your goal**

At the initial interview, treatment is usually far from the patient's mind. What is paramount is garnering support for their argument with authorities. They may say: *"I need a report saying I'm safe to see my children,"* or *"I need housing support restored."* 

Your impulse may be to use this as a window to deeper work on mentalizing, violence reduction, or prosocial behavior. This is a mistake. If you do so, the patient will experience you as just another authority figure imposing aims they don't own. They will engage in "pretend therapy"—attending sessions, telling you what you want to hear, but never truly invested.

**The engagement strategy: Three core principles**

1. **Accept the person's stated practical aim as legitimate**

Do not try to establish your own psychological and behavioral aims (mentalizing stabilization, violence reduction, prosocial behavior) from the outset. The patient will experience this as manipulation or judgment.

Instead: Affirm that their practical goal makes sense. *"You want access to your children restored. That's understandable. Let's work on what would need to happen for that to be safe and possible."*

2. **Be ordinarily human and use the not-knowing stance**

Do not make assumptions or judgments. Do not launch into formulation or interpretation. Speak naturally. Be curious without agenda.

*"Tell me about your children. What are they like?"* (not *"Let's explore your capacity to parent"*)
