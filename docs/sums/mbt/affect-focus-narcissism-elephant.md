## Affect Focus and the Elephant in the Room with Narcissism

The characteristic relational dynamic that develops between therapist and narcissistic patient must be identified, lived through, and explored. This dynamic is the "elephant in the room"—an emotionally salient interactional pattern that is expressed but not verbalized, often remaining implicit and automatic.

**Recognizing the elephant: Your counter-response as data**

Your counter-response is often the first signal of an unexpressed emotionally significant process. With narcissistic patients, you may notice:
- Everything you say is dismissed, even when said politely
- The patient's tone sounds reasonable, but you feel unappreciated and increasingly irritated
- You find yourself over-explaining: "No, that's not right," "I don't think this is relevant," "Perhaps I need to explain more"
- You experience worry (e.g., about suicide risk) that the patient disavows, and this worry becomes increasingly insistent

This irritation, confusion, or concern is data. It signals a subdominant theme that is interfering with treatment.

**The task: Moving from implicit to explicit, from automatic to reflective**

There are two goals when working with the elephant:

1. Move the interactional dynamic from automatic (implicit, non-conscious) to explicit and conscious
2. Explore it from a point of equity—where you and the patient are both parts of a system to which you both contribute

You do this by openly identifying some of the dynamic processes as you become aware of them. Your counter-relational experience may be the initial indicator.

**Clinical example: Naming the dynamic**

Instead of becoming irritated and ending the session, name it:

*"You sound very confident about how you would manage such a disappointment. From my perspective I worry a little more than you seem to. You are sensitive to others' criticism. It worries me that when I ask you more about it you push me away. I would hate to miss something important here."*

Or in the moment:

*"I notice that as soon as I start to bring up your difficulties with others, you seem to close off and I feel that you've made up your mind. I feel you make me out to be wrong before I even get started."*

**When challenge is appropriate: Timing and motive**

Challenge to rigidity should be:
- Avoided when you feel intense annoyance (wait until compassion dominates)
- Sensitively done with vulnerable narcissism (may trigger shame and compromise mentalizing)
- More direct with grandiose narcissism, though still carefully timed
- Only deployed when the therapeutic alliance is robust

High-risk challenge (used sparingly): *"I really cannot get my mind around the fact that all my attempts to understand your difficulties are a 'waste of space.' You and I need to decide what makes you come to see someone who you regard as so useless."* Hold the line while showing you want to solve the problem, not end treatment.
