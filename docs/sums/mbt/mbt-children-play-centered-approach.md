## Play as the Vehicle for Mentalizing in MBT-C

Play is at the heart of MBT-C. Research shows that pretend play is associated with improved reflective functioning (mentalizing), and that play mediates the relationship between trauma and later mentalizing capacity. During play, the therapist can gradually help the child become aware of mental states — their own and others' — in a non-threatening way.

### The Progression of Play-Based Mentalizing Techniques

**Level 1: Describing the Play Process**

At its simplest, the therapist describes, summarizes, and integrates what the child is doing during play:
- "You're making the doll jump high"
- "That car is going really fast"
- This mirrors and validates the child's activity without adding interpretation

**Level 2: Stimulating the Play Narrative**

For children who are anxious, inhibited, or withdrawn, ask gentle questions to help them elaborate the story:
- "What's happening in your game?"
- "Why did the doll do that?"
- "What happens next?"

This technique:
- Helps the therapist understand the play context
- Encourages the child to elaborate and add detail
- Creates a shared narrative
- Is parallel to clarification and exploration used in MBT for adults

**Level 3: Mentalizing the Play**

With children capable of symbolic play, the therapist can verbalize the narrative and explore the thoughts/feelings behind it:
- "It looks like the doll is feeling scared of the other children"
- "Maybe the character is wondering if anyone likes her?"

The therapist remains **cautious and wondering** (not-knowing):
- "I wonder if…" rather than declarative statements
- The child corrects or elaborates the therapist's account
- The goal is to stimulate mentalizing in a **playful and undogmatic** way
- Different perspectives are introduced respectfully
- The child's play is valued; their agency is treated as interesting

### Alternatives When Symbolic Play Doesn't Fit

Not all children engage in pretend play. Alternatives include:

**For older children (who feel they've "outgrown" play):**
- Drawing, painting, clay work
- Board games
- Other structured activities

**For younger or inhibited children:**
- Body-based games: throwing/catching, sword fighting, music
- Sand box or clay work
- Rhythm and ritual-based activities
- These still involve interaction, rhythm, and opportunity for experimentation

### Marked Mirroring for Dysregulated Children

For children who are chaotic, disorganized, and unregulated in their actions:

**Contingent coordination** using nonverbal modalities:
- Soothing sounds with soft tone of voice
- Facial expressions full of compassion
- Predictable, regulated responsiveness
- Joint attention and "being together"

**Marked mirroring** — reflecting back the child's physical actions in a regulated way:
- "That's a big loud noise, you hit so hard!"
- Provides awareness of self and how it's experienced by others
- Uses the therapist's own emotional regulation to model managing intensity
- Children in school often meet aggression with punishment (rejection); here they meet it with understanding

Once contingent coordination is established, the therapist can begin to **label and name feeling states**, using tools like picture books or asking the child to tell a story about a character.
