## Reflective Fostering: Building Stable, Mentalized Relationships in Care

Approximately 80,000 children in England are in local authority care, with an increasing number in foster placements rather than residential settings. Foster care, when stable and attuned, can be profoundly reparative—but placement instability (22–56% breakdown rates) is associated with severe compounding trauma. Children in foster care are five times more likely to have diagnosable mental health disorders (72% prevalence).

The **Reflective Fostering Programme (RFP)** addresses a critical gap: while traditional foster carer training focuses on managing challenging behavior, it largely ignores the relational and emotional work required to help deeply hurt children develop trust and security.

### The Evidence Base

Research shows that improving a carer's capacity to **mentalize both their own distress and the child's behavior** leads to:
- Better response to worrying behavior (seeing fear or grief beneath defiance)
- Stronger sense of agency and value in the child
- Reduced parental stress in the carer
- Better socio-emotional and cognitive outcomes for the child
- Reduced mental health risk later in life

When a child feels mentalized—truly seen and understood—they develop a sense that they matter and have agency in the world.

### Two Key Tools: Carer Map and Carer APP

The RFP adapts the Reflective Parenting model for the specific context of fostering:

**Carer Map** — Foster carers build self-awareness about:
- Their own stress responses and triggers
- How their state of mind affects caregiving
- When they are most at risk of losing patience or mentalizing capacity

**Carer APP** — A stance toward the child combining:
- **Attention and curiosity** — What is driving this behavior? What past trauma might be activated?
- **Perspective taking** — How does this child see the world after what they've experienced?
- **Providing empathy** — How can I validate their experience while helping them stay safe?

### A 10-Session Group Structure (12–14 weeks)

1. Introduction to the model
2. Carer self-reflection (Carer Map)
3. Understanding the child differently
4. Understanding and responding to developmental/relational trauma
5. Trust, relationships, and helping the child connect with others
6. Responding to problem behavior reflectively
7. Putting Carer Map and APP together
8. Building support systems around the carer
9. Preparing for program end
10. Review and sustaining the practice

Groups are co-facilitated by a social care worker and an experienced foster carer, with weekly consultation from specialists. This distributed expertise builds confidence within the system.

### Impact: Pilot Evidence

Pilot evaluations showed high recruitment, retention, and acceptability. Foster carers reported:
- Reduced stress levels
- Improved relationships with children
- Increased understanding of trauma responses
- Better capacity to stay regulated when children are dysregulated

A large-scale randomized controlled trial is underway to evaluate long-term effectiveness and cost-effectiveness.

### Why Foster Carers Are Often Unsupported

Foster carers carry enormous emotional load—managing a traumatized child's fear, anger, and distancing while often feeling under-trained and under-resourced. Without mentalizing support, carers can become depleted, punitive, or dismissive. The RFP explicitly recognizes that **caregiver mentalizing is just as important as child behavior change**.
