## Trauma's Impact: The Collapse of Mentalization

Childhood maltreatment and attachment trauma can profoundly undermine the development of mentalization. In some cases, it leads to a motivated, defensive aversion to thinking about mental states, as exploring the mind of an abuser is terrifying and dangerous. When mentalization collapses under the weight of trauma, individuals often revert to earlier, pre-mentalistic modes of experiencing reality. The oscillation between these modes is a hallmark of trauma.

### Re-emergence of Psychic Equivalence

In this mode, the internal experience of the trauma is equated with external reality. Thinking about the trauma feels identical to reliving it.
*   **Clinical Picture:** This is most evident in post-traumatic flashbacks, which are compelling, resistant to reason, and feel imminently dangerous until they can be mentalized. Clients may refuse to think or talk about their experiences because doing so is not remembering, but re-experiencing. A client might say, "When you write to someone, it’s like you’re going in that letter... you are the words." This concrete equation of self with symbol is a clear sign of psychic equivalence.

### Intrusion of the Pretend Mode

As a defense against the terror of psychic equivalence, the mind may decouple from reality, leading to the pretend mode.
*   **Clinical Picture:** This manifests as dissociative experiences. The client may report "blanking out," "clamming up," or feeling that nothing has any meaning or implication. Thoughts are cut off from other thoughts and from reality, leading to a profound sense of emptiness and disconnection. This is not a playful fantasy but a profound separation from reality that leaves the person feeling hollow.

### Return to the Teleological Mode

This is a pre-mentalistic mode of understanding the world based on observable physical outcomes rather than internal mental states like beliefs or intentions.
*   **Clinical Picture:** The client believes that minds can only be changed through physical action. Verbal reassurance means little. They may feel that their distress can only be communicated or addressed through concrete acts. A client might describe breaking things or self-harming as "the only way I could make them understand." This is not manipulation, but a regression to a mode where only physical acts are perceived as having the power to influence reality and the minds of others. For these clients, physical assurances of safety and security become paramount, as the mental level of interaction has been compromised.
