## Managing Hypermentalizing and Pretend Mode in AvPD

### The Trap of Sophisticated-Sounding Pseudomentalizing

Avoidantly attached clients often develop the **ability to talk extensively about their psychology** in ways that can fool both them and the therapist. The narratives sound insightful and coherent, but they are actually a defensive elaboration—hypermentalizing in pretend mode—that keeps genuine emotion and relational mentalizing at bay.

### Markers of Hypermentalizing in AvPD

Watch for these red flags:

1. **Excessive repetition and circularity**: The client revisits the same story, the same explanations, the same conclusions repeatedly. Each telling adds more micro-detail but no new understanding.

2. **Overly analytical and self-serving focus**: The narratives focus obsessively on the client's internal experience ("Why am I like this?") or recast events to portray the client as victim of others' neglect. These tend to generate counter-responsive boredom or aggression in the therapist.

3. **Imbalance between cognition and affect**: Either the narrative is so intellectualized that it feels bloodless and empty, or it is affectively flooded but uncontained—neither creates genuine mentalizing.

4. **Inability to shift perspective**: When you try to move the client from self to other (contrary moves), they cannot sustain the shift. They revert to self-focus or become confused.

5. **Communication gap**: The therapist loses the thread. The exploration becomes so detailed and micro-focused that the larger meaning dissolves. Both parties end up in their own "silos," feeling isolated and unsupported.

### The Result: Abandonment Through Mentalizing

From the client's perspective, this pattern confirms their worst fear: **mentalizing leads to rejection**. The therapist becomes exhausted or bored, the client feels abandoned, and both experience a massive communication failure. The client concludes: "See, I'm too complicated, too broken. Even when I try to understand myself, it makes people leave."

### Intervention: Stopping, Naming, and Reorienting

The antidote is not more analysis but **pause, explicit naming, and gentle reorientation**:

1. **Stop gently**: "I notice we've been going deeper and deeper into detail, and I'm losing the thread. Let me pause us here."

2. **Name the process**: "This feels like we're both getting a bit lost in the weeds. This is the kind of thing that happens when we're in that hypermentalizing mode we talked about in our formulation—lots of thinking, but the feeling part gets left behind."

3. **Validate and reorient**: "You're clearly trying hard to understand yourself. And at the same time, I'm wondering if all this detail is actually keeping us away from what's most painful—maybe the loneliness, or the shame, or the fear of being judged?"

4. **Shift focus outward or to affect**: "Let's try something different. Instead of 'Why am I this way?', let's ask 'What happened between you and that person?' Tell me the story, but focus on what you observed—their words, tone, expression—not your interpretation."

### Affirming Emotional Truth Over Cognitive Detail

The client has often learned to prioritize **cognitive explanation over emotional experience**. Flip this: "What matters more right now isn't getting all the details straight. What matters is what you felt in that moment. Can you stay with that feeling for a minute instead of explaining it?"

This shifts mentalizing from pretend mode back toward authentic affective mentalizing—the foundation for genuine connection and understanding.
