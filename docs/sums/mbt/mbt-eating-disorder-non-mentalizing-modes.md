## Non-Mentalizing Modes in Eating Disorders

Eating disorder thinking and behavior are organized around three hallmarks of ineffective mentalizing: psychic equivalence, pretend mode, and teleological mode. Understanding how each operates is essential to guiding clients toward mentalization.

### Psychic Equivalence: "Inside-Out" Thinking

In psychic equivalence, bodily metaphors and internal mental events are experienced as concrete facts rather than representations. The mind and body are not separate—the thought *is* the reality.

**Clinical examples**:
- *"I just knew that thin was attractive, desirable. It was everything that was good."* This is not a thought about beauty; it is experienced as fundamental truth.
- Thinking about cake becomes eating it: *"I would have a 'bad thought' about a cake in a shop window. Then I would think that I had eaten it and then I would have to exercise the corresponding amount to lose the weight."*
- Negative beliefs about self feel utterly real: *"I had no right to exist. I was a fraud. I felt like a cuckoo in the nest, alien, too big, too obtrusive, unwanted."*

The body's appearance becomes equated with the self. There is no "as if"—only "is." This absolute certainty makes change feel impossible; the client is not arguing with a thought but fleeing from a fact.

### Pretend Mode: "Elephant in the Room" Thinking

Pretend mode creates a protective fantasy world disconnected from felt reality. Thoughts and feelings are decoupled from observable truth, creating self-deception and meaninglessness.

**Clinical examples**:
- *"I was cheating, eating things that had no calories in them—cabbage, carrots, celery. But while I was doing it I kind of believed it was nutritious."*
- Social presentation replaces authentic experience: *"I spent a lot of time trying to hide from people... I would try to hide it from people because I thought they would not understand... I felt intruded on—anxious that they would try to stop me."*
- In therapy, pretend mode generates inconsequential narratives: *"After my first session I knew what I had to say. I said it. I just talked about 'what food meant to me.' It meant nothing. She lapped it up. After the sessions I felt quite pleased. 'I could be good at this,' I thought."*

The client is simultaneously deceiving themselves and others. The fantasized identity (controlled, disciplined, untouched by need) is protected by rejecting any alternative reality. This drives the secrecy and isolation typical of eating disorders.
