## Mentalizing Relationships Affected by Trauma and Treatment Closure

Once trauma narratives have been mentalized at the level of internal experience, the work extends to the social world. How did this trauma shape relationships? How do the client's coping strategies now affect their connections with others? This relational exploration mirrors the same structure of affective reflection used for trauma itself.

### Exploring Trauma-Affected Relationships

The clinician guides exploration through four mentalizing components:

1. **Past relationships as they unfolded with the trauma**: How did relationships function during or immediately after the traumatic event? Who knew? Who didn't? What was said or unsaid?

2. **Reflection on past relationships from the current perspective**: Looking back, how do you understand what happened between you and important others? What did they need? What did you need?

3. **Experience of self in the present exploration**: How does it feel now, in this moment, talking about these relationships in the group?

4. **Current relationships and social context**: How are these patterns showing up in your life now? In the group?

A **relational map** is often used—a visual representation of the client's social network. This makes visible the structure of support and disconnection, and becomes a working document throughout treatment. The group naturally helps explore each participant's network, offering observations about patterns and possibilities for repair.

### Phase 3: Review, Reappraisal, and Future Orientation

The final 4–6 weeks of the group are structured differently from the intense trauma processing of Phase 2. Sessions include check-ins, agreed focuses, and time-sharing so all members have space to speak. The tone shifts toward reflection: *What has changed? What have you noticed about yourself and each other?*

**Key questions in closure include**:

- Are you more compassionate toward yourself and forgiving of your difficulties?
- Can you experience your past as "there and then" rather than "here and now"?
- Have you built a more coherent life narrative?
- Do past memories still trigger low-mentalizing modes, or are they less painful and destabilizing?
- Can you think about yourself coherently across time and be present with others, rather than feeling like an outsider?

The clinician explicitly revisits the group's initial values and asks members to reflect: *Are these values still relevant? How will you carry them forward?* This consolidates the mentalization work and shifts the client's identity away from being defined by trauma response toward being defined by capacity for reflection, connection, and self-compassion.

The ending itself is processed: What does ending the group bring up? Does it connect to previous loss or abandonment? This allows the group to hold the experience of ending with the same mentalizing capacity they have developed for trauma—not as re-traumatization but as a manageable, understandable life transition.
