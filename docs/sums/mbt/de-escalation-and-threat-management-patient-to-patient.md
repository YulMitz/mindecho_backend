## De-escalation and Threat Management: Patient-to-Patient

Aggression between group members in MBT-ASPD groups often stems from misinterpretation: one patient mishears another's tone, misreads a facial expression, or makes automatic assumptions about hostile intent. The time between verbal and physical aggression can be very small.

When threats escalate, the clinician's anxiety rises, mentalizing collapses, and the group becomes unsafe. Your task is to retain your own mentalizing and intervene rapidly.

### Immediate De-escalation Techniques

**Intervene as soon as you sense rising tension.** Do not wait to see if it resolves itself.

**Maintain calm verbal and physical presence:**
- Keep your voice level and modulated
- Maintain a relatively neutral facial expression with natural eye contact
- Do not stare at the patient, lean forward, or clench your fists
- Maintain physical distance if the patient is standing
- **Do not touch the patient.** Physical contact in low-mentalizing mode is often perceived as intrusion or attack, triggering escalation.

**Clinical example of how touch can escalate:**
A staff member asked two loud patients to be quiet and unthinkingly touched one on the shoulder. The patient immediately became aggressive: "What did you touch me for? Don't you dare touch me." For someone in low-mentalizing mode, physical contact threatens the continuity of bodily and mental self, and is experienced as dangerous.

### Redirect to Earlier Mentalizing

**Keep talking and suggest returning to an earlier subject when mentalizing was present.** Use "broken-record" repetition if needed—keep calmly restating what you want to do: "Let's rewind and reconsider what was happening earlier."

### Balance Authority with Mentalizing

You have two roles that can conflict: managing the group and facilitating mentalizing. Escalating threats mean mentalizing has collapsed. **This is when you take authority**—distinct from becoming authoritarian, which would escalate further.

**Clinical example (Roger and Steve):**
Roger: "Come over here and say that."
Steve: "You don't want me to come over there. You're a piss artist."
Clinician: "Thank you. We are not here to call each other names. Steve, thanks for the apology, and Roger, thanks for accepting the apology. Let's go back to where we were talking about how we work out if someone is trustworthy."

Here the clinician quickly manages the situation by reducing aggression, taking control of the interaction, and redirecting. The clinician gives apologies that weren't actually offered—this may feel inauthentic but it serves de-escalation. You may revisit the actual problem between Roger and Steve later, or never. People with ASPD often bear grudges and rarely forgive meaningfully.

### If One Patient Leaves

**If Steve storms out**, one clinician (if two are available) follows him out for a brief conversation to maintain alliance and de-escalate. The other clinician stays with Roger to calm the group atmosphere. It may be better for Steve to go home and return next week rather than forcing a quick return.

If Steve calms down, the clinician can negotiate a safety agreement before reconvening the group. The principle: **support different protagonists** (called "siding"). Each clinician is psychologically protective of one party, and the clinicians jointly decide when it's safe to reconvene.

### If Both Must Leave

Do not ask both patients to leave simultaneously. Suggest they both leave, then have one clinician take out one patient (brief conversation, maintain alliance), and the other clinician does the same with the second patient after a suitable interval. Clinicians maintain authority over the decision to reconvene.
