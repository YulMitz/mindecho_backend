## Embodied Mentalizing and Eating Disorders

Eating disorders involve a fundamental distortion in how clients experience and make sense of their bodies. Hilda Bruch, a pioneer in psychodynamic treatment of eating disorders, identified that her patients lacked awareness of inner experiences and did not rely on their own feelings, thoughts, and bodily sensations to guide behavior. They compensated by asserting control through extraordinary feats of physical discipline.

MBT understands this as a disruption in **embodied mentalizing**—the ability to recognize, interpret, and regulate oneself through bodily experience.

### The Developmental Foundation

Embodied mentalizing begins in infancy. When a caregiver is attuned—noticing the infant's cries, hunger, discomfort, joy—and responds with accurate physical care and comfort, the infant learns: *My body matters. My inner states are recognized. I am safe.* This caregiver responsiveness predicts later attachment security, language development, academic skills, and social competence.

When caregiver responsiveness is reduced by parenting stress, depression, or neglect, the infant does not develop the sense that their bodily signals are meaningful or worth attending to. Later, this child may become a young person who ignores hunger, dismisses fatigue, or cannot distinguish sadness from stomach pain. The body becomes alien territory rather than a home.

In adolescence, a normal developmental task is to integrate mental experience (thoughts, feelings) with embodied experience (sensation, appetite, sexuality). In eating disorders, there is a pathological persistence of embodied mentalizing into adolescence and beyond—but inverted. Instead of the body reflecting internal well-being, **body shape and weight become concrete representations of internal states like control, self-worth, and social value.**

Clients with eating disorders often report: *"Thin is good. I just knew it."* This is not a thought; it is experienced as an absolute, unchallengeable fact—a core belief encoded in the body itself.

### The Disconnection from Bodily Knowing

Many clients with eating disorders describe their eating and body-management behaviors as mechanical, ritualized, and emotionally numb. One recovered patient described: *"I did things in a very ritualized way. I was not thinking or feeling anything. I remember feeling physical hunger and the satisfaction that I was not giving in to it was the only important thing."*

This reflects a split: the body is managed, controlled, acted upon—but not experienced as part of a mentalized, felt self. The client is a spectator to their own embodied life. Over time, this dissociation from bodily experience creates a **disjointed sense of selfhood**, where the person experiences themselves as fragmented—a public self that others see, a secret self that eats or restricts, and no integrated "I" that connects them.
