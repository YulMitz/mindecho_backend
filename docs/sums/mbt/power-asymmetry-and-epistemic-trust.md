## Power Asymmetry and Epistemic Trust

The therapeutic relationship itself is a mentalizing tool in MBT. Power imbalance—where the patient feels "inferior" or subordinate to the clinician—directly interferes with their capacity to think for themselves and learn from the therapeutic experience.

**Why this matters:** If patients accept the clinician's formulations passively, they are not truly mentalizing. They are depending on the clinician to do their thinking. This creates a one-directional flow of mental-state interest and prevents the patient from developing the robust, flexible mentalizing they need outside therapy.

**Creating epistemic trust:** For a patient to genuinely learn from you, your mind must be perceived as approachable, accessible, and agreeable. This is achieved through "levelling up"—explicitly sharing your own mentalizing process, acknowledging your vulnerabilities, and modeling how you recover when *you* lose your capacity to think clearly.

**Practical moves:**
- When a patient touches on something that unbalances you (anxiety, fear), name it: *"I'm realizing you've touched on something that's making it hard for me to think straight. Can we pause a moment?"*
- Share your genuine uncertainty: *"I'm not sure what to say about that. You've stumped me. Help me think about it."*
- Be transparent about your own thought process when relevant to the work, but keep it benign—open, courteous, generous, kind—never critical, haughty, or dismissive of the patient.

The patient needs to become equally interested in your mind: *Why does she say that? What does she mean about me?* This reciprocal curiosity is what builds epistemic trust over time, allowing the patient to gradually accept influence from your perspective while maintaining their own thinking.
