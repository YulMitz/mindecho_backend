## Restoring Mentalizing When It Breaks Down in the Group

In group therapy, low mentalizing in one person triggers low mentalizing in others. The clinician's job is to interrupt this cascade with sensitivity and skill, protecting vulnerable members while keeping the group focused on understanding minds rather than attacking or dismissing each other.

### The "Siding" Intervention

When one member criticizes another and the criticized person withdraws, momentarily become the advocate for the vulnerable person. This breaks the escalation spiral.

**Example**: Member A says to Member B: "You're always needy. You need to get over it."
Member B goes silent.

**Clinician intervention**: "That sounded quite harsh. Where did that come from?" (Addressing Member A, but on behalf of Member B's mental state.)

Once the immediate tension eases, you can return to a more neutral, "not-knowing" stance and explore both people's perspectives. Siding prevents humiliation and preserves the member's willingness to remain psychologically open.

### Preventing the "Central Distribution Office" Problem

A common error: the clinician becomes the hub through which all discussion flows. Member A talks to the clinician. Clinician talks to Member B. This prevents peer learning and keeps relationships superficial.

**Redirect to peer interaction**:
- After a member shares a problem, ask another member to respond or ask a genuine question
- Use the not-knowing stance and triangulation so the group talks *to each other*, not at you
- Fade into the background as a catalyst, not a director

### Intervention Levels for Low Mentalizing

- **Diversion**: If tension is high, gently shift focus: "Let's pause here. What are others thinking about what we've heard?"
- **De-escalation**: Slow the pace, lower your voice, ask for concrete examples rather than abstract criticism
- **Siding with the vulnerable**: Briefly protect the person under attack
- **Triangulation**: Bring in a third person to ask curious questions and interrupt the dyadic conflict
- **Validation before curiosity**: "It sounds like this really matters to you. Help me understand what triggered that reaction."
