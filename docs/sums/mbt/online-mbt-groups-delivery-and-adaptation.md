## Delivering MBT Groups Online: Technical and Relational Considerations

Online MBT groups increase access and often improve attendance, but they require deliberate adjustments to maintain mentalizing. The screen itself becomes part of the therapeutic work.

### Pre-Group Preparation

**Assessment phase**: Ideally complete initial assessment face-to-face if possible. This builds alliance, allows crisis planning, and gives the clinician embodied experience of the patient. If fully remote, offer tutorial sessions on platform navigation.

**Group orientation**: In the first online session, use an "icebreaker" to normalize the format and address the elephant in the room:
- "Meeting on screen is different from in person. What feels awkward or hard about this for you?"
- Discuss camera-on/off preferences. Some members feel self-conscious; offer the "Hide Self View" option or sticky note on screen.
- Agree on how to leave if distressed (hand-raise, chat message, move to breakout room).

### Enhancing Mentalizing on Screen

**Spotlight mode**: When discussing one person's problem, keep that person and the clinician visible while others become viewers. This allows detailed observation of facial expression and micro-expressions.

**Exaggerated expressiveness**: Encourage members to be more expressive than in person because screen fatigue and small images make subtle cues harder to read.

**Facial emotion practice**: Early sessions, use simple exercises: clinician makes a happy/sad/angry face; members guess the emotion. This eases anxiety and sharpens emotional literacy.

### Using Platform Tools

**Breakout rooms**: Divide the group into two subgroups (with a clinician in each) to discuss a problem or value. Subgroups report back to the main room. This:
- Increases peer interaction
- Prevents the "central hub" problem
- Allows focused work on specific themes
- Reduces anxiety for quieter members

**Chat function**: Members hesitant to speak aloud can note thoughts in chat. Clinician can then bring these into spoken discussion: "I noticed in the chat that someone said... shall we explore that?"

**Screen sharing**: Display agreed values, session themes, or materials. Create them in real-time so members feel involved in building the group culture.

**Personal space sharing**: Ask members to show one object in their room that matters to them and briefly explain why. This humanizes the space and builds connection.

### Managing Technical and Relational Disruptions

**Frequent dropouts or connection problems**: Some members' technology is unstable. Agree on a pre-arranged plan:
- Clinician will contact them by phone at session end
- Patient agrees to answer the call
- This prevents the rest of the group from becoming anxious or derailed

**Members arriving late or leaving early**: More common in online groups. Use the same triangulation and closure techniques but be prepared to re-orient latecomers briefly.

**Anxiety about being visible**: Some members feel watched and uncomfortable. Validate: "It can feel exposing being on screen." Offer concrete options (camera off for parts of session, Hide Self View, smaller window size).
