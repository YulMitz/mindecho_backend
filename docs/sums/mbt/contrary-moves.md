## Contrary Moves: Rebalancing the Mentalizing Dimensions

MBT recognizes that effective mentalizing involves fluency across several dimensions: self/other, internal/external, automatic/controlled, cognitive/affective. Many clients with BPD become "stuck" in one corner of these dimensions, and contrary moves are the technical intervention for unsticking them.

### The Self/Other Imbalance

A common pattern in BPD is hypervigilant focus on others' mental states at the expense of self-awareness. The client monitors the other person's mood, facial expressions, and reactions obsessively, trying to reconstruct what the other person thinks of them. Meanwhile, their own internal world—their own preferences, feelings, needs—becomes neglected or invisible.

Sarah, the case example, illustrates this: she asks her boyfriend repeatedly what he thinks of her and counts the chilies he puts in the dish to gauge whether he cares. She has lost sight of her own inner experience in service of reading his mind. The clinician identifies this and makes a *contrary move*: instead of continuing to explore the boyfriend's likely thoughts, the clinician redirects: "How does that worry leave you feeling about you?"

### How Contrary Moves Work

The principle is simple: when the client's attention is exclusively focused on the other's mind, encourage elaboration of self-experience. Conversely, when the client offers only a superficial, projected version of the other (replacing genuine mind-reading with a fantasy of who the other "should" be), encourage deeper awareness of the other's actual mental states.

This is not simple oscillation. It's strategic rebalancing. The goal is to develop adaptive flexibility—the ability to move fluidly between poles depending on context. Good mentalizing means using all dimensions fluently to adapt optimally to each social situation.

### Practical Application

In the session with Sarah:
- **Clinician identifies the stuck pattern**: She's lost her sense of being loved, so she's monitoring his mental states constantly.
- **Clinician makes the contrary move**: "But somehow you lost having a feeling for what he felt about you—otherwise why judge him in terms of the number of chilies? You kind of lose sight of him and start to worry about his feelings for you, but how does that worry leave you feeling about you?"
- **Shift in focus**: Sarah moves from "Does he care?" to exploring her own internal experience of relaxation with him.

The clinician then engages in the opposite movement, asking her about her boyfriend's mind states in relation to hers, maintaining the back-and-forth that develops flexibility.

### The Broader Dimensions

Contrary moves also apply to cognitive/affective balance. When a client over-relies on intellectual processing and detachment, the clinician coaxes them to pay attention to and trust their feelings. When emotional flooding predominates, questioning shifts to the beliefs and expectations driving those feelings, recruiting more cognition to help manage the emotions.

With Sarah and her son Jack, when she describes caregiving in purely cognitive, externally verifiable terms ("eating well," "being clean"), the clinician makes a contrary move into affect: "Oh, that is so worrying. But tell me, what does it feel like to be a mother who is being observed all the time?" This forces her to access the emotional dimension she's been bypassing.
