## Activating the Relational Process and We-Mode

MBT does not treat the patient's mind as an isolated object of study. Instead, it explicitly names and works with what is happening *between* therapist and patient—the "we" that is being created moment by moment.

**What Is We-Mode?**

We-mode is not simply both people being friendly or aligned. It is a shared, collective form of mentalizing in which both you and the patient are attending to what is true for the relational unit—the "us"—not just for individuals. It is characterized by:

- Shared intentionality (you are working toward a common goal)
- Mutual understanding and recognition
- Both parties' minds being genuinely open to influence by the other
- Recognition that neither of you has a monopoly on truth

In well-functioning relationships, we-mode emerges naturally. In therapy with patients with BPD—especially those with trauma histories and fractured attachment—it must be actively cultivated and supported.

**Path to Relational Work**

The journey from individual to relational mentalizing follows a sequence:

1. **Joint formulation**: At the start, you and the patient create a shared, simple model of the pattern that brings them to therapy. This is not the therapist's formulation imposed on the patient; it is co-created.

2. **Personal mentalizing profile**: Help the patient understand their own characteristic patterns—how they tend to process mental states when stressed, which poles they favor or avoid, what triggers them.

3. **Explicit attention to clinician's mind**: You do not hide your own mental processes. When you are confused, stuck, or emotionally touched by something the patient has said, you can say so. "I'm uncertain here" or "That landed hard for me" models that mentalizing is mutual.

4. **Empathic validation as foundation**: Before moving to relational exploration, establish that you see and care about the patient's experience. This safety is essential.

5. **Focus on alliance ruptures and repair**: When tension or misunderstanding arises (and it will), treat it as precious data. Name it together: "I notice we seem to be on different pages about something. Can we look at that?"

6. **Self-disclosure about interpersonal patterns**: When relevant and boundaried, share a slice of your own struggle with understanding or connecting—not your personal trauma or life story, but your human experience of misunderstanding, defensiveness, or uncertainty.

**Moving from Content to Process**

Early in therapy, the patient may arrive with a story: "My partner said X and I felt abandoned." The content is important. But as stability increases, you gently shift focus to process: "As you're telling me this story now, I'm noticing something. You seem kind of distant, almost like you're reading a script. What's happening between us right now, in this moment?" This invitation—to mentalize the relational moment—is the threshold into real change.
