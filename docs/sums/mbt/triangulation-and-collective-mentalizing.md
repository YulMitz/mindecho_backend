## Triangulation and Collective Mentalizing

Triangulation is the systematic inclusion of a third person (then more) into a focused two-person discussion, structured to move the group from individual perspective to shared perspective. This builds "we-mode"—the experience of belonging to something larger than oneself—which is particularly transformative for people with personality disorders who often feel profoundly alone.

### The Four Steps

**Step 1: Two protagonists with a shared problem theme**

Appoint two members who have different expressions of the same underlying issue. Example:
- Sarah: "I keep asking my partner if he loves me because I'm terrified he'll leave me"
- Stanley: "I need to control what my girlfriend does because I'm afraid she'll meet someone else"

Common theme: anxiety about being abandoned or losing the relationship.

**Step 2: Introduce the third person as commentator**

The third person (e.g., John) is tasked with asking *curious questions*, not offering advice. They are trained (in earlier MBT-I sessions) to use the not-knowing stance:
- "What was going through your mind when that happened?"
- "How did you interpret her reaction?"
- "What do you need from her in that moment?"

John helps the protagonists elaborate their internal states without problem-solving.

**Step 3: Expand to four-or-more-way interaction**

As the discussion deepens, invite others to contribute. They may notice patterns, resonances, or offer their own experience: "I do something similar when I feel insecure..." Now multiple perspectives are building on each other.

**Step 4: Collective reflection—what do *we* learn?**

The whole group pauses and reflects:
- What did we learn about Sarah and Stanley's struggles?
- What did you notice about yourself as we discussed this?
- What do we take home from this?

This final step embeds the learning into the group's collective awareness and helps each member translate the shared insight into their own life.

### Why Collective Mentalizing Matters

Patients with personality disorders often lack the experience of feeling understood by a group—of belonging to a "we" that shares struggles and minds. This repeated practice of triangulation creates robust affiliative bonds and trust, which reduce isolation and build the psychological foundation for change.

The process is gradual and repeated across many sessions. Each iteration strengthens the group's capacity to hold multiple perspectives simultaneously without collapse into blame, advice, or avoidance.
