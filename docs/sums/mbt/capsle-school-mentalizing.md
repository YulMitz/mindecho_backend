## CAPSLE: Creating a Peaceful School Learning Environment

Schools are the primary social environment beyond the family where adolescents develop their sense of safety, predictability, and trustworthiness in the wider world. A troubled school environment—one marked by aggression and bullying—can teach young people that relationships operate on domination and fear, not shared goals and cooperation. **CAPSLE** is a mentalization-based, whole-school intervention designed to reshape this learning.

### How CAPSLE Works: Four Core Components

**1. Positive Climate Campaign**
Reflective discussions happen *immediately after* incidents of violence or bullying occur, led by counselors in classrooms. The focus is not on punishment but on understanding what happened in mental state terms. This timely, reflected-upon approach prevents incidents from escalating and gaining social currency (where "toughness" or "winning" becomes admired).

**2. Classroom Management Plan**
Rather than individual discipline, the teacher frames problem behavior as a class problem. Pupils are helped to recognize their roles—as bullies, victims, or bystanders—and to understand that all three roles are products of group dynamics, not fixed character traits. This approach:
- Reduces scapegoating
- Shifts focus to *meaning* rather than punishment
- Helps children see how their choices influence the social system

**3. Physical Education Program**
Role-play, relaxation, and self-defense techniques teach children concrete skills for responding to victimization and bystanding. Through enacting bully–victim–bystander scenarios, pupils work out alternatives to fighting and learn how to manage fear and maintain agency when threatened or pressured to watch.

**4. Peer and Adult Mentorship**
Mentors model non-aggressive problem-solving and teach practical skills like game refereeing, dispute resolution, and helping others. This provides relational containment and language scaffolding for power struggles.

### The Bystander Problem in Non-Mentalizing Environments

In violent schools, bystanders often experience sadistic pleasure watching fights. This is not complete mentalizing failure—empathy is present—but it is **highly limited mentalizing**: the victim's suffering is *recognized* but not *felt*. The bystander uses the victim to contain their own unwanted (frightened) parts, creating distance from the other's internal world.

Mentalizing capacity is fragile in adolescence and requires environmental scaffolding. CAPSLE deliberately constructs a social environment where children practice and experience the benefits of more balanced mentalizing, where a fight is stopped because people *feel* the other's distress, not just recognize it abstractly.

### Why Peer Support Matters Most

Secure early attachment increases resilience, but **peer support and acceptance in adolescence** are the strongest predictors of later resilience. Schools that foster mentalizing climates teach adolescents that social relationships work through understanding, mutual care, and interdependence—a template they carry forward.
