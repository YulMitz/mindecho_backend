## "We-Mode" Mentalizing: Relational Presence and Shared Understanding

"We-mode" is relational mentalizing—the joining together of the mental states of self and other in the spirit of joint intentionality. It is the deepest level of mentalizing work and is only accessible once the client has stabilized in I-mode and begun exploring me-mode with genuine curiosity.

In we-mode, the clinician and client see the same whole person (not selected, distorted, or idealized parts); they can attend jointly to personal narratives while acknowledging and respecting that they may have different perspectives—both valid. This requires epistemic trust: the client trusts that the clinician understands the meaning of their experience and is genuinely "with them," not judging or dismissing.

### Prerequisites for Real We-Mode

**Stability in I-mode.** The client must have sufficient coherence in their self-experience that they do not need to defensively inflate or collapse in order to maintain a sense of self.

**Curiosity about others' minds.** The client must show genuine interest in understanding how others think and feel, not just in being understood by them.

**Capacity to hold difference.** The client can tolerate that the clinician sees something differently without experiencing it as rejection or criticism. Difference is information, not threat.

### What We-Mode Looks Like in Session

The clinician and client jointly explore a relational pattern or narrative:

**CLINICIAN:** "So when you walked out of that meeting, you were protecting yourself from the humiliation. That makes complete sense. And I imagine your boss and colleagues experienced your leaving as an abrupt exit, maybe even as you confirming that you couldn't handle feedback. From where they stood, it might have looked like you couldn't tolerate any suggestion of improvement."

**CLIENT:** "I see that. They didn't understand that I was protecting myself."

**CLINICIAN:** "Right. And I wonder whether it's possible that *both* things are true: you were protecting yourself from real pain, *and* they experienced your response as dismissive or defensive. Neither cancels out the other."

**CLIENT:** "That's... harder to hold. But I see what you mean."

This is we-mode: two people attending to the same event from different angles, each perspective illuminating something true about what happened.

### The Risk of "Pretend We-Mode"

A significant complication arises when clients with narcissistic features engage in unconscious mimicry of the clinician—matching perspectives, adopting the clinician's language or values, creating an illusion of perfect alignment. This is sometimes called the "chameleon effect." It feels like we-mode to the clinician (finally, they understand each other!) but is actually the client masquerading as the clinician's perspective to maintain connection or avoid conflict.

**Signs of pretend we-mode:**
- Sudden, complete agreement with the clinician's formulation
- The client adopting the clinician's therapeutic language in subsequent sessions
- Visible discomfort or shutdown when the clinician gently introduces a different perspective
- The client's behavior or values seeming to shift to match the clinician's, then shifting again with a different person

**How to address it:** Explicitly notice and name it with compassionate curiosity: "I notice that after I offered that idea, you seemed to shift toward agreeing completely. I wonder whether that was genuine agreement or whether you might have been matching my perspective to keep things smooth between us. What's your sense?"

Real we-mode requires the courage to maintain separate perspectives while staying connected.

### Generalization Beyond Therapy

The ultimate aim is for the client to develop we-mode capacity in relationships outside therapy. This means:

- Showing interest in others' internal experiences and motivations
- Tolerating disagreement without devaluing the other person or withdrawing
- Recognizing that others have valid reasons for their perspectives, even when different from the client's
- Building relationships characterized by mutuality rather than dominance or dependency
- Experiencing belonging and acceptance while remaining an independent person

These are the foundations of genuine friendship and intimate connection—outcomes that are profoundly meaningful for clients who have relied on defensive narcissism to manage vulnerability.
