## De-escalation and Threat Management: Patient-to-Clinician

Threats toward the clinician are more immediately significant than patient-to-patient aggression because your mentalizing is essential for the group's function. If you cannot feel safe, you cannot continue to work effectively.

### First Priority: Personal Safety

**If you are not confident you are safe, stop the group session immediately and seek support from other staff.** This is not negotiable. Your safety comes before everything else.

### If You Feel Reasonably Safe

Assuming your personal safety is not under immediate threat, you have several options:

**1. De-escalate using the techniques described for patient-to-patient threats.**
Keep calm, maintain physical distance, use a level voice, and avoid staring or leaning forward. Do not touch the patient.

**2. Apologize for your difficulty in thinking when under threat.**
Find language that communicates vulnerability without becoming submissive. Examples:
- "It is really difficult for me to think about how to help when I feel under threat."
- "It will make it hard for me to continue to try to help if I cannot feel safe in developing our work together about your problems."

This names the reality (threat undermines mentalizing) without surrendering authority or becoming defensive.

**3. Use affect focus if the threat relates to hierarchical dynamics.**
If the threat is not severe but reflects an ongoing struggle about authority or power between you and the patient, identify this as a pattern interfering with treatment. For example: "I notice when I suggest something, you push back. Is that something that happens with you and other people in positions of authority?"

### The Core Principle

Your mentalizing in the context of threat is a top priority. The patient's threat is often a test of whether you will remain mentalizing and non-retaliatory, or whether you will become defensive and punitive. If you remain calm and curious ("Help me understand why you said that"), you model that threat does not derail thinking. This is powerful for patients with ASPD who may never have experienced an authority figure who didn't retaliate or collapse.
