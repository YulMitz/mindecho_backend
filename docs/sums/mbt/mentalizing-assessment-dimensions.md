## Mentalizing Assessment: The Four Dimensions

During the initial assessment, the clinician systematically evaluates the patient's flexibility across four primary dimensions of mentalizing. Rigidity, imbalance, and over-reliance on one pole signal vulnerability and become explicit targets in treatment.

**Core principle:** Healthy mentalizing involves rapid, self-correcting movement *between* the poles of each dimension. Concern arises only when mentalizing becomes fixed, rigid, and inflexible—with one pole regularly over- or under-used and applied out of context.

### **Dimension 1: Self / Other**

The ability to distinguish and shift between your own mental states and others' mental states.

**Vulnerability pattern:** Patient cannot create stable representations of self distinct from how others see them. They oscillate between:
- Over-focus on self (self-centered, difficulty reading others' minds)
- Over-focus on other (constantly trying to read others' thoughts in relation to themselves; needs reassurance about whether they are loved)

*Clinical marker:* Patient says *"I need to know if he loves me"* and cannot be soothed by reassurance because they lack a stable internal representation of themselves as worthy of love. The representation collapses under anxiety.

**Context matters:** Check whether the difficulty is specific to attachment relationships or shows across all contexts. A patient might read a colleague's mind well (cognitive domain) but collapse when attachment processes activate (emotional domain).

### **Dimension 2: Internal / External**

The balance between relying on subjective, internal experience versus external, observable signs when making judgments about mental states.

**Vulnerability pattern:** Over-reliance on external (observable) without adequate reasoning from context.

*Clinical marker:* Patient assigns significance to marginal physical details. *"He didn't text me for 3 minutes = he doesn't love me."* *"The bus was late = today will be bad."* Patient treats external appearance (facial expression, tone of voice, body posture) as direct evidence of internal state, without considering context or the possibility that their impression could be wrong. They "live the experience" rather than grounding it in reality.

**Why it matters:** External focus without flexible internal reasoning leaves the patient prey to automatic, poorly regulated thinking triggered by ambiguous social cues. They cannot correct assumptions using reflection or memory.

### **Dimension 3: Automatic / Controlled**

The balance between rapid, intuitive "fast thinking" and reflective, deliberate "slow thinking."

**Vulnerability pattern:** Over-reliance on automatic mentalizing; minimal activation of controlled, reflective processes.

*Clinical marker:* Patient makes assumptions ("People don't like me," "He will leave me") that are not reflected on, questioned, or modified when evidence contradicts them. They sail on oblivious until hitting immovable reality (job loss, rejection, humiliation). When they try to reflect, prompted by others, the effort is disappointing—they misread people and construct implausible narratives.

**The collision:** The caring observer (friend, clinician, partner) becomes increasingly perplexed and frustrated: *"I want to help but I don't know what you're talking about."* Communication breaks down because the patient has stopped considering the conversational partner's mind. There is a mismatch between the patient's narrative and the partner's understanding of shared reality.

### **Dimension 4: Cognitive / Affective**

The balance between thinking-based mentalizing and emotion-based mentalizing when processing mental states.

**Vulnerability pattern:** Domination by affective processing; inadequate use of cognitive mentalizing to regulate emotions.

*Clinical marker:* Patient becomes so anxious in interpersonal interactions (especially intimate ones) that thoughts become uncontrolled. They feel frightened and need others to help calibrate their internal states. However, no response truly soothes them—reassurance is only momentary and they seek further reassurance. Anxiety is not tethered to previous similar experiences, context, or cause.

*In high arousal:* Patient experiences metacognitive disorganization. They cannot use the normal emotion-regulation strategies: recognizing bodily experience → assigning appropriate causal attribution → identifying source and context → linking to similar past experiences → taking appropriate action.

**The pattern:** When emotion is extremely activated, the patient's cognitive capacity collapses. The two poles are out of balance.

---

### **How to use this in formulation:**

For each dimension, note:
1. Which pole is over-used or stuck?
2. In what contexts does the imbalance appear (all contexts or specific triggers like attachment stress)?
3. What is the consequence for the patient's mentalizing and behavior?

When one pole is under-used, there will typically be excessive, inappropriate use of the other pole as a compensatory strategy.
