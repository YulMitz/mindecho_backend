## Games, Role-Play, and Playfulness in AvPD Treatment

### Why Games Work in AvPD

For clients with avoidant personality disorder, **direct relational exploration often generates unbearable anxiety**. Games sidestep this by creating a **safe, inconsequential space**—labeled as "play" or "pretend"—where the client can approach mental states without the full weight of real consequences. Paradoxically, the client's habitual avoidance strategy becomes the entry point: they are asked to engage in pretend mode *therapeutically*, transforming avoidance into a compassionate challenge to mentalizing.

Games also serve a crucial **counter-relational function**. Many avoidantly attached clients' pervasive withdrawal and difficulty engaging can trigger frustration, boredom, or secondary avoidance in the therapist ("hitting your head against a brick wall"). Shared playfulness in the room restores the therapist's agency and curiosity, creating a "we-mode" that triggers epistemic trust—the belief that the therapist's words and reassurances are credible.

### Types of Therapeutic Games

**Simple Role-Reversal**

Swap roles with the client to illuminate their automatic interpretations:

> **Therapist as Client**: "I'm noticing you're looking away. You must think I'm judging you. I knew this wouldn't work." *(exaggerating the client's habitual self-focus)*
>
> **Client as Therapist**: "Oh... actually, I wasn't. I was just thinking about something else."
>
> **Therapist**: "So my assumption wasn't accurate? What do you think I could have been thinking instead?"

This reveals the gap between the client's interpretation and reality in a contained, almost humorous way.

**Inverted Relational Role-Play**

Address therapeutic impasses by inverting the dynamic:

> **Therapist**: "Imagine I'm you and you're the therapist. I'm telling you how I feel, and you have to respond to what I'm saying."

This is less anxiety-provoking than direct discussion of the therapeutic relationship, but accomplishes the same goal.

**Mind-Brain Scan Game**

Provide the client with an outline of a brain showing blank spaces labeled "me," "them," "them about me," and "me about them."

> **Therapist**: "Let's treat this brain like a person's mind. Write in what you think was going on in your colleague's head. I'll write what I'm hearing too."

The therapist can intentionally write things the client might find absurd or amusing, creating **communication about mental states in an atmosphere of fun**. The main benefit is not just elaboration of mental states, but **shared communication about minds**—breaking the isolation and "silo" feeling that characterizes many avoidant clients.

### Creating the Right Therapeutic Atmosphere

Games must be undertaken in a spirit of **genuine enjoyment and inconsequentiality**, not as a technique imposed from above. The clinician should:

- Allow genuine lightness and humor (not at the client's expense)
- Expect the client to relax as the game unfolds
- Be willing to be playful about the therapist's own reactions ("My mind goes blank when you withdraw—maybe I need therapy from you!")
- Use humor about shared human confusion rather than mocking the client's avoidance

### Games as a Bridge, Not an End Point

Games are **stimuli that move the client gradually from clinician-directed explicit exposition of mental states toward patient-owned implicit reflection**. They are not the goal; they are the gate into more genuine mentalizing. Over time, as safety increases, the need for games diminishes and the client can engage more directly with their own and others' minds.
