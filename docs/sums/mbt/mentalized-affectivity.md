## Mentalized Affectivity: The Balance of Heart and Mind

Mentalized affectivity is the capacity to both experience emotions and reflect on them simultaneously—to feel and think about feeling at the same time. It represents an optimal balance between emotion and cognition. Many clients, especially those who are dysphoric, equate emotion regulation with squelching negative emotions. The goal of therapy is to shift this perspective.

We can use the metaphor of "hitting the pause button." This allows for a moment of reflective thought to intervene between an emotional trigger and the behavioral reaction that emotion prompts. This pause is where mentalizing happens. It's not about stopping the feeling, but about understanding it, making sense of it, modulating it, and then choosing how to express it.

This concept is closely aligned with Dialectical Behavior Therapy's (DBT) idea of "wise mind," which is the integration of the "reasonable mind" and the "emotion mind." When emotion is too intense, mentalizing collapses, and we are driven by stereotyped, reactive behaviors. When emotion is too suppressed, mentalizing becomes sterile and disconnected from our lived experience. Wise mind, or mentalized affectivity, is the synthesis of these two states.

The therapeutic stance is to encourage clients to move away from viewing negative emotions as something to be eliminated and positive emotions as something to be chased at all costs. Instead, we help them cultivate curiosity about the meaning of their emotions. We encourage them to reflect on what their feelings might be signaling about their current experiences, their relationships, and their past. The goal is not just to regulate emotion, but to find and create meaning through the process of reflecting on it, even while in the midst of the emotional experience itself.
