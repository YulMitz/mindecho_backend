## Group Idealization and Pretend-Mode Cohesion

People with ASPD often experience isolation and a lack of understanding from others. When they sit down together in a group for perhaps the first time, they rapidly identify with each other, recognizing shared problems and struggles. This is reassuring and valuable. But it quickly becomes idealization of the group itself, with groupthink replacing genuine mentalizing.

### What Happens

- Group members begin to believe they understand each other better than clinicians do
- They answer for each other when clinicians ask questions
- They may arrange meetings or support outside the group
- Practical advice-giving becomes the norm: "I had that problem, you should do X"
- Clinicians are framed as outsiders who "don't understand how we help each other"
- If you challenge this, you are seen as interfering and not supporting their mutual help

The problem: **teleology does not help recover mentalizing.** Advice ("do X") skips over understanding why X might work for one person but not another.

### The Clinical Approach

**Phase 1: Accept the cohesion initially.** Let the pretend mode run for a time at the beginning of the group. Validate it.

**Validate the experience of belonging:** "It's really valuable that you all recognize shared problems. For many of you, this may be the first time you've felt understood by others who truly get it. That's important."

**Phase 2: Move from validation to curiosity about differences.** Suggest that the unity is excellent *because* it gives them a safe background to explore *differences* in perspective, which is actually very helpful.
