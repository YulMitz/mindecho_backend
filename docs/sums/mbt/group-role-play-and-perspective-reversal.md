## Role Play and Perspective Reversal in the Group

Role play in MBT-G serves a dual purpose: it embeds explicit mentalizing (conscious perspective-taking) into implicit, intuitive understanding (automatic attunement to others' minds). The goal is not theatrical performance but rehearsal of mental flexibility in a safe container.

### Two Forms of Role Play

**1. Clinician-Led Mini Dramatization**

The clinician and co-clinician (or clinician and willing member) act out a brief scenario to illustrate poor mentalizing or the not-knowing stance. Example:
- One clinician becomes rigid and dismissive: "I'm not interested in your excuses."
- The other takes a not-knowing stance: "Help me understand what led to that?"

Members observe and reflect on the difference. This provides a model without demanding vulnerability from the group.

**2. Member Role Reversal After Conflict**

After an argument between members (once calm is restored):

**Step 1**: Name and note the conflict. Summarize it at session end.

**Step 2**: Revisit it in the next session, ideally when mentalizing is more stable.

**Step 3**: Ask the two protagonists to *swap chairs* and "become" the other person.

**Step 4**: Before replaying the argument, ask each person to articulate aloud what was happening in the other's mind beforehand: "John, you're now Stanley. What was going on for Stanley before this conflict started?"

**Step 5**: Replay the interaction with reversed roles.

This technique frees people from their own defensive concerns momentarily. By stepping into the other's shoes, they often discover fears, vulnerabilities, or intentions they had missed.

### When to Pause

- If a member refuses to participate, do not push. Low mentalizing is present; repair the relationship before revisiting.
- If dysregulation escalates, pause and return to conversation.
- If the group enters "pretend mode" (performing rather than truly exploring), gently interrupt: "What are you actually feeling right now, rather than what you think you should say?"

### Observer Learning

Members who watch the role play also learn. In Step 4 of triangulation, ask observers: "What did you notice about how they each experienced that moment? What did you learn about yourself from watching?"

This builds the group's collective understanding without requiring every member to be a protagonist.
