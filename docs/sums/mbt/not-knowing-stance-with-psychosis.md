## The Not-Knowing Stance in Psychosis Work

One of the most challenging aspects of MBT for psychosis is sustaining genuine curiosity and respect for the patient's subjective experience in the face of confusing, frightening, or bizarre delusional content. The not-knowing stance is not about pretending ignorance; it is about monitoring your own arousal and maintaining the attitude that the patient's mind is worth understanding.

### Why This Stance Matters in Psychosis

Psychotic manifestations—paranoia, hallucinations, disorganization—can induce fear, confusion, and thought blockage in the clinician. You may find yourself feeling tired, apathetic, or sensing that nothing makes sense. This is not a sign of failure; it is vicarious traumatization. What matters is recognizing it and recalibrating.

The patient picks up on the clinician's loss of mentalizing capacity. If you freeze, retreat, or lose curiosity, the patient feels even more alone and beyond help. Conversely, if you maintain genuine interest in their inner world—even when that world contains delusions—you model the possibility that disturbing thoughts and feelings can be tolerated and reflected upon.

### Core Elements

**Monitor your arousal.** Before intervening, check your own nervous system. Are you holding your breath? Bracing? Flooded with competing thoughts? Self-regulation comes first. You cannot help the patient mentalize if you are dysregulated.

**Tune in through reflective curiosity.** Rather than trying to understand the content of the delusion, ask yourself: *What is it like for this person to experience such persecution? What effect does it have on their daily functioning? How are they experiencing this right now, in this moment?* These questions orient you toward the subjective, lived experience rather than the "wrongness" of the belief.

**Find words that make curiosity relevant to the patient.** The not-knowing stance only works if it is communicated in ways the patient can receive. Ask yourself: *How would I phrase this question so that the patient feels I am genuinely trying to understand their mind, not interrogating them or testing whether they "know" they are ill?*

### What Not-Knowing Is NOT

- It is not agreement with the delusion
- It is not collusion or validation of distorted reality
- It is not avoidance of the patient's suffering
- It is not asking questions you don't actually care about

### Observing Dynamics in Session

The not-knowing stance gives you the opportunity to notice:
- The specific triggers or moments when the patient loses mentalizing capacity
- How the patient retrieves mentalizing (or fails to, and why)
- What resources the patient uses to regulate distress in individual vs. group settings
- The effects the psychotic narrative is having on functioning and relationships

A key instruction from the text: **Do not seek to explain the unexplainable to yourself or to the patient.** This is liberating. You do not need to make logical sense of the delusion. You need to understand what it is doing for the patient—what agency or coherence it is maintaining—and how to help them tolerate the uncertainty and fear beneath it.
