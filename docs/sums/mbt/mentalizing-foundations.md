## Mentalizing: The Ability to Understand Mental States

Mentalizing is the capacity to understand and reflect on mental states—your own and others'—and to recognize that these mental states drive behavior. This is not the same as empathy alone. Mentalizing involves accurately interpreting what someone might be thinking, feeling, intending, or desiring, and understanding how these internal experiences explain why they act the way they do.

In clinical work, mentalizing breaks down in predictable ways. When clients are under stress—especially when attachment needs are activated (fear of abandonment, rejection, or threat)—they lose the ability to think reflectively about mental states. Instead, they may slide into automatic, reactive thinking: assuming they know what others think without evidence, jumping to conclusions about others' intentions, or becoming preoccupied with their own emotional state without stepping back to see the bigger picture.

MBT is built on the premise that many mental health difficulties—from borderline personality disorder to self-harm, depression, anxiety, and relationship problems—involve disrupted mentalizing. The therapeutic work focuses on restoring and strengthening the capacity to think about mental states in a regulated way, even when emotions are high.

One critical insight from the research: **activation of the attachment system inhibits the neural systems that support mentalizing.** This means that traditional talk therapy, which relies on reflection and insight, can paradoxically make things worse if the client's attachment system is activated (they feel rejected, abandoned, misunderstood, or unsafe). Your job is to recognize when mentalizing is offline and respond by validating and regulating the emotional state first, not by asking the client to think more deeply.

Mentalizing is not constant—it fluctuates based on stress, safety, and whether the client feels understood. Your therapeutic stance must support the client in recovering mentalizing capacity gradually, in the moment, when it matters most.
