## Establishing Safety in Trauma-Focused MBT Groups

Safety is the essential precondition for trauma processing. Without it, clients cannot mentalize their traumatic experiences; they remain in survival mode. In MBT-TF (Mentalization-Based Treatment for Trauma in groups), Phase 1 is entirely devoted to establishing safety and embedding mentalizing as the organizing principle for understanding trauma.

### What Creates Psychological Safety

Safety in a trauma group is not simply the absence of danger—it is the active presence of predictability, respect, and shared responsibility for containment. The text lists specific group values that have been found useful:

- **Overwhelm management**: Group members know it is acceptable and supported to signal when they feel overwhelmed
- **Fairness in shared space**: Clear agreements about how the group uses time and respects boundaries
- **Tolerance for difference**: Explicit acknowledgment that diverse opinions and perspectives belong in the group
- **Benign intent**: Trust that others come with good intentions, even if their words sometimes sting
- **Kindness and sensitivity**: Active care in how members interact with each other
- **Open communication**: Permission and encouragement to say what is really happening inside
- **Attunement to others' feelings**: Regular checking in about emotional states
- **Reciprocal feedback**: Safe structures for giving and receiving honest reflection
- **Confidentiality**: Absolute protection of what is shared

### The Clinician's Role in Phase 1

The clinician does not simply announce these values. Rather, the group co-creates them through discussion, and the clinician models them consistently. When a member shares something painful, the clinician responds with curiosity and respect. When someone is overwhelmed, the clinician normalizes stepping back. When conflict emerges, the clinician mentalizes it—helping the group understand what thoughts and feelings drove the rupture, rather than punishing it.

Safety-building takes time. Rushing into trauma processing before psychological containment is established leads to re-traumatization and dropout. Phase 1 should be completed before moving to Phase 2 (mentalizing the trauma itself).
