## Embodied Mentalizing in Depression: The RADLE Sequence

Many depressed patients struggle to connect bodily sensations with emotional states. They may report physical symptoms—tightness, fatigue, numbness—without recognizing what feelings underlie these sensations. Some patients even claim they have no feelings at all, despite clear emotional responses. This is called **embodied mentalizing difficulty** or alexithymia: the inability to read and reflect on one's own emotions through bodily signals.

This gap between body and mind is a core feature in severe depression. Clients experience themselves as a *physical self* (sensations, fatigue, tension) rather than a *psychological self* (a person with emotions, thoughts, and agency). The therapeutic task is helping them develop **mentalized affectivity**—the capacity to reflect on and modulate emotional experiences by first recognizing them.

### The RADLE Sequence

The RADLE sequence is a structured five-step intervention designed to help clients move from unmentalized bodily experience to emotional awareness and eventually to understanding how emotions connect to their relational patterns and symptoms.

#### 1. **Recognizing Emotions**

Start by focusing on the physical sensations the client reports. Use a not-knowing, curious stance to generate hypotheses about what emotions *might* be underneath.

**Example:** A client says, "When my wife criticized me, I just shut down. I felt nothing." But the therapist observes tension, blushing, clenched hands. Rather than accepting "nothing," the therapist might say:

> "When you said you felt nothing, I noticed your whole body tensed up. Your face turned red and your hands clenched. I'm wondering—could you have felt anxious just then? Or maybe ashamed?"

The key is *marked mirroring*: the therapist reflects back the bodily signs they observe and offers emotional labels as tentative hypotheses, not certainties. This respects the client's experience while inviting them to consider what their body might be expressing.

#### 2. **Amplifying Emotions**

Once the client acknowledges an emotion, amplify it using marked mirroring to help them feel it more fully and consciously.

> "So you felt anger in that moment. How did that anger feel?"

This draws attention to the emotion without interpreting its meaning. The goal is to help the client *notice and name* the emotion as a distinct experience.

#### 3. **Differentiating Emotions**

Unmentalized bodily experiences usually involve *multiple* emotions that are causally linked. Explore each one separately and trace how they connect.

In the example above: the client felt anger *first*, then shame about the anger, then anxiety about what the anger might lead to, then a sense of emptiness from "shutting down." Help the client see this sequence: anger → shame → anxiety → shutdown.

#### 4. **Linking Emotions to Patterns**

Once emotions are recognized and differentiated, connect them to the client's prototypical ways of relating to self and others. This is where the client begins to *see themselves* in the pattern.

> "So when someone criticizes you, you respond with anger, but then quickly feel ashamed of your anger. That shame and anxiety make you shut yourself down. Is that right? And when you shut down, you end up feeling empty and drained. Does that match your experience?"

This micro-slicing of recent interpersonal events in the here-and-now of the session helps the client experience the link between patterns and symptoms *directly*.

#### 5. **Marking the Emotional Cost**

Finally, highlight what this difficulty *costs* the client emotionally and relationally. Validate it as understandable, but make explicit the downside.

> "I can see how this response makes sense—you're protecting yourself from overwhelming feelings. But it also leaves you unaware of what's actually happening to you, and you're stuck with all these feelings you can't process. That's a high price to pay."

This step helps the client develop motivation to change by seeing both the logic of their current pattern *and* its cost.

### Shifting from Physical to Psychological Self

Through this sequence, clients gradually move from experiencing themselves as a body with physical symptoms to experiencing themselves as a *mind* with emotions, thoughts, and agency. This shift is crucial: it restores a sense of self-coherence and makes change possible.

The RADLE approach is particularly important with depressed patients because it avoids jumping too quickly to insight or interpretation. Instead, it stays grounded in immediate, observable experience—the client's own body and emotions—and works outward from there.
