## Mentalizing Trauma Through Affective Reflection

Traumatic experiences become frozen in memory partly because they were not shared or processed "in company"—not mentalized with another person present. MBT-TF uses a structured yet dynamic process of elaboration to gradually link fragmented experiences into coherent narrative while maintaining the emotional vitality of the memory.

### The Three-Step Elaboration Process

The framework is not rigid; it flows based on the client's mentalizing capacity in the moment.

1. **Recall**: The client recounts what happened—the autobiographical facts and sequence. "What was he wearing? What did the room smell like?" This activates implicit emotional memory and anchors the narrative.

2. **Reflection (Implications)**: The group asks about the feelings and thoughts that accompanied the memory. "What feelings go with that memory? What thoughts accompanied it?" This begins to link emotion to narrative, moving from pure sensation into emotional experience.

3. **Meta-Reflection**: The group reflects on the reflection—on the implications of what has been shared. "How does this memory affect you now? What feelings do you have in this moment as you remember it?" This creates the critical step of "remembering remembering"—awareness that the memory is now being held in a different context (the group, the present) than it was when it occurred.

Throughout, the clinician and group act as **conversational partners** (not advice-givers). They offer empathic validation of the experience ("with" the patient) rather than reassurance or solutions ("to" the patient). The aim is to accompany the client and prevent avoidance—to gradually activate different memory systems until the trauma is no longer fragmented but integrated across autobiographical, emotional, procedural (how the person copes), and semantic (what it means) memory.

### Affective Reflection: Integration of Feeling and Thought

Full mentalization of trauma requires **affective reflection**—the integration of feelings and thoughts into "felt feelings," where the person experiences the emotion while simultaneously reflecting on it. This happens on multiple levels:

- **I-mode**: Mentalizing one's own inner experience
- **Me-mode**: Understanding others' minds and perspectives  
- **Personalized me-mode**: Considering how others see and experience you

The clinician brings the group's attention to all three. "How might others in the group see your experience?" and "Is what she thinks what you really thought?" invite the client to hold their memory in the minds of others without losing themselves.

### Avoiding Common Pitfalls

The clinician must watch for thought avoidance, which appears as:

- **Slipping into generalities**: Moving away from the specific traumatic episode into abstract discussion
- **Refusal to contemplate**: Shutting down the narrative
- **Destruction of thought**: Dissociation or fragmentation

A common trap is jumping too quickly into semantic memory (meaning)—"What does this say about you as a person?"—before the implicit emotional and procedural memories have been fully explored. This move into meaning can trigger **pretend mode**, severing affect from thought and leading to detached, meaningless discussion. The clinician keeps the group grounded in the specific, emotionally vivid details while managing participants' anxiety through the supportive presence of the group.
