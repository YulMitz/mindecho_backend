## Non-Mentalizing Modes: Identification and Function

When a patient's mentalizing becomes rigidly imbalanced across the four dimensions, they typically stabilize themselves using one of three non-mentalizing modes. These are adaptive—they "save the day" by preventing complete disorganization—but they trap the patient in limited, inflexible ways of understanding themselves and others.

**Key point:** These modes are not pathological; they are solutions to extreme confusion. The task is not to eliminate them but to build the patient's capacity to *shift out* of them.

### **Psychic Equivalence (Know and Now Mind / Inside-Out Mind)**

**What it is:** An unerring belief in one's own perspective, with apparent inability or unwillingness to represent others' perspectives as distinct. Thoughts are experienced as facts; images are reality.

**How it shows up:**
- Shallow, schematic representations of self and others (*victim/victimizer*, *good/bad*, *loved/rejected*)
- Rigidity of self-experience that can be both painful and immutable
- Certainty about internal states that contradicts observable evidence
- Present-moment perspective treated as permanent reality

*Clinical marker:* Patient says *"He doesn't love me"* with absolute conviction, despite evidence. The thought is not questioned or treated as an anxiety—it *is* reality. Patient experiences rejection as a current, definite fact rather than as an anxious thought.

**In formulation language:** "You feel unloved and unwanted, which in this moment is experienced as current and definite reality rather than an anxious thought that might shift."

---

### **Teleological Mode (Action Person / Outside-In Mind)**

**What it is:** Reliance on external, physical change to produce internal psychological change. The logic: *I am what my actions tell me. You are what you do.* Internal states are accessed only through action and observable outcomes.

**How it shows up:**
- Compulsive behavior driven by the belief that physical change will fix internal states
- Risk-taking, substance use, self-harm, sexual behavior as stabilization strategies
- Inability to wait, reflect, or tolerate ambiguity without acting
- Sense that internal state can only change if someone else acts toward them or shows them physically that they are cared for

*Clinical marker:* Patient feels "Yuk" when alone, leaves the flat, enters a bar, is noticed by someone—and suddenly feels real, matters, is loved. The sequence: *external action → physical/sensory change → internal state shift*. Patient cannot stabilize internally without triggering physical-world events.

**The vulnerability:** This is a compulsive adaptation to intolerable emptiness or dread. While it "works" temporarily, it leaves the patient vulnerable to high-risk environments and dependent on others' physical actions for emotional stability.

**In formulation language:** "Your terrible empty feelings when alone drive you to go out and seek external proof that you matter. The moment someone notices you, you feel real again."

---

### **Pretend Mode (Bubble Mode / Talking the Talk But Not Walking the Walk)**

**What it is:** Disconnection between understanding and action; between describing mental states and living them. The person retreats into their own world, understanding seems sophisticated but is circular and ungrounded, and descriptions bear little relation to behavior.

**How it shows up:**
- Blandness of description with little elaboration (*"He's fine," "We're perfect"*)
- Dialogue that stops unexpectedly or goes in circles without resolution
- Insistence that everything is fine while behavior contradicts the claim
- Lack of emotional resonance; rumination without
