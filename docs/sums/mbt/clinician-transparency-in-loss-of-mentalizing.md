## Clinician Transparency in Loss of Mentalizing

One of MBT's defining moves is the clinician's willingness to disclose moments when their own mentalizing fails. This is not self-disclosure about your personal life—it is transparency about your current mental state *in relation to the patient and the work*.

**Why this matters:** When the patient undermines your mentalizing capacity—through intimidation, touching on latent fears, or creating anxiety—you cannot think clearly or deliver effective help. The patient can sense this and becomes confused: *Why is my therapist suddenly ineffective?* If you hide the breakdown, the patient is left alone with an incomprehensible clinician. If you name it, you model recovery and prevent collusion.

**When to disclose:**
- Your mind goes blank or you become uncertain how to respond
- The interaction is creating interference in the work (you're defensive, pulled off-center, emotionally reactive)
- You recognize the patient has touched something in you that makes thinking difficult

**How to do it:**
- Simple and direct: *"You know, I'm not sure what to say about that. You have stumped me there. Help me think about it."*
- Or: *"I need a moment to get my mind back. I'm not sure what I think here. Can we wait a minute?"*
- Acknowledge the shared nature: *"Something in what you're saying has made it hard for me to think clearly."*

**The effect:** By naming the breakdown and asking the patient to help you recover mentalizing, you simultaneously:
1. Prevent the patient from experiencing a clinician who has abandoned mentalizing
2. Help the patient see the effects they have on others (crucial social learning for people with personality disorder)
3. Activate your own better mentalizing through the act of putting your thoughts into words
4. Model that mentalizing failure is normal and recoverable—not catastrophic

This is a benign openness that keeps your mind an "open book" for the patient to consider.
