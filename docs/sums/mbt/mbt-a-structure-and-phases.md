## MBT-A: Treatment Structure and Delivery

Mentalization-based treatment for adolescents (MBT-A) is typically delivered as individual sessions combined with family therapy (MBT-F) and/or group sessions (MBT-G). Standard treatment runs for approximately 12 months, though this can be adjusted based on severity and complexity.

### Assessment Phase (Approximately 2 weeks)

The assessment involves:
- One or two individual sessions with an individual therapist
- One or two sessions with a family therapist (ideally a different person)
- Use of diagnostic and mentalizing measures (e.g., Reflective Functioning Questionnaire for Youth)
- Assessment of family functioning and factors that disrupt parents' mentalizing

The assessment gathers information on the adolescent's presenting difficulties (self-harm, depression, anxiety, eating problems, aggression), their mentalizing capacities, their family history and current relationships, and the stressors and social contexts that trigger dysregulation.

### Initial Phase: Formulation and Contracting

**Written Formulation**: The adolescent receives a clear, collaboratively developed written formulation that explains:
- Their presenting difficulties and symptoms
- Their mentalizing strengths and vulnerabilities
- How their social, family, and school environments contribute to dysregulation
- Non-mentalizing patterns they tend to fall into (hypermentalizing, psychic equivalence, etc.)
- A path forward for recovery and change

The same formulation process occurs with parents, acknowledging that different family members may have different perspectives on the problems.

**Crisis Plan**: For adolescents at risk of self-harm or suicidality, a detailed crisis plan is developed that:
- Identifies specific triggers (rejection, humiliation, feelings of worthlessness)
- Proposes a 10-minute delay strategy: pause the urge to act, reflect on what triggered the feeling
- Once reflection occurs, brainstorm alternatives to self-harm (physical activity, distraction, talking to someone)
- Describes what the adolescent can do if mentalizing fails (call the clinic, reach out to someone they trust)
- Educates the family on self-harm and suicide risk, with guidance on how to respond with support rather than panic or punishment

**Contract**: An agreement is made regarding:
- Duration and frequency of treatment (typically individual sessions weekly, family sessions monthly)
- Commitment required from the adolescent and parents
- Expectations around attendance and engagement

**Psychoeducation**: A session (individual or multi-family group) introduces the core principles:
- Behavior has meaning rooted in mental states and feelings
- Feelings arise in relational contexts
- People have powerful emotional impacts on one another

### Middle Phase (Typically 9–10 months)

The bulk of therapeutic work occurs here. Sessions are largely unstructured, following the adolescent's current and recent interpersonal experiences.

**Key interventions**:
- Support and empathy, especially when the adolescent is dysregulated
- Clarification and elaboration of difficult interactions, tracing the build-up of feelings and the meaning attributed to events
- Affect identification and elaboration, paying careful attention to emotion and its role in escalating the interaction
- Challenge (used sparingly and gently), introducing alternative perspectives once the adolescent is more stable
- Mentalizing the relationship: Helping the adolescent notice what is happening between them and the therapist, and how their patterns play out in the room

**Impulse control and safety management**: Specific work on managing suicidal, parasuicidal, and other harmful behaviors (substance use, aggression, eating disturbances).

- Build relational stability and support agency and autonomy within the adolescent's relationships
- Develop independence from the therapist
- Create a coping plan for managing difficulties if they recur
- Appointments become more widely spaced as discharge approaches
