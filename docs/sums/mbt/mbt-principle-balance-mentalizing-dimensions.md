## Balancing Mentalizing Across All Dimensions

Mentalizing has four key dimensions (see Chapter 2 for detail), and patients with personality disorders characteristically over- or underuse certain poles. The goal is not to move the patient to a single "correct" pole, but to restore *flexibility*—the ability to move back and forth depending on context.

**The Four Dimensions**

1. **Automatic vs. Controlled**: Spontaneous gut reactions vs. deliberate reflection
2. **Self vs. Other**: Focusing on one's own states vs. understanding others' perspectives
3. **Cognitive vs. Affective**: Thinking about mental states vs. feeling into them
4. **Internal vs. External**: Looking inward for causes vs. attending to observable behavior and context

**Characteristic Patterns in BPD**

Patients with BPD, especially when anxious or in attachment-activation contexts, show:

- **Self/other fusion**: Cannot distinguish between what they feel and how they believe the other person experiences them. Their shame and self-criticism collapse together with their sense of being rejected or devalued by the other. The boundary between self and other gets blurry.
- **Affective hyperactivation**: Flooded by feeling, without enough access to reflective distance or cognitive mentalizing to modulate it.

**The Contrary Move Technique**

When you observe that a patient is stuck in one pole, a "contrary move" deliberately activates the opposite pole to restore balance. Examples:

- **If fused self/other**: Ask them to separate—"What did you feel when he said that?" (their feeling) vs. "What do you think *he* was feeling when he said it?" (his perspective). Help them sit with the discomfort of two separate experiences at once.

- **If overwhelmed by affect**: Move toward cognitive mentalizing—"Let's slow down and think about what happened step by step." This gives them scaffolding and distance.

- **If overly cognitive and disconnected**: Move toward affective—"As you think about that, what comes up for you in your body or heart?"

The contrary move is not an instruction or a reframe. It is a gentle shift in the direction of inquiry that invites the patient's mentalizing to flex and strengthen.
