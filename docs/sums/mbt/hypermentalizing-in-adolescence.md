## Hypermentalizing: A Common Adolescent Pattern

Hypermentalizing is a distinctive phenomenon in adolescence, particularly in young people developing borderline personality features or experiencing social anxiety. It involves the adolescent frantically trying to figure out what others are thinking—but doing so with excessive certainty and typically distorted conclusions.

### What Hypermentalizing Looks Like

Rather than thinking "I wonder what she meant by that," the hypermentalizing adolescent concludes with certainty: "She was flirting with my boyfriend. I know exactly what she's doing—she wants to ruin my relationship." The thoughts may become increasingly elaborate and persecuted: "She's a slag. She's trying to show everyone I'm worthless. She knows how shit I am."

What feels like mentalizing is actually a collapse into psychic equivalence—the adolescent's fearful internal state becomes the external reality. Their jealousy and shame become facts about the world.

### Why Hypermentalizing Develops

Hypermentalizing is partially adaptive. By frantically constructing theories about others' intentions, the adolescent creates an illusion of control and predictability. It also protects—paradoxically—against an even more frightening collapse into non-mentalizing, impulsive action, or the intrusion of the alien self (see below).

However, hypermentalizing escalates distress. Each theory spawns more worry, rumination, and evidence-seeking ("Why did she say that? What was that look?"). The adolescent becomes increasingly dysregulated and, unable to think clearly, may act on their false convictions—leading to social conflicts, self-harm, or aggressive behavior.

### Therapeutic Approach

Do not directly challenge or argue against hypermentalizing thoughts. This typically deepens defensiveness and conviction.

Instead:
- **Validate the feeling**: "I can see that your friend telling you this made you feel really jealous and upset."
- **Normalize the affect**: "Hearing something like that would make anyone's mind go there."
- **Slow the escalation**: "Let's look at how it jumped so far so quickly. You went from upset to certainty about what he wants to do."
- **Introduce uncertainty gently**: "I wonder... what are other ways he could have been talking to her?" (Not as an argument, but as genuine curiosity.)
- **Connect to pattern**: Over time, help the adolescent notice their own hypermentalizing process: "I've heard you do this before—you get one piece of information and then your mind fills in a whole story about what people are thinking."

The goal is not to make the adolescent stop hypermentalizing but to help them recognize it as their mind's protective strategy, and gradually tolerate more uncertainty about others' intentions without needing to resolve it through action or self-harm.
