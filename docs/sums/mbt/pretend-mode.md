## Pretend Mode: Recognizing and Disrupting False Mentalizing

Pretend mode is an ominous clinical sign because it prevents change while appearing to facilitate it. The client talks about their mind and others' minds, but with a hollow quality—the conversation is occurring between the client and themselves, with the clinician present but not truly engaged.

### Signs of Pretend Mode

Pretend mode has a distinctive clinical presentation:
- **Excessive rationalization and intellectualization**: The client offers convoluted explanations for behavior that lack emotional grounding.
- **Narrative feels hard to follow**: Recursive reasoning about what others think about what they think about what they think, leading to perplexity.
- **Bland, "canned" comments**: Observations about self, others, or therapy sound like textbook responses, predictable and disconnected.
- **Felt disconnection in dialogue**: There is a sense that the clinician is not quite present or in the same room as the client. Engagement feels hollow.
- **Hypermentalizing**: Over-recruitment of cognitive processing with limited affectively informed experience.

The text uses the colloquial term "bullshit mode"—not pejoratively, but descriptively. It's as if the client is talking to themselves, and the clinician is needed only to maintain the *pretense* of communication.

### Why Pretend Mode Matters

Pretend mode is self-serving and fails to engender change. It keeps things the same. The client feels as if they are working, as if they are mentalizing, but they are actually reinforcing defensive patterns. The illusory link between clinician's mind and patient's mind means no genuine mutual recognition can occur.

### How to Intervene

The text outlines four approaches, to be used in sequence if the initial intervention does not shift the state:

**1. Probe and Name**

Ask penetrating questions about the content of the narrative—not to express doubt, but to make clear that you are a real part of the conversation. Comment on the qualities of the presentation: "You seem rather flat as you tell me about that." The aim is to strike something that shatters the illusion of monologue masquerading as conversation.

**2. Increase Immediate Relational Anxiety**

Pretend mode is sensitive to current relational affective experience. When there is momentary immediacy of minds, the client's isolation can no longer maintain its "quarantine" from representing other minds. Make counterintuitive or "what if?" interventions that bring focus onto the clinician and current moment: "But what does a good mother who wants to look after Jack look like?"

**3. Challenge with Counterfactuals**

Introduce surprise into the interaction. This can include light-hearted mischief that is unexpected and outside the normal therapeutic dialogue. The principle is to trip up the pretend-mode pattern with something the client's prepared defenses cannot absorb.

**4. Therapeutic "Ambush"**

If earlier maneuvers do not work, use a more direct challenge—communicated in a tone of light-hearted mischief and, ideally, with shared humor. The clinician's example of looking out the window while thinking, then responding to Sarah's complaint with a paradoxical comment about gender and multitasking, illustrates this. The surprise gives time for the clinician to present their authentic state of mind, which Sarah can no longer ignore.

### Critical Caution

Clinicians worry about disrupting pretend mode because the content often appears insight
