## The Not-Knowing Stance

The "not-knowing" stance is the MBT clinician's most effective avenue for exploring problems hand-in-hand, so the patient doesn't have to venture into dark places alone. It is rooted in authenticity: you genuinely do not fully know about mental states because they are subjective and never fully knowable. What you know is the framework of MBT and the patterns of difficulty typical in personality disorder—but not *this person's* mind.

**Key distinction:** Not-knowing is not the same as ignorance or incompetence. You know about BPD as a set of psychological processes. You are tentative about the constructions you make—treating your own mental-state observations with the same provisional quality you offer to the patient. Neither mind is privileged.

**What derails it:** When the not-knowing stance becomes an interrogation. This happens especially with repeated "why" questions, which patients experience as accusatory and which activate demanding cognitive effort when mentalizing is already compromised. "Why are you sad?" sounds like blame. Most people don't know "why" anyway.

**The corrective:** Think about how you'd talk to a friend with the same problem. You wouldn't ask "why"; you'd ask them to elaborate on what happened and what it felt like. You'd express genuine astonishment and curiosity: *"Tell me what it was like for you. What did you think about it?"* This pulls mentalizing into the session without interrogating it.

**Three questioning modes exist—use all three as needed:**
1. **Fact-finding**: Establishing what actually happened (useful early on)
2. **Socratic questioning**: Open but cognitively focused (useful for building understanding)
3. **Exploratory, not-knowing stance**: Interactive discussion rooted in genuine curiosity (essential for building trust and mentalizing once rapport is established)

The not-knowing stance promotes "we-mode"—shared intentionality where both minds are focused on understanding the same problem together.
