## Establishing Group Values and Agreements

In MBT groups, the explicit discussion and agreement of group values is not a bureaucratic exercise—it is a therapeutic intervention in itself. Values work teaches mentalizing by requiring members to think about why a principle matters, how to recognize it in action, and how it applies to their own lives.

### The Three-Part Value Discussion

For each proposed value (respect, tolerance, acceptance, kindness), guide the group through:

1. **Personal relevance**: Is this important to *me*, or am I adopting it because I think others expect me to? This fosters "I-mode" mentalizing about their own beliefs.

2. **Behavioral definition**: If we agree it's important, what does it actually look like? What are concrete signs of respect vs. disrespect, fairness vs. unfairness? This moves from abstract to observable, anchoring values in shared reality.

3. **Life application**: How does this value play out in my relationships right now? This bridges the group experience to outside life and increases ownership.

### Common Values in MBT Groups

- **Respect**: Listening to each other; thinking about the impact we have on others; accepting that people can disagree while still valuing each other
- **Kindness**: Using kind or polite language even when upset; sharing, waiting turns, giving positive regard
- **Curiosity**: Asking genuine questions; accepting difference; resisting quick judgment
- **Safety**: Not threatening, damaging, or verbally attacking; managing conflict constructively

### Clinical Attention Points

Watch for members who struggle to distinguish between agreement and respect. Help them see that you can respect someone while disagreeing. Notice when values become rhetorical—members reciting them without genuine buy-in—and revisit with curiosity rather than correction.

Document agreed values on a visible "Values Sheet" that can be printed or emailed. This creates accountability and continuity across sessions.
