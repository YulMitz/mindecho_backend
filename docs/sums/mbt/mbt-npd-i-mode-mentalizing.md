## Stabilizing "I-Mode" Mentalizing in Narcissistic Clients

"I-mode" refers to the client's direct, first-person sense of who they are—their internal self-experience. In narcissistic personality functioning, this can be rigidly structured, defended, or disconnected from authentic emotional awareness. The first therapeutic task is to help the client develop clearer contact with their own internal states before exploring how others perceive them.

### The Challenge of Self-Exploration

Many narcissistic clients are "unusually well read" about psychology, philosophy, or self-help. They can analyze problems intellectually but find it difficult to access or articulate how they actually *feel*. Feelings may be dismissed as weakness, irrelevance, or distraction. The client may say: "I don't think about feelings much—I prefer to work things out logically."

**Gently separate thinking from awareness.** Ask: "What are you *aware of*, rather than what do you *think*?" This distinction invites the client to notice their immediate internal state—bodily sensations, emotions, impulses—without the mediation of intellectual analysis.

### Identifying Hypermentalizing

Some clients with narcissistic features engage in constant mental monitoring of themselves and others. They listen intently for any comment that might refer to them, mentally work out relationship implications, and track whether the clinician appreciates or criticizes them. This creates a state of perpetual vigilance that prevents spontaneity and genuine connection.

**Name it gently.** Use accessible language: "You mentioned that even while you're with me, part of your mind is monitoring whether I'm appreciating you or criticizing you. That's a lot of thinking—a lot of working things out. What's that like, to live with that constant surveillance in your mind?"

This opens space for the client to notice the cost of their hypervigilance and consider whether there might be another way.

### Affect Focus: Finding the Feeling Beneath the Armor

Narcissistic clients often present with grandiose narratives ("I'm vindicated; they were stupid; I'm moving on") that contain hidden vulnerability. The clinician's task is to slow down, stay curious, and probe gently for the affective reality.

**Clinical example:** A client describes being criticized by their manager in front of colleagues. They initially say it was unfair and they walked out. When you ask, "Is it important that this was done in front of other people?" they suddenly acknowledge: "Yes. It was to humiliate me. I couldn't bear it."

This is the breakthrough moment. The client has made contact with shame and hurt beneath the defensive superiority. **Do not push further at this moment.** Immediately return to empathic validation: "That's really painful—especially when they see you as the exact opposite of how you are. Managing being exposed like that is so hard."

The goal is to help the client *hold* this vulnerable awareness, not dissociate from it or retreat into defensive grandiosity.

### Coherence of the "I": Integration Over Defensiveness

Over time, the work aims to generate greater coherence in the client's self-representation. This means:

- Acknowledging both strengths and genuine limitations
- Integrating moments of success with moments of failure without black-and-white thinking
- Tolerating the existence of contrasting self-states (capable *and* sensitive, intelligent *and* sometimes wrong)
- Building an embodied, continuous sense of personhood rather than a fragmented or context-dependent self

This is slow work and requires patience. The client's defensive structure has protected them; removing it too quickly creates unbearable exposure.
