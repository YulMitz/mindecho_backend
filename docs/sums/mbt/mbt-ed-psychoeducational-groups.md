## MBT-I-ED: Psychoeducational Introduction to Mentalization for Eating Disorders

Psychoeducational groups serve as the introductory phase of MBT for eating disorders (MBT-ED). Typically 5–10 sessions of MBT-I-ED precede the main therapeutic work. These groups introduce patients to the mentalizing perspective and orient them to how the treatment model works.

### Core Topics and Structure

The sessions focus on themes directly relevant to eating disorders:
- **The nature of mentalizing** — with particular emphasis on *communication* (more than in BPD groups), because avoidant tendencies are common in this population
- **Misunderstandings and relational difficulties** — how patients struggle to "read" others and to know themselves
- **Social difficulties** — recognizing how eating disorder symptoms function as social self-regulation attempts

### Therapeutic Stance and Delivery

The clinician adopts a **didactic style that requires patient contribution**. Rather than lecturing, the therapist presents material and actively invites patients to:
- Provide personal examples
- Present alternative perspectives from their own history
- Illustrate particular points through lived experience

**Discussion is kept at a high level** — deliberately abstract rather than deeply personal. This is protective because the topics themselves (body image, eating, identity) and the experience of being in a group can trigger significant distress in patients with eating disorders.

**Language is simple and concrete.** Examples used should come from the therapist's clinical experience with other eating disorder patients — these relatable vignettes can trigger emotional reactions, so the therapist maintains a **light-hearted, non-intense atmosphere** throughout.

### What NOT to Do in MBT-I-ED

- Do not allow discussions to become "hot" or emotionally intense
- Do not encourage in-depth personal exploration at this stage
- Do not allow these sessions to resemble the intensity of weekly group therapy

The aim is to create a safe, educational space where patients begin to understand the model without becoming overwhelmed.

### Engagement and Retention

Evidence from the NOURISHED RCT suggests that attendance patterns matter: patients with co-occurring BPD and eating disorders had difficulty staying engaged in a 12-month program that included MBT-I-ED (5 sessions) followed by both individual and group sessions. This suggests that:
- 18 months of treatment may be more appropriate for comorbid presentations
- Day-hospital contexts may be necessary for patients with both personality disorders and eating disorders
- The first 3 months should consist of psychoeducational work (MBT-I-ED)
