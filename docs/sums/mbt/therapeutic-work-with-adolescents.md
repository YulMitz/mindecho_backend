## Working Therapeutically with Adolescents

Adolescents occupy a unique developmental position. Their capacity to think about mental states—their own and others'—is still maturing and remains sensitive to stress, shame, and social threat. Unlike adults, adolescents can show dramatic swings between sophisticated reflection and complete collapse of mentalizing, especially under interpersonal pressure.

### Understanding Adolescent Mentalizing Patterns

Two common presentations require distinct therapeutic approaches:

**Hypermentalizing**: Some adolescents develop intense, persecutory theories about what others think of them. They are certain—without evidence—that peers despise or reject them, that teachers want to stress them out, or that friends are deliberately trying to humiliate them. This "excessive certainty" about others' hostile mental states, though it looks like mentalizing, is actually a form of distorted thinking that protects against a deeper collapse into non-mentalizing. The adolescent is frantically trying to predict threats but doing so inaccurately. Helping them recognize and gently challenge their hypermentalizing (rather than arguing directly against it) can reduce the anxiety driving their defensive responses.

**Non-mentalizing or concrete thinking**: Other adolescents struggle to access mental-state language at all. They describe feelings as purely physical ("I have a headache," "I feel sick") or global and inexplicable ("I hate everything, that's all"). They may see their teachers' demands simply as stress-causing, with no curiosity about why or what their teachers intend. This young person needs the therapist to validate their experience first, show genuine interest in the specifics of what happened, and only gradually introduce the possibility of hidden, complex thoughts and feelings.

### The Therapeutic Stance: Warmth Without Pressure

The mentalizing stance with adolescents must embody authentic curiosity and compassion while respecting their separateness. Avoid premature or heavy-handed mentalizing ("You're really angry at your parents deep down"). Instead, use warmth, light-heartedness, and self-deprecation to lower defenses: "I know I probably get things wrong most of the time, but I wonder if…"

This stance is particularly important because adolescents in distress often expect disdain from adults. By showing genuine, non-judgmental interest in their inner world, you help them experience themselves as worth understanding—a corrective emotional experience that directly supports mentalizing development.

### Key Intervention Sequence

1. **Support and empathy first**: When an adolescent describes a difficult day or interaction, validate how hard it was. "That sounds really awful."
2. **Clarification and elaboration**: Only after they feel understood, explore the detail: "Can you walk me through what happened?" Often the story reveals escalating affect and distorted thinking about peers' intentions.
3. **Affect identification**: Pay close attention to emotions as they emerge. Notice how quickly feelings can intensify and lead to action (self-harm, aggression). Help the young person slow down and recognize the feeling states driving their behavior.
4. **Light challenge**: Only when mentalizing is more stable can you gently introduce less catastrophic interpretations. "I wonder if maybe the girls weren't trying to humiliate you, even though it felt that way?"

The timing and tone matter enormously. Too early or too dogmatic a challenge will feel like criticism and rupture trust.
