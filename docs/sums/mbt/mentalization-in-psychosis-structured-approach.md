## The Mentalizing Process in Psychosis: Scaffolded Intervention

Unlike work with other client groups, mentalizing interventions with psychotic patients must be carefully scaffolded from "safe" to more activating. The text outlines four interconnected elements that underpin effective mentalizing work.

### Element 1: Establishing Joint Attention Through Marked Communication

Before you can invite a psychotic patient into mentalizing, you need joint attention—a shared focus between you and the patient. This is achieved through **meta-communication**, which is most effective when you explicitly mark it as coming from you.

**Examples:**

- *"Robert, something came to my mind that I'd like to share, if that's OK? You were talking about your dad, and then somehow I lost what you wanted to say about your relationship with him."*

- *"I'm noticing you are very quiet and not looking at me today. That's fine with me. I just wanted to check in whether everything was OK for you today, being here?"*

- *"Can I just ask for your attention? We're about to transition to the next topic. Is that all right for everybody?"*

In each example, the clinician affectively marks their own perspective as a starting point for questions about the patient's mental states. Marking signals: *This is my thought. I'm sharing it. I'm distinguishing between my mind and yours.* This repeated modeling is critical because it teaches the patient to distinguish self from other—a capacity often compromised in psychosis.

### Element 2: Using Mediation Tools and Playfulness

There is no fixed set of validated mediation tools for MBT with psychosis, so flexibility and responsiveness are essential. **Playfulness** is probably the most underrated intervention in psychosis work—likely because negative symptoms dampen both the clinician's and patient's affect, creating a false impression that playfulness would be unwelcome.

Playfulness is actually very safe in the MBT framework because you are constantly monitoring its effect on arousal. If the patient becomes dysregulated, you adjust. If they engage, you've opened a channel for mentalizing that doesn't rely on verbal abstraction.

Other mediation tools include:
- Drawing or simple visual metaphors
- Games or structured activities that externalize and make visible what is hard to talk about
- Movement or gesture to connect non-verbal communication with mental-state processing

The key principle: **Link mediation tools to the formulation and treatment objectives.** Make explicit the path between in-session activities and real-world change.

### Element 3: Making Your Thought Process Explicit, Genuine, and Simple

Many psychotic patients experience mentalizing questions as literal intrusions into their mind or as tests of whether they are "crazy." One antidote is to **divulge your own clear, simple mental processes as a model.**

Explain what you are thinking in plain language. For example:

*"I'm wondering whether you felt hurt when your manager said that, or whether you felt angry, or both?"*

This is better than:

*"Tell me about your emotional response to that interaction."*

The first is transparent and human. The second is abstract and potentially mentalizing-as-intrusion.

### Element 4: Connecting Non-Verbal Communication to Mental-State Thinking

The clinician makes explicit links between observable behavior and inner experience:

- *"Robert, I can't see what type of face I'm making, but here I am wondering: how can I interrupt you to ask a question?"* (Points to own face and head)

- *"Johanna, I'm leaning in here because I'm wondering whether we've said something that made you go silent. Would you like to comment on that, or do you wish to keep silent for now?"*

These interventions model the process of decoding non-verbal signals and translating them into curiosity about minds. They make mentalizing observable and teachable.

### A Word on Hyper-Reflectivity

Playfulness and explicit thought-sharing can risk triggering hyper-reflectivity (excessive, circular self-reflection) in some psychotic patients. The guard against this is **calibration**. You adjust the intensity, frequency, and depth of mentalizing based on the patient's moment-to-moment capacity. If the patient becomes lost in their own mind or more fragmented, you pause, ground them in the here-and-now, and return to safer interventions.
