## Process Before Content: How Mental States Are Processed

One of the easiest traps for a clinician is to take a patient's unmentalized content at face value and elaborate it. This feels helpful—you are engaging, showing understanding, building on their ideas. But it actually *reinforces low mentalizing* and misses the point entirely.

**The Content vs. Process Distinction**

- **Content** = the "what" and "why": "What am I thinking? Why did I feel that way? What does my partner's behavior mean?"
- **Process** = the "how": How am I processing this experience? What quality of awareness am I bringing to it? Am I genuinely curious or locked in certainty?

When a patient says, "My therapist thinks I am selfish because I care about myself," they are presenting content. The therapist might naturally respond: "But you do care about others too. Your therapist is wrong about that." This feels supportive. But you have just *agreed* with the patient's unmentalized assertion (that the therapist definitely thinks this, that the patient's self-concern is the core issue) and elaborated it. You have reinforced the low-mentalizing frame.

**The Mentalizing Alternative**

Instead, pause and notice: *How is the patient processing this? Are they genuinely wondering about it, or are they locked in a conviction?* If locked, then address the process first:

"I notice as you say that, it sounds like it's settled truth for you—that your therapist sees you as selfish. But I'm curious: did they use that word, or is that how you're interpreting their reaction? And when you look at that, does it feel certain, or does it feel uncertain?"

You are not debating content. You are inviting the patient back into a mentalizing stance—one of genuine curiosity and openness to multiple interpretations.

**Hypermentalizing and Pretend Mode**

Be alert to a subtle form of poor mentalizing: **hypermentalizing**. This is when the patient (or the clinician!) describes mental states in elaborate, psychologically sophisticated language—but with no real emotional grounding or connection to lived experience. It sounds like genuine insight. It is not. It is what Chapter 2 calls "pretend mode"—a kind of performance of understanding that mimics mentalizing without being it.

Example: A patient says, "I have deep-seated abandonment fears stemming from my mother's emotional unavailability, which activates my anxious-preoccupied attachment style, causing me to project my need for reassurance onto my partner." This is *content*. It may be true. But if the patient is reciting it without feeling, without genuine curiosity about their own experience, and without any sense that it *connects* to what is happening right now in their body or in the room—then it is hypermentalizing, not real mentalizing.

**Do Not Take Over the Patient's Mentalizing**

Clinicians, by training and temperament, are good at understanding psychology. This is a curse in MBT. If you use your robust mentalizing capacities to solve the patient's problem, you are doing their "mentalizing muscles" no good. You are preventing the exercise that builds strength.

So: Offer alternatives. Ask questions. But when the patient is struggling to understand something, resist the urge to finish the thought for them or to present a polished interpretation. Instead, say: "I have a thought, but I'm more interested in what comes to you. What do you think?"
