## Continuous Reinforcement: Getting Behavior to Stick—At First

Continuous reinforcement means reinforcing *every single instance* of a designated behavior. This schedule is useful in the early stages of learning or shaping because it produces very high frequency of the behavior quickly. However, it has a critical drawback: behaviors on continuous reinforcement are fragile and disappear rapidly once reinforcement stops.

### When Continuous Reinforcement Works Best

Continuous reinforcement is ideal for:
- **Initial learning** of a new behavior
- **Shaping** the first steps toward a larger goal
- **Building confidence** through consistent success early on

### The Problem: Behavioral Fragility

Once a behavior is established on continuous reinforcement, it becomes dependent on that reinforcement schedule. The moment reinforcement stops, the behavior collapses quickly.

**Real-world examples:**

- **Vending machine analogy**: A vending machine that always produces a soda every time you insert money reinforces your use of that machine continuously. But the moment it fails once or twice, you stop using it. You won't keep trying an unreliable machine.

- **Calling a parent**: If your mother always answers the phone, you call regularly. But if she doesn't answer one day, you might be alarmed enough to drive over to check on her. If she *intermittently* answers the phone, you'll keep calling for days—because intermittent reinforcement is much more durable.

- **Car starting**: A reliable car (always starts) teaches you to expect immediate results. When it fails once, you try a few times but quickly give up. An unreliable car (often fails, but sometimes starts after multiple tries) teaches you persistence—you'll keep trying much longer.

### The Clinical Implication

Continuous reinforcement gets behavior going fast but doesn't sustain it. Once a behavior is established (and reliable), you need to **transition to intermittent reinforcement** to make it durable and independent of your constant feedback. This is why shaping protocols always include a plan to gradually space out reinforcement.

### In DBT Context

This principle explains why some clients appear to comply well in sessions (where continuous feedback and validation occur) but struggle to maintain new behaviors at home (where reinforcement is sporadic or absent). The solution is to deliberately build intermittent reinforcement into the learning process and coach clients on self-reinforcement.
