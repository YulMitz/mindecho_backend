## One-Mindfully: Complete Presence in This Moment

One-mindfully, the second "how" skill in DBT mindfulness, means bringing your entire self to bear on what you are doing right now. It has two parts: being completely present to the current moment, and doing only one thing at a time. Both involve deliberate focus.

### The Reality of Past and Future

**The past is over.** It does not exist now. Clients may have vivid thoughts, images, and emotions about the past—but these experiences are happening *now*, in the present. When a client gets lost in rumination ("I can't believe I said that; they must think I'm stupid; I'll never be able to face them"), their mind has traveled backward. They are not aware of what is actually happening in front of them right now. Their emotions may feel identical to emotions they felt in the past, creating the illusion that they are reliving it. They are not.

**The future has not come into existence.** Clients can plan for the future while being present (planning is a present-moment activity), but when they spend hours worrying about "what if" scenarios that haven't happened, they are lost in imagination. The worry is real; the threat is not. Like past rumination, future worry eats up the present moment and often creates emotional pain that mirrors the imagined scenario.

### Why One-Mindfulness Works

**It reduces suffering.** A toothache hurts. But if you add hours of worry ("What if it's infected? What if I need a root canal?"), you have multiplied the pain. The pain of the present moment is sufficient. Adding past regret and future dread is unnecessary suffering.

**Research shows multitasking is ineffective.** Most people believe doing two things at once saves time. It does not. Switching attention between tasks slows both down and reduces quality. Trying to listen in a meeting while checking your phone means you don't fully do either. One-mindfully actually gets things done faster.

**Life passes by unnoticed.** When clients are on autopilot—thinking about yesterday or tomorrow instead of being present—they miss the texture of their lives. They don't taste their food. They don't hear what a loved one is saying. They don't notice beauty. A client once said, "I realized I've been alive for 30 years but I haven't really *lived* any of it."

### How to Practice One-Mindfulness

**Be present to your own experience right now.** This is the opposite of avoiding, suppressing, or dissociating. It means noticing what you are actually experiencing: sensations, emotions, thoughts, movements.

**Rivet yourself to now.** Actively anchor your attention to the present moment. Use sensory anchors:
- Notice 5 things you can see, 4 you can hear, 3 you can touch, 2 you can smell, 1 you can taste.
- Feel your feet on the ground. Notice your breath.
- When your mind wanders to the past or future, gently notice it ("I'm thinking about what happened earlier") and return to what's happening now.

**Do one thing at a time.** You can have five dishes to wash, but you wash one at a time, with full attention to that dish. You can have a complex project with many steps, but you bring full awareness to each step as you do it. You are not mindlessly scrubbing while your mind spins about tomorrow's meeting. You are washing *this* dish, with your whole attention.

**Example interventions:**
- Client: "I'm so anxious about the presentation next week."
- You: "That's understandable. And right now, what's actually happening? You're sitting here with me. What do you notice in this moment?"
- Client (after pause): "I'm tense in my shoulders. I can hear traffic outside."
- You: "Yes. That's here and now. The presentation is next week. For now, we can work with what's actually present."

### The Paradox

Interestingly, focusing on the present moment often leads to better planning for the future. When you are calm and present, you think more clearly. You can ask, "What do I actually need to do to prepare?" instead of spinning in "what if" anxiety.

### Common Obstacles

- **Habit of avoidance:** Some clients use busyness and distraction to avoid difficult present-moment experiences. Gently name this: "I notice you keep switching topics. What are you aware of right now that might be uncomfortable?"
- **Attachment to outcome:** Worrying about the future is often an attempt to control it. Reassure: "Being present now doesn't mean you don't prepare. It means you prepare while present, not while anxious."
- **Resistance to "just being":** Some clients fear stillness or feel it's unproductive. Reframe: "Being fully present is actually the most productive state. Your mind and body work better when not split."
