## Orientation to Skills Training

The first session of DBT skills training is not just a logistical introduction—it is a *teaching* and *sales* opportunity. Orientation is where you help clients understand what they're about to invest time in, connect it to their personal goals, and establish the expectations and collaborative tone of the group.

### Core Purposes of Orientation

1. **Build motivation.** Help clients see how the skills connect to *their* specific struggles and goals
2. **Establish structure.** Clarify the format, schedule, attendance expectations, and why they matter
3. **Create psychological safety.** Introduce guidelines and assumptions that make the group feel safe and predictable
4. **Get explicit buy-in.** Clients agree to guidelines not because you're the authority, but because they see the value

### Key Components

**Introductions**
- Leaders and members introduce themselves, sharing briefly how they came to this work
- This personalizes the leaders and reduces the felt distance between "expert" and "learner"

**Overall Goal**
Present the overarching purpose: "We're here to learn skills for changing unwanted behaviors, emotions, thinking patterns, and life situations that cause you distress."

**Identifying Personal Goals**
This is critical. Ask clients to review the handout listing possible goals (organized by module) and check off which ones apply to them. Then:
- Ask them to circle the *most important* ones
- Have them write personal goals in their own words
- Discuss these with the group (even briefly)
- Return to these at the start of each new module to track progress

Doing this makes skills training *their* work, not something imposed on them.

**The Four Modules**
Present each module and its relevance:
1. **Mindfulness:** Focus, presence, observing without judgment
2. **Distress Tolerance:** Surviving crisis, accepting what you can't change
3. **Emotion Regulation:** Understanding and managing emotions
4. **Interpersonal Effectiveness:** Getting what you want, maintaining relationships, keeping self-respect

For each, ask: "Which of these areas is important to you?"

**Session Format**
Briefly describe: opening ritual, skills practice review, break, new material, closing wind-down. This makes the session predictable.

**Guidelines and Assumptions**
These are non-negotiable but not authoritarian. Discuss them as agreements everyone makes so the group works:

*Key guidelines:*
- **Attendance matters.** Missing 4 weeks in a row means you've dropped out. Consistency is how you learn.
- **Confidentiality.** What happens in group stays in group (except with individual therapists and if safety is at risk).
- **Support each other.** Come on time, do homework, give noncritical feedback, validate each other.
- **No judgment.** Everyone is learning; everyone stumbles.

*Key assumptions:*
- All behavior is learned and can be changed
- Clients are doing the best they can *and* need to do better
- Change is hard and requires practice
- Therapy involves some discomfort
- Clients don't fail—skills fail (if it's not working, we adjust the approach)

### Linking Goals to Skills

After identifying what clients want to change, explicitly connect it to the skills:
- "You want to stop yelling at your kids. That's an emotion regulation skill—we'll teach you how to calm down and respond instead of react."
- "You feel overwhelmed and don't know what to do. That's distress tolerance—we'll teach you ways to get through the crisis without making it worse."

This prevents skills from feeling abstract. Clients see: "I signed up to stop yelling. Here's how these skills help me do that."

### The Tone Matters

**Don't sound like you're laying down the law.** Instead:
- Frame guidelines as *agreements* that make group work
- Invite discussion: "Does this make sense? What questions do you have?"
- Acknowledge that some guidelines (like consistency) are hard and that's okay
- Have group members explain guidelines to new members when possible
- Revisit guidelines at the start of each module: "Are these still working? Do we need to adjust anything?"

A collaborative, non-authoritarian tone is especially important for clients with control issues or a history of feeling controlled. If orientation feels like punishment or control, you'll lose buy-in immediately.
