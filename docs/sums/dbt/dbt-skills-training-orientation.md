## Orienting Clients to DBT Skills Training

Clients often enter DBT with expectations shaped by previous therapy experiences. Clear orientation prevents misalignment and early dropout.

### How Skills Training Differs from Other Therapies

This distinction cannot be over-emphasized. Many clients have received unstructured psychotherapy where the agenda is open—whatever comes to mind is fair game, with focus on emotional exploration, insight, and relationship dynamics. DBT skills training is fundamentally different.

**Skills training is structured and agenda-driven:** The session topic is determined *before* the client arrives. It's the behavioral skill to be taught that week, not the client's current crisis or whatever feels most pressing. In individual psychotherapy, the therapist adjusts the agenda based on what the client brings; in skills training, the agenda is fixed.

**Skills training emphasizes practice and repetition:** Unlike exploratory or insight-oriented therapy, skills training is practical and behavioral. Clients learn specific techniques, practice them in session, and practice them at home. The focus is on *doing*, not just understanding.

**Sharing is focused, not unlimited:** While group skills training includes sharing and mutual support, it is bounded. Clients cannot talk about "whatever comes to mind" or whatever crisis occurred during the week. Sharing is structured around skills practice attempts, rationales, and difficulties applying skills. Unlimited crisis discussion is not part of skills training format.

**This causes real friction.** Many clients have been taught (often by excellent previous therapists) that certain "necessary ingredients" for change include unlimited talk time, emotional catharsis, complete sharing, and the therapist's attention to whatever the client brings.
