## Allowing the Mind: Mindfulness of Current Thoughts

Mindfulness of current thoughts is the skill of observing thoughts as they come and go—as sensations of the brain rather than as facts about the world. This creates distance between you and your thoughts, reducing their power to create suffering.

### What This Skill Is (and Isn't)

**What it is:**
- Noticing thoughts and radically accepting them as mental events
- Observing thoughts without trying to control, change, or get rid of them
- Changing your relationship to thoughts rather than changing the thoughts themselves
- A form of mindfulness that applies to beliefs, assumptions, interpretations, and internal labels

**What it isn't:**
- Thought suppression or pushing thoughts away
- Trying to have an empty mind (the goal is never a blank mind)
- Cognitive therapy (which analyzes and changes thought content)
- Positive thinking or thought replacement

### Why Observe Thoughts Rather Than Fight Them?

**Research reality:** Trying to block or suppress thoughts actually makes them worse. Paradoxically, setting aside specific time to focus completely on worry thoughts is one effective treatment for excessive worries. The treatment for obsessional thoughts involves consciously focusing on them over and over.

**In clinical practice:** Much unnecessary suffering comes from reacting to thoughts instead of to the facts they're meant to represent. Many people respond to their thoughts as if they are facts about themselves, others, or the world. The problem is that people have difficulty accepting that a thought is just that—a thought.

### Core Principles

**1. Observing thoughts provides distance.** When you step back and observe your thoughts, you separate yourself from them. This makes it easier to figure out what is a thought, what is a fact, and what is your emotional reaction to the thought. Most importantly, distance allows you to discover that **you are not your thoughts**. You are not defined by your thoughts or by others' thoughts about you.

**2. Thoughts are temporary, like waves.** Every thought you've ever had has come into your mind and left your mind. They come and go. A thought might return often, but it's still temporary. All sensations are temporary.

**3. Observing thoughts reveals they are less catastrophic than we fear.** Although events can be catastrophic, thoughts about events are not themselves catastrophic. The thought "I might fail" is not a failure. The thought "I am dying" is not death.

**4. Observing thoughts is the path to freedom.** Letting go of trying to control thoughts or get rid of them is the path to freedom. Many people believe they should have control over their thoughts at all times. This belief makes them controlled by their thoughts. True freedom requires the ability to allow the natural flow of thoughts to come and go—to experience them without being controlled by them.

### How to Be Mindful of Current Thoughts

**Step 1: Observe your thoughts.** Acknowledge their presence. Step back. Imagine you're on top of a mountain looking down on your thoughts below.

**Step 2: Adopt a curious mind.** Notice your thoughts coming and going. Ask: Where do they come from? Where do they go? Notice that every thought you've ever had has both arrived and departed. Don't evaluate or judge your thoughts. Let go of judgments about having the thoughts.

**Step 3: Remember you are not your thoughts.** You have had thousands and thousands of thoughts in your life. All are gone. You are not gone. You are not your thoughts.

**Step 4: Don't block or suppress.** Open yourself to the flow of thoughts. Don't try to get rid of the emotions that go along with thoughts. Don't push them away. Be willing to have your thoughts. Trying to suppress painful thoughts actually keeps them coming back.

### A Useful Analogy: Observing Without Acting

Asking "why" about a thought is usually not helpful. When mountain climbing and you encounter a crevasse, you need to know how to cross it, not why it's there. Similarly, when painful or intrusive thoughts arise, your task is to observe them and continue moving forward, not to understand their origins or suppress them.

### Building the Skill Through Practice

Initial practice often isn't effective. Remind clients that mindfulness provides freedom, and this takes practice. Throughout therapy, consistently refer back to mindfulness of current thoughts, especially when a client gets caught in rumination or becomes fused with a thought.

Early exercises might include:
- Noticing thoughts that arise when you hear certain words
- Distinguishing between thoughts that come unbidden versus thoughts you deliberately choose to think about
- Practicing with low-intensity thoughts before attempting high-intensity ones

The skill deepens over time with consistent practice. The goal is gradual freedom from thought control, not instant mastery.
