## Building Commitment and Therapeutic Alliance in DBT

A strong collaborative commitment and therapeutic alliance are essential foundations for DBT to work. These must be actively built, not assumed.

### Recognizing Reluctance and External Pressure

Many clients enter DBT under duress:
- Family members pressuring adolescents or young adults
- Court orders or probation requirements
- Individual therapists insisting on skills training as a condition of treatment
- Clients skeptical that any therapy will help given past failures

Internal ambivalence is also common. A client may intellectually understand DBT is needed but emotionally resist the structure, the group format, or the behavioral demands.

### The Therapist's Commitment First

Before seeking commitment from the client, the therapist must make an internal commitment to treating this person. Entering treatment reluctantly, with reserve, or antagonism severely impairs relationship quality and treatment outcomes. Your own DBT team consultation is crucial here for strengthening personal commitment when you feel depleted or doubtful about a particular client.

**Working with reluctance as data:** If you feel reluctant about treating a client, that reluctance is real clinical information. Discuss it with your team. It may signal that the match is poor, the client needs a different level of care, or you need support to engage authentically. Do not proceed without genuine commitment.

### Eliciting Motivation and Reframing Treatment

The therapist's task is to help potential clients see benefits of DBT, not just costs. This often requires creative reframing:

**"Life enhancement, not just symptom reduction":** DBT is not a suicide-prevention program, substance-abstinence program, or symptom-management program. It is a life-building program. The goal is not to prevent clients from dying, but to help them construct lives worth living—lives they actually want to live. This reframe shifts the focus from "staying alive" to "living well."

**Addressing specific doubts:** Many clients carry beliefs that therapy cannot help them:
- "Everyone has failed me before; why should DBT be different?"
- "These skills seem too simple/too hard/too childish to work."
- "I've tried therapy before and nothing changed."

**Honest messaging about effectiveness:** The most credible approach is to tell clients what is actually true in your experience: "In my experience, these skills have been helpful to *some* people *some* of the time. I can't promise they'll work for you, but many people have found them useful. Some people do very well. Others benefit from just one or two skills. No one benefits from everything, and that's okay."

If you are new to DBT, reference outcome data from established programs or acknowledge the limits of your experience while expressing openness to learning with them.

### Obtaining Genuine Commitment

**Distinction between pressured and voluntary:** Participation can be both pressured *and* voluntary simultaneously. A client may be in treatment because a parent demanded it *and* willing to try. Your job is to shift the balance toward genuine willingness.

**Never skip or assume commitment work:** Many errors occur when therapists assume commitment has been obtained by an intake interviewer, individual therapist, or earlier meeting. The skills trainer must independently obtain commitment. A common early mistake in DBT programs is therapists assuming responsibility was transferred when it was not. Pay the price of doing this work yourself.

**Commit to reviewing DBT assumptions and guidelines:** During pretreatment, explicitly walk through the DBT assumptions and treatment guidelines (attendance expectations, confidentiality rules, skills practice between sessions, etc.). Seek agreement. This is not an administrative task; it is a core therapeutic conversation where you learn about the client's reservations and address them directly.

### Relationship Acceptance and Enhancement

Two complementary strategies build alliance in group and individual settings:

**Relationship acceptance:** The therapist experiences and communicates genuine acceptance of the client as they are right now—with their progress, defenses, resistance, and pain. Acceptance means:
- Not demanding change before the client is ready
- Seeing all problems as part of the therapeutic process ("grist for the mill")
- Understanding that emotionally dysregulated clients *will* create challenges—that's why they need skills training
- Responding to problems nonjudgmentally rather than with criticism or rejection

Without acceptance, therapists unconsciously communicate rejection, which is iatrogenic.

**Relationship enhancement:** Behaviors by the therapist that increase the therapeutic value of the relationship:
- Professionalism, neatness, preparation (prepared room, distributed handouts, organized materials)
- Warmth, genuine interest, self-confidence
- Demonstrating expertise: leaders know what they're doing and have something to offer
- Appropriate self-disclosure: sharing your own experience with skills (if authentic)
- Trustworthiness: protecting absent group members from negative judgment; handling confidences carefully
- Advocacy: protecting the client from harsh judgment by other group members; being their ally

**Building credibility about effectiveness:** Communicate your genuine experience with the skills. If you have found DBT helpful in your own life, say so. Share success stories (without identifying details). Be honest about limitations: "This is a shotgun approach. Some skills work for some people some of the time."

Credibility is destroyed by overpromising ("This skill will solve your problem") or by presenting false certainty. 

### Special Considerations for Group Skills Training

In group settings, alliance-building is more complex because there are multiple relationships:
- Between leaders and members
- Among group members themselves
- Between the leaders as a team

**Early calls and outreach:** Leaders should call new group members a few days before the first session to remind them, clarify logistics, and convey genuine interest in meeting them. This simple act reduces pre-session anxiety and addresses unspoken plans to drop out before starting.

**Greeting and mingling:** Leaders arrive early to greet members, interact briefly, and hear concerns. For timid or fearful clients, this informal contact can be soothing and reduce dropout risk.

**Initial sharing ritual:** Begin by going around the group asking each person their name, how they heard about the group, and what they'd like to share. Leaders also share about themselves. This demystifies the leaders and reduces initial fear.

**Addressing group cohesion:** Before new members join, discuss with existing members the importance of welcoming them. New members joining mid-cycle should arrive early for a brief orientation and receive a skills review. This creates inclusion rather than a sense of being added to an established clique.
