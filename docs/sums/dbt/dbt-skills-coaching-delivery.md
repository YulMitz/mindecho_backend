## Between-Session Skills Coaching in DBT

Skills coaching is practice support delivered outside formal session time to help clients apply DBT skills in their natural environments. Coaching dramatically increases skill generalization and is one of the strongest mechanisms for skill transfer.

### Delivery Models

**Standard DBT (with individual therapy):** Phone calls for skills coaching are severely limited. Most coaching requests go to the individual therapist rather than the skills trainer, unless that role is explicitly designated. This prevents dual-relationship confusion and maintains clear therapeutic boundaries.

**Alternative systems:** If an individual therapist does not take calls or provide coaching, skills trainers may accept coaching calls, especially when the call's sole purpose is skill instruction and practice troubleshooting.

**Inpatient/residential settings:** Coaching is embedded into the milieu. Staff members should learn the same skills clients learn, then serve as day-to-day coaches. Effective structures include:
- Weekly skills consultation meetings run like office hours—clients drop in for coaching
- Client-to-client peer coaching (clients call one another for help)
- Regular homework review groups led by nursing or milieu staff
- Individual therapists who reinforce and coach skill use within sessions

**Advanced group coaching:** In residential settings, advanced skills groups allow members to practice applying skills to their current daily situations, with peers providing feedback.

### Extending Coaching to the Client's Environment

Skills generalization improves dramatically when people in the client's life learn the skills and coach daily:

- **Family members:** Parents, partners, and other relatives can be taught the DBT skills and coached in how to support the client's practice. Family coaching is standard in adolescent DBT.
- **Parole/probation officers:** Can be trained in DBT skills to coach parolees and probationers.
- **Primary care providers:** Physicians and nurse practitioners can learn skills to coach patients during routine visits.
- **School settings:** Teachers and school counselors receive DBT skills training to coach students.

### Key Coaching Principles

- Coaching is directive and behavioral, focused on what the client practiced and why it worked or did not work
- Timing matters: coaching is most effective immediately after or shortly after the client attempts a skill
- Coaching requires consistency across providers to avoid confusion or conflicting instruction
- Each coach should understand the DBT rationale for the skill, not just recite steps
