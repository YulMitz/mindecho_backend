## The STOP Skill: Creating a Pause Between Emotion and Action

STOP is the most fundamental distress tolerance skill because it creates the psychological space required for all other skills to work. Without the ability to interrupt the impulse-to-action sequence, a client trapped in emotion mind will continue destructive patterns regardless of what else they learn.

### The Four Steps: S-T-O-P

**1. STOP (Freeze)**

At the moment of impulse, the instruction is literally to stop moving—physically freeze. Don't react. Don't make a gesture. Just pause.

The mechanism: Emotions organize the body for action. Anger prepares for attack, shame prepares for hiding, fear prepares for flight. These action tendencies are *part* of the emotion, not secondary to it. By interrupting the motor output (freezing the body), the client disrupts the entire emotion-action cycle, buying milliseconds to gain control.

In session, you might say: "The first thing emotions do is tell your body what to do. Freezing interrupts that message. You're the boss of your body, not your emotion."

**2. Take a Step Back (Create Psychological Distance)**

Both literally and mentally, the client steps back. This might be physical (literally moving away from the provocateur) or internal (taking a deep breath, mentally distancing from the hot thoughts).

The mechanism: Extreme arousal narrows attention and makes situation assessment impossible. Taking a step back—whether physical or mental—creates distance from the heat of the moment. This is where paced breathing becomes crucial (see TIP skills). Deep breathing literally activates the parasympathetic nervous system, which is the body's brake pedal.

The client's internal dialogue shifts from "He just disrespected me and I need to respond NOW" to "Something happened. Let me see what it actually is."

**3. Observe (Gather Information)**

Now, with arousal beginning to drop, the client observes:
- What happened?
- Who is involved?
- What is each person doing or saying?
- What am I assuming vs. what do I actually know?

This is where the client practices non-judgmental observation (a mindfulness skill). They notice without immediately interpreting. They gather facts.

**Clinical note:** Many clients skip this step or rush through it. They say, "I already know what happened." Insist on observation. In the example from the manual—the drunk partner yelling accusations—the observation reveals that the partner is intoxicated, which entirely changes the wise-mind response. The observation step prevents the client from acting on their initial interpretation.

**4. Proceed Mindfully (Choose Action Aligned with Goals)**

Only now does the client ask: "What do I want from this situation? What are my goals? What choice will make this better or worse?"

They consult their wise mind (integrating emotion mind and reasonable mind) and then act—or choose not to act—in alignment with their values and long-term goals.

In the drunk partner example, the wise-mind response is to explain about the flat tire, calm the partner, and postpone discussion until morning. Not because the emotion says "Oh, he's drunk, so forgive him," but because the goal is a functional relationship and immediate arguing serves no purpose.

### How to Teach STOP

Start with a concrete, relatable scenario: being insulted or pushed, having a plan disrupted, being accused of something you didn't do. Have the client role-play.

First, demonstrate the STOP sequence yourself—freeze visibly, take a step back, narrate your observation, then state your action choice.

Then have the client practice with escalating difficulty. Start with the simple scenario. Then have someone in the room escalate (insult them again, push further, accuse them of worse things) while the client practices staying frozen and continuing to breathe.

### Common Obstacles

**"I can't just freeze—I'll look weird."**  
Reframe: "Freezing inside counts. You can hold still for 2 seconds while looking normal. That's enough to break the automatic reaction."

**"By the time I remember STOP, I've already said/done it."**  
This is normal early on. Have them practice STOP multiple times a day when NOT in crisis, so the skill becomes automatic. Teach them to recognize their personal early warning sign (jaw tightening, heat rising, voice getting louder) and commit to using STOP at that moment.

**"I do the steps but I still want to react."**  
That's fine. The skill isn't "don't want to anymore." It's "I notice the urge and I choose not to act on it." Wanting to yell at someone while still choosing not to yell is wise mind in action.

### Integration with Other Skills

STOP is the foundation that allows access to:
- **Distraction** (ACCEPTS): After STOP, the client can distract themselves
- **Problem-solving**: After STOP, they can think clearly about solutions
- **Interpersonal effectiveness**: After STOP, they can communicate their needs without aggression
- **TIP skills**: If STOP alone doesn't bring arousal down enough, use TIP afterward

In session, if a client gets activated, you can say: "Let's STOP right now. Freeze. Take a breath. What do you observe about what just happened? What's your goal in this moment?" This teaches the skill in vivo.
