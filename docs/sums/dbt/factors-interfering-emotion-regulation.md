## Why Emotion Regulation Is So Hard: Six Key Obstacles

Validating that emotion regulation is genuinely difficult is foundational to DBT. Clients often blame themselves for struggling. Understanding the structural barriers helps shift blame from self to system, and opens pathways for intervention.

### A. Biological Factors

Some people are born with higher emotional sensitivity and intensity. This is not a choice or a character flaw—it's a neurobiological difference. Babies with high emotional sensitivity often remain that way into adulthood. This means:

- They are more likely to notice emotional cues
- They react more intensely when triggered
- They take longer to return to baseline after emotion fires
- Learning emotion regulation strategies is *harder for them* even when they work hard

**Clinical note:** Ask clients directly: "Were you always more emotional than your siblings? Did emotion regulation difficulties show up early in childhood, or more recently?" This grounds the conversation in observable history and reduces shame.

### B. Lack of Skill

This is different from lack of motivation. People learn emotion regulation starting in infancy through interactions with caregivers—the back-and-forth emotional exchange that builds regulatory capacity. When parents have:
- Limited emotion regulation skills themselves
- Limited time or resources to teach
- Limited desire to prioritize this
- Children with high biological sensitivity (who are harder to teach)

...the child doesn't develop the skills. This is a *learning gap*, not a willpower gap.

Skills are also context-dependent: a client might regulate emotions perfectly fine at work (where stakes feel lower) but completely fall apart at home (where attachment is high and old patterns trigger). The skill isn't absent; it's not accessible in all contexts or emotional states.

**Analogy:** Learning emotion regulation is like learning golf. Everyone can improve somewhat, but for some people, becoming truly skilled is much harder, even with practice.

### C. Reinforcement of Emotional Behaviors

**This is crucial.** When emotional expressions *work* to get needs met, they become entrenched and very hard to change.

**Examples:**
- If anger consistently gets you what you want, you will keep getting angry
- If crying and depression are the only times others listen and help, sadness becomes adaptive
- If avoidance keeps you safe (or seems to), fear becomes rational

The emotion is being reinforced even if the client consciously doesn't want it. The functions emotions serve include:

- **Communication:** "I matter; listen to me"
- **Motivation:** Anger drives you to fight for what you want
- **Validation:** "My sadness proves how much this situation hurt me"
- **Emotion regulation itself:** Sometimes a painful emotion feels better than numbness or shame

When emotions do "good work" (even destructive work), they're very hard to surrender. Clients may not even realize they can't change the emotion because it's serving too many important functions.

**In session:** Assess what the emotion is doing. What would the client lose if they regulated this emotion? Sometimes the emotional behavior is solving a real problem (getting attention, asserting boundaries). Before asking them to change it, you may need to solve the underlying problem differently.

### D. Moodiness (Low Energy/Willingness)

Emotion regulation requires effort, energy, and willingness. It's not automatic—it's work. When someone is already depleted (depressed, exhausted, physically ill), the *motivation and capacity* to practice skills drops sharply, even if they have the skills.

This is not laziness. It's that mood and energy state directly interfere with the willingness to engage in effortful regulation.

**In session:** When skills aren't being used, ask: "How's your energy been? Are you getting enough sleep? Eating regularly?" Low energy is a signal to address basic self-care *first*, not to increase skill demands.

### E. Emotional Overload (Rumination and Escalation)

When emotions reach extreme intensity, the client's brain literally cannot access wise mind. Instead, worry thoughts and ruminations fire automatically:

- "Why did I do that?"
- "What will happen to me?"
- "This is terrible."

These thoughts feel like they're trying to solve a problem, but there often *is no solution*. So the thoughts loop endlessly, keeping the emotion firing like a computer program on repeat.

**Rumination as emotion escape:** Paradoxically, rumination can function as a way to escape the *feeling* of the emotion by staying in the thinking mind. But this keeps the emotion active because the body stays aroused and the thought loop feeds the original emotion.

**Clinical example:** A client cries and becomes increasingly distressed, ruminating about everything that's wrong. The rumination was meant to help but instead created an uncontrollable vortex. The reparative function of tears got blocked by the escalating thoughts.

When emotional arousal is extreme and rumination is active, distraction or other crisis skills may be more effective than trying to use a complex emotion regulation technique.

### F. Myths About Emotions

Faulty beliefs about emotions create obstacles. Two types of myths:

**Invalidating myths** (undervalue emotions):
- "Negative emotions are selfish"
- "I should be able to control all my emotions if I just use willpower"
- "Strong emotions mean I'm weak"

These invalidate the client's experience because they don't match their lived reality.

**Overvaluing myths** (overvalue emotions):
- "If I feel it, I shouldn't try to change it"
- "Emotions are always valid sources of truth"
- "Expressing every emotion is healthy"

Both sets of myths block emotion regulation. Clients get stuck: Either they believe they *must* change what they can't (guilt, shame spiral), or they believe they *shouldn't* change what they can (refusing to challenge distorted thoughts).

**Myth example:** "Once an emotion starts, it never goes away." 

**Reframe:** "An emotion is like being on a surfboard. You ride the wave up and down until it finally passes."

**In session:** Use the "devil's advocate" technique. You argue passionately *for* the myth while clients generate counterarguments. This gives them practice challenging dysfunctional beliefs in a low-stakes way, and the arguments they generate become their own "cheerleading statements" for later use.
