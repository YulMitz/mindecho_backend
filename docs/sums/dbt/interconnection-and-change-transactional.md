## Transactional Change & Reciprocal Influence

One of dialectics' most liberating insights for clients caught in blame cycles is that change is transactional. Nothing happens in isolation. Every action by one person alters the environment, which then alters the other person's response, which alters the first person—and the cycle continues.

### The Core Principle

The world is one large system with interacting parts. The sun, trees, water, farmer, grocer, teacher, friend, parent, sibling—all interconnected and influencing one another. A affects B, which alters B, which in turn alters A. Reciprocity is the key.

**This leads to understanding, not to blame.**

### Emotional Escalation as a Transactional Example

A classic case illustrates this perfectly. A 6-year-old (Catalina) at a concert thinks she sees fire behind the stage and whispers to her mom. Mom doesn't see it and whispers back, "There is no fire." Catalina looks again, sees it again, whispers louder. Mom whispers even louder, "No, there is not!" This escalates to Catalina shouting, "Mom! Fire!" and Mom shouting back, "BE QUIET!" Eventually Mom carries her out.

The transactional loop:
- Catalina's escalated emotional expression (shouting) was reinforced by Mom finally removing her from the situation.
- Mom's escalated response (shouting, then removing her) was reinforced by Catalina calming down once they left.

Neither "caused" the escalation; both shaped it. Catalina didn't escalate to manipulate (though that's an easy pejorative inference). She escalated because her attempts to communicate weren't being heard. Mom didn't escalate to be cruel; she escalated in response to feeling unheard.

### Clinical Application: Emotion Dysregulation

When emotions are not validated or their communicative function is blocked by the environment, they often escalate. The environment then tries harder to block them, creating a vicious cycle.

Rather than saying, "You're being too emotional" or "Why do you always escalate?" a dialectical stance says: "I see how this pattern developed. Your emotions are trying to communicate something important. When they're dismissed, they naturally get louder. That makes sense."

This framework:
- Reduces the client's shame ("I'm not trying to manipulate")
- Clarifies the system ("We've been caught in a loop together")
- Opens space for change ("We can interrupt this cycle from either side")

### Key Point: All Behavior Is Caused

Each individual influences their environment while being influenced by it. A teacher with a new student with a learning disability does extra work. The student progresses and feels more secure. The teacher evolves too and is now better prepared for future students. Both grew from the transactional experience.

This removes the fiction of pure individual agency ("If you just tried harder, you'd be different") while maintaining accountability ("Your behavior matters in this system").
