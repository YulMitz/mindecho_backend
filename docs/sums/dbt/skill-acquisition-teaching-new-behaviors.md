## Skill Acquisition: Teaching New Behavioral Patterns

Skill acquisition is how trainers teach clients new responses they do not yet have in their behavioral repertoire. It differs from skill *strengthening* (practice and feedback) and skill *generalization* (homework and in-vivo use).

### Orienting and Commitment: Selling the Skill

Before teaching any skill, the trainer must **orient** the client to why this skill matters and what the learning process will entail. Orienting is "selling" the skill as worth learning and likely to help.

**Why orientation matters:**
- Clients with emotion dysregulation often feel hopeless that anything will work
- They may have skill deficits *and* fear learning new behaviors
- They may worry that acquiring a skill means they *must* use it

**What to address in orientation:**

1. **Clarify that learning ≠ using**: "You can learn this skill and then choose whether to use it in each situation. Learning it doesn't commit you to using it."

2. **Normalize that skills work for some people, not others**: Point out that every skill taught has helped either you or people you know. "However, I can't guarantee it will help you specifically. No skill works for everyone."

3. **Explain the skill's purpose and rationale**: Draw the client into a Socratic discussion: "What do you think happens when you…?" or give an overall rationale. At times a comment suffices; at other times, extensive discussion is needed.

4. **Explain why *you* teach the way you do**: This is the time to frame the DBT teaching model. The most critical message: **"Learning new skills requires practice, practice, practice. Practice has to occur in situations where the skills are needed. If these points don't get through, there's not much hope you'll actually learn."**

5. **Secure recommitment**: Once oriented, ask the client to recommend to learning each skill and the skill module, and to commit weekly to practicing between sessions.

### Assessing What the Client Already Knows

DBT does not assume all problems are motivational. Before teaching, assess whether the client **lacks the skill** or **has the skill but is emotionally inhibited or constrained by environment**.

This distinction is complex with emotion-dysregulated clients because they:
- Confuse "I'm afraid to do it" with "I can't do it"
- Face powerful contingencies against admitting capability (fear, shame, family beliefs)
- May genuinely not know what they feel or think
- May have been taught to see all problems as either entirely motivational ("I can do anything; I just don't want to") or entirely ability-based ("I'm incapable")

**How to assess:**
- **For interpersonal skills**: Use role play during session or ask what the client would say in a situation. Alternatively, have one client coach another in a role play.
- **For distress tolerance**: Ask what techniques the client uses or thinks helpful in tolerating stress.
- **For emotion regulation**: Interrupt an exchange and ask if the client can shift their emotional state.
- **For self-management and mindfulness**: Observe the client in session when not the focus, and ask about day-to-day behavior.

**Key clinical point**: If the client produces the behavior, it's in their repertoire. If they don't, you can't be sure (the null hypothesis problem in statistics). When in doubt, proceed with skill acquisition anyway—it usually does no harm and may reduce inhibitions rather than add new skills.

### Instructions: Direct Teaching of Skill Components

"Instructions" are **verbal descriptions of skill components**. They can vary from general guidelines ("When checking whether thoughts fit the facts, verify the probability that dire consequences will occur") to very specific directives ("The minute an urge hits, get a cold gel pack and hold it for 10 minutes").

**Formats for instructions:**
- Lecture with whiteboard aids (especially in groups)
- Socratic dialogue (drawing the skill out of the client's own thinking)
- Hypothesis-testing approach ("What if you tried…?")
- Thesis-antithesis-synthesis framework (presenting opposing views to balance)

**Critical teaching points:**

1. **Avoid oversimplifying**: Do not make the skill sound easier than it is. Clients with emotion dysregulation will dismiss it if it seems unrealistically simple.

2. **Link skill to outcome repeatedly**: Not only describe *how* to do the skill, but *when* it works, *why* it works, *what* it works for, *when* it doesn't work, and *how to make it work* when it seems ineffective.

3. **Address the immediate relief expectation**: This is a major teaching challenge. Most clients believe a skill "didn't work" if it didn't make them feel better *immediately*. You must repeatedly discuss **long-term goals vs. short-term relief**. Many DBT skills do not produce immediate emotional relief—and that's okay. Some skills (like distress tolerance) are about surviving the moment without making things worse, not feeling better right away.

4. **Do not be pulled into promising immediate relief**: "Even if it were true that this skill would make you feel better right away, that wouldn't necessarily be beneficial."

### Using the Handouts

The skills handouts (available on the manual website) are **not scripts to read verbatim**. Instead, they function as:
- Cues for your teaching
- Written reminders for clients about what to practice
- Space for clients to take notes and add their own ideas
- Reference material clients can carry with them

**Practical note**: Clients often carry their skills binders with them and use them during real-life situations. One client famously asked his wife during an argument to wait while he checked his skills book. When you revise handouts, clients typically want to keep the old ones because they've written notes on them.
