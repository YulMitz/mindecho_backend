## Nonjudgmentalness: Releasing Evaluations While Keeping Discernment

Nonjudgmentalness is one of the three "how" skills in DBT mindfulness. It is not about becoming passive, naive, or unable to see consequences. Rather, it involves consciously separating two kinds of judgment: *discriminations* (accurate, factual discernments) and *evaluations* (good/bad labels we add in our minds).

### What Clients Need to Know: The Two Types of Judgment

**Discriminating judgments** are necessary and useful. A jeweler discriminates whether a stone is a real diamond. A teacher discriminates whether work meets a standard. A swimmer discriminates a full pool from an empty one before diving. These judgments describe factual differences, compare against standards, and predict consequences. They save lives and help us function.

**Evaluative judgments** are something we *add* to facts. They are not observed; they are opinions and values projected onto reality. When we say "This is bad" or "This is good," we are inserting our personal evaluation—not stating a fact. The same behavior, person, or event can be judged "good" by one person and "bad" by another, depending on their values and preferences. A piece of rotten fish is factually dangerous (full of bacteria), but calling it "bad" is shorthand for that danger. When we forget what danger we mean and simply call things "bad," we confuse our judgment with reality itself.

### Why This Matters in Therapy

When clients layer evaluations onto reality, emotions intensify and relationships suffer. A client thinks, "I'm a bad person" (evaluation) rather than "I missed my appointment and that will create consequences" (discrimination + feeling). Loved ones feel judged and become defensive or withdrawn. Self-directed judgments ("I'm stupid," "I'm worthless") fuel shame and self-harm. Judgments also block problem-solving: yelling "This shouldn't have happened" doesn't change anything. Understanding the causes of behavior and changing those causes does.

### How to Teach and Practice Nonjudgmentalness

**The core move:** Replace "good" and "bad" with factual descriptions and honest feelings.

- Instead of "That's bad," say: "This fish is old and smells slimy. If I eat it, I may get sick. I don't want to serve it."
- Instead of "I'm a bad person," say: "I missed my appointment for the third time. My friends will lose trust in me if I keep doing this."
- Instead of "My therapist is good," say: "I feel understood when my therapist listens carefully and remembers details I shared."

**Let go of "should":** The word "should" is almost always judgmental. It means "reality is not what I demand it to be." Replacing "should" with descriptive language or causal language works better:
- Instead of "People should love others more," say: "I want people to be more loving" or "When people are more loving, there is less conflict."
- Instead of "I should have known better," say: "I didn't have that information at the time" or "Next time, I'll check before I act."

**Key clarifications:**
- Nonjudgmentalness does **not** mean approval. You can describe facts (This behavior is destructive) without judging the person or event as inherently "bad."
- It does **not** mean denying consequences. Observe and name consequences factually: "This choice will damage our relationship" is not judgmental; it's accurate.
- It does **not** mean staying silent about preferences. You can ask for what you want: "I'd prefer if you did this differently" is not judgmental; "You *should* do this" is.
- It does **not** mean suppressing values and emotions. Values and preferences are real; they just aren't facts about the world.

### Common Client Resistance

Clients often fear that nonjudgmentalness means they must accept harmful behavior or lose their moral compass. Address this directly: "We can see that something causes harm without labeling it or the person as 'bad.' Judgment often closes off understanding. Describing facts and causes opens the door to change."

### Practice in Session

When a client uses judgmental language, pause and ask them to rephrase:
- **Client:** "My boss is terrible."
- **You:** "What specifically happened, and what effect did it have on you?"
- **Client (rephrase):** "My boss ignored my idea in the meeting. I felt dismissed, and I'm worried my contributions don't matter here."

Over time, this becomes automatic. Clients report feeling less emotionally reactive and more able to solve problems when they stop judging and start observing.
