## Half-Smiling and Willing Hands: Accepting Reality with Your Body

These two skills demonstrate a fundamental DBT principle: emotions are partially controlled by facial expressions and body posture. By adopting a half-smile and willing hands position, clients can shift their emotional state through their body.

### Why Half-Smile?

A half-smile is a serene, accepting facial expression adopted deliberately. Research shows that emotions are partly controlled by the face we make. If you relax your face and adopt an accepting expression, you actually feel more accepting. This is not about faking happiness—it's about signaling acceptance to your own brain.

Example in practice: Half-smiling when thinking about someone you dislike helps you feel more accepting of them, more understanding.

### How to Practice Half-Smiling

1. **Relax your facial muscles.** Start with your forehead, move down through your eyes, cheeks, and jaw.

2. **Turn your lips up slightly at the corners.** Just barely—just enough that you feel it. This is not a wide grin or a fake smile.

3. **Adopt a serene expression.** The goal is peace, not happiness.

4. **Remember: it's for you, not others.** The half-smile is mainly a communication to your own brain, not a performance for other people. Whether anyone else sees it doesn't matter.

### The Practice Exercise: Contrast Grinning, Impassive, and Half-Smiling

Guide clients through three states:

- **Phony grin** (tight facial muscles, forced smile): Notice how this feels—exhausting, conflicted
- **Impassive face** (tight, expressionless mask): Notice the flatness and how it can actually increase distress
- **Half-smile** (relaxed face with gentle upturn at lips): Notice the lightness and acceptance that emerges

When your face is tense and grinning, you send your brain conflicting messages: "This is awful" and "This is nice." When you mask emotions, it often backfires and increases distress. Half-smiling—with relaxed muscles—sends a clear message of acceptance.

### Why Willing Hands?

Willing hands is another way of accepting reality with your body. The physical position communicates acceptance: hands unclenched, palms up, fingers relaxed.

Willing hands is part of opposite action for anger. Clenched fists are indicative of anger and fighting. Anger says, "What is should not be." While anger has its place as a motivator for change, here we're practicing reality acceptance. Unclenched hands signal willingness.

### How to Practice Willing Hands

1. **Sit with hands resting on your thighs or lap, palms up, fingers relaxed.**

2. **Imagine a conflict where you felt genuinely angry at someone.** Let the anger come up.

3. **Notice what happens to your hands and body.** Usually people find their hands want to clench.

4. **Now place your hands in the willing-hands position and continue imagining the conflict.**

5. **Notice the shift.** Almost always, it is difficult to stay as angry and nonaccepting once hands move to willing-hands position.

Anger is almost always a reflection of a belief that current reality "should" be different than it is. As acceptance goes up, anger goes down, and understanding and sometimes peace increase.

### Practicing These Skills Over Time

Suggest clients practice:
- Upon waking in the morning
- During free moments throughout the day
- While listening to music
- When irritated
- In lying-down or sitting positions
- While contemplating a person they struggle with (but start with "easy" people first)

**Important caution:** When practicing with someone the client dislikes or has trauma history with, start with people of low emotional intensity. This is a shaping exercise—like learning to drive on surface streets before the expressway. Overwhelming emotional intensity can backfire.

These are deceptively simple skills with real neurological impact. Consistent practice increases their effectiveness.
