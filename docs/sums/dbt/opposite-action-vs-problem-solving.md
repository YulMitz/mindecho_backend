## Choosing Between Opposite Action and Problem-Solving

The distinction between **opposite action** (for unjustified emotions) and **problem-solving** (for justified emotions) is one of the most practically important decisions in emotion regulation. Clients often confuse the two, applying the wrong strategy to the wrong emotional situation.

### The Core Distinction

**Opposite action** is for emotions that **don't fit the facts** or that, even when justified, are not currently helpful. You change the emotion by acting opposite to it.

**Problem-solving** is for emotions that **are justified by the situation**—the situation is actually problematic. You change the emotion by changing the situation.

Matching the right strategy to the right situation is critical. Trying to opposite-action away an emotion that has a valid cause wastes effort and risks invalidating the client's legitimate response to their circumstances. Conversely, trying to problem-solve an emotion that is unjustified keeps the client stuck in rumination.

### Decision-Making Process in Session

Help the client **check the facts** first (using Emotion Regulation Handout 8 if needed):
- What is the prompting event?
- Does this event actually justify the emotion?
- Is the emotion a reasonable response to the facts?

If **YES**—the emotion is justified:
→ Move to problem-solving. Work through the four strategies: Act on the emotion, change the situation, avoid/leave, or reframe thoughts about it.

If **NO**—the emotion is unjustified or unhelpful:
→ Move to opposite action. Act in a way that contradicts the emotion's action urge.

### Common Client Confusion

Clients often resist this framework because:
- They may feel that opposite action means "I don't have the right to feel this way"
- They may feel problem-solving means "I have to keep working on an unsolvable situation"

Clarify: The goal is **effectiveness**, not judgment. If an emotion doesn't fit the facts and isn't helping, opposite action is the effective move. If an emotion does fit the facts, problem-solving honors that reality while working toward change.
