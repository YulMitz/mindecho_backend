## Building a Life Worth Living: Values and Long-Term Goals

Temporary pleasant events and mood regulation techniques alone are insufficient for lasting happiness. DBT assumes that **it is hard to be happy without experiencing your life as worth living**—a life that is satisfying and aligns with what you actually value.

This skill addresses the long-term architecture of a meaningful life, not just moment-to-moment mood.

### Core Principle: Values Are Not Goals

Many clients conflate values with goals, which creates confusion and failure. Help them understand the distinction:

**Values** are ways of living, ongoing priorities, directions for your life. They have no endpoint. You can never fully "achieve" a value; you practice it continuously. Example: "Being a caring person" is a value you live out through countless acts over a lifetime. You don't finish being caring.

**Goals** are specific, achievable outcomes. Once accomplished, you move to the next goal. Example: "Get a college degree" or "Make one friend" are goals that support the value of "being educated" or "having close relationships," but achieving the goal doesn't mean the value is complete.

Values are like **guideposts**—they tell you direction and what matters, but they're not a destination you reach and then abandon.

### Identifying Personal Values

Many people have never articulated their values clearly. Rather than defining "value" abstractly, give them a **concrete list of 58 values across 14 categories** (relationships, work, spirituality, health, creativity, etc.). Have them check off what matters.

Be prepared: **This can trigger distress.** Seeing a values list often makes people painfully aware of how far their current life is from their values. Head this off by asking first: "What values are already present in your life?" Acknowledge both what's there and what's missing.

**Good clarifying questions:**
- "What in life are your highest priorities? What really matters to you?"
- "What direction do you want your life to go?"
- "What do you have now that you don't want to lose?"
- "What valuable things are absent from your life right now?"

**Check whether values are truly their own.** Many people carry values they've internalized from family, culture, or a partner without questioning them. A piercing question: "If you could live by this value but couldn't tell anyone about it, would you still do it?" If not, it may not be an authentic personal value. Distinguish between values the client genuinely cares about and values they pursue to get approval.

### Building a Life Worth Living, Step by Step

**1. Avoid Avoidance**
The central barrier is that building a valued life requires sustained effort, and avoidance is the default when mood is low. Acknowledge the legitimate obstacles (not knowing what you want, emotional overload, grief about an unfair life), but name that work is needed now.

**2. Identify One Key Value**
Resist the urge to work on everything. Have the client choose **one highest-priority value** to focus on first. A too-large task feels overwhelming and leads to inaction.

**3. Identify Goals Related to That Value**
Generate a few specific, achievable goals that move the person closer to living that value. Goals should be concrete enough that completion is recognizable.

**4. Prioritize and Choose One Goal**
Order the goals by importance and realistic feasibility. Select the single most important and achievable goal to begin with.

**5. Break Into Small Action Steps**
Translate the goal into tiny, manageable steps. "Get a college degree" breaks into: research schools, fill out an application, submit it, register for a class, attend the first class. Each step should be doable.

**6. Commit to One Action Step Now**
Don't leave the session without the client committing to one concrete action they can take this week. Examples: "Turn on the computer and look up clubs online" (toward the value of belonging). "Call the doctor and make an appointment" (toward the value of health).

### Important Teaching Notes

**Short-term pleasure versus long-term happiness:** Clients often trade long-term valued living for short-term escape or comfort. This erodes the foundation of a life worth living. Help them see that building permanent positive events (meaningful relationships, achievement aligned with values, integrity) creates lasting contentment, not just temporary relief.

**Values change over time.** A value hierarchy at age 20 differs from age 40. Life events shift priorities. This is normal and healthy, not failure. Periodically revisit values, especially after major transitions.

**Patience.** Building a life worth living takes time, often months or years. Small consistent actions compound. Don't promise quick transformation; promise direction and meaning-making.

**Validation and work coexist.** The fact that a life is far from valued does not mean the person is broken or lazy. Validate that this awareness is painful. Then work.
