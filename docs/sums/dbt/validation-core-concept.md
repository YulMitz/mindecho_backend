## Validation: Core Concept & Why It Matters

Validation is one of the most powerful and misunderstood interpersonal skills in DBT. Many clients avoid it thinking it means "agreeing with something I don't agree with." In reality, validation is much simpler and more powerful: finding and communicating the kernel of truth in another person's perspective or experience.

### What Validation Actually Is

Validation means verifying the facts of a situation. If a friend says his arm hurts, validating that means acknowledging: yes, his arm does hurt. If someone feels intensely sad after losing a beloved pet, validating means acknowledging: given that you loved this pet, intense sadness is understandable and reasonable.

When you validate, you communicate: *I understand where you're coming from. Your experience, emotion, or behavior makes sense.*

### Critical Distinction: Validation ≠ Agreement

This is the core confusion. A client may think: "If I validate my mother's anger at me, I'm agreeing I did something wrong." Not necessarily.

**Example:** Your friend drove drunk after two beers and got a DUI. You can validate: "I understand how it made complete sense to you to think you'd be safe driving after waiting an hour." You're not agreeing drunk driving is safe. You're understanding his reasoning given his beliefs at the time.

Similarly, in politics: "I can see you and I come from very different political positions" validates the person without requiring you to adopt their views.

### Why Validation Is Transformative

**It improves interactions.** Validation shows you're listening, being nonjudgmental, and seeing the facts of the situation.

**It reduces obstacles to effectiveness.** It lowers defensiveness, anger, and the pressure to prove who's right. When people feel understood first, they're far more receptive to feedback or requests.

**It makes problem-solving, closeness, and support possible.** Invalidation creates distance and defensiveness. Validation creates openness.

**Invalidation hurts—profoundly.** Many clients carry deep wounds from chronic invalidation. A classic example: Bill accidentally knocks his computer off a table in an important meeting. His team leader says sharply, "Stop multitasking! We've got an important guest." (The truth: Bill was taking notes.) His partner later says, "Why do you keep doing this? You'll never get a promotion." What's missing from both responses? Any understanding of Bill, his experience, or his feelings.

What would validate? The leader simply ignoring it (implying it's insignificant). The partner saying, "Oh, that must have been so upsetting and frustrating, especially since you were doing everyone a favor by taking notes."
