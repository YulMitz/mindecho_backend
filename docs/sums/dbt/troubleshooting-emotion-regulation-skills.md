## Troubleshooting Emotion Regulation Skills: When Skills Aren't Working

When a client practices an emotion regulation skill and reports it "didn't work," the skill itself may not be the problem. There are six systematic places to look before concluding the skill is ineffective.

### 1. Check for Biological Vulnerability

Many emotion regulation skills simply don't work well when the body is out of balance. Ask clients:

**Temporary biological disruptions:**
- Are you physically ill (fever, infection, injury)?
- Women: Where are you in your menstrual cycle? (Hormonal shifts dramatically affect emotional regulation capacity)
- Have you eaten recently? (Hunger destabilizes emotion regulation)
- How much sleep did you get? (Sleep deprivation catastrophically impairs emotion regulation)
- How much exercise/movement? (Too little or too much destabilizes mood)
- Have you used alcohol, mood-altering drugs, or medications? (Many substances interfere with skill effectiveness)

**Chronic biological factors:**
- Some mental disorders (bipolar disorder, schizophrenia) create baseline biological imbalance that makes emotion regulation skills less effective. These may require medication support.

**The intervention:** If biological vulnerability is suspected, the first step is not more skills practice—it's getting the body back in balance. Help the client address sleep, food, exercise, illness, or medication before expecting skills to work.

### 2. Ask: Did I Use the Skill Correctly?

Many clients forget the precise steps or misapply a skill. Ask them to:
- Carefully read through the written instructions for the skill they tried
- Describe exactly how they used it (step by step)
- Review whether they followed the instructions as written

If they didn't follow instructions, coach them through the correct application. Often this solves the problem immediately.

### 3. Ask: Is My Environment Reinforcing the Emotion?

If everything else checks out and the skill still isn't working, suspect **hidden reinforcement**. Outside of conscious awareness, something in the client's environment or relationships may be rewarding the emotional intensity.

**Signs of hidden reinforcement:**
- "When I get really upset, my partner pays more attention to me."
- "When I'm anxious, people let me off the hook for responsibilities."
- "When I'm angry, people take me seriously."
- "My depression gets me out of work or school demands."

**The investigation:**
- Have clients complete Emotion Regulation Handout 3 (Checking the Facts About Emotions) and Worksheets 2 and 2b (which explore the function and payoff of emotions).
- Ask directly: "What do you get when you feel this intensely? Does anyone treat you differently? Do you get out of something you don't want to do?"

**The intervention:** If reinforcement is found, the solution is not more emotion regulation skills—it's changing what the environment rewards. Help the client find alternative (non-emotional) ways to get their needs met: clearer communication, boundary-setting, or behavioral changes that meet the same underlying need.

### 4. Ask: Am I Putting in Enough Time and Effort?

Some emotions are stubborn and require sustained effort to regulate. Clients sometimes expect instant results and give up too quickly.

**Questions to ask:**
- "How many times did you actually practice this skill?"
- "How long did you practice it?"
- "Are you expecting it to work right away, or are you giving it real time?"

**The intervention:**
- Assign Emotion Regulation Worksheet 1 (Pros and Cons) to explore ambivalence about effort
- Teach **radical acceptance and willingness** (Distress Tolerance Handouts 11 and 13): sometimes emotion regulation requires accepting that the work is hard and choosing to do it anyway
- Teach mindfulness of **participation** and **effectiveness** (Mindfulness Handouts 4c and 5c): doing what works, even if it's not easy

### 5. Ask: Am I Too Upset to Use Complicated Skills?

This is the **skills breakdown point** again. If emotional intensity is extreme, complicated skills (like problem-solving or cognitive restructuring) won't work. The solution is wrong.

**What to do:**
- If the problem is solvable *right now*, solve it immediately (begin problem-solving)
- If the problem cannot be solved now and the client is worrying about it:
  - Teach **mindfulness of current emotions** (focus on physical sensations, not catastrophic thoughts)
  - Remind them that worry is often an escape from painful sensations
  - Help them tolerate the sensations without trying to suppress or enlarge them
- If emotional intensity is too high to think straight:
  - **Use TIP skills or crisis survival skills** first (Emotion Regulation Handout 23, Distress Tolerance Handouts 6–9a)
  - Return to emotion regulation once arousal is lower
