## Interpersonal Effectiveness: Core Goals and Frameworks

The interpersonal effectiveness module teaches concrete skills for getting what you want from others *while maintaining the relationship and your self-respect*. This is the DBT answer to classic assertiveness training, with added emphasis on relationship quality and personal integrity.

### Three Core Goals

**1. Objectives Effectiveness (DEAR MAN)**
Getting what you want—making requests, saying no, getting others to take your perspective seriously.

**2. Relationship Effectiveness (GIVE)**
Keeping or strengthening the relationship during the interaction.

**3. Self-Respect Effectiveness (FAST)**
Maintaining your own integrity, values, and sense of self-worth through the interaction.

These three can sometimes be in tension. A client must **clarify which goal matters most** in any given situation, because the skills may differ slightly depending on priority.

### The Core Skill: Clarifying Priorities

Before teaching specific techniques, clients must learn to **identify what they actually want** in a situation. This sounds simple but is often the hardest part.

A client might say: "I need to talk to my boss about my workload." But do they want to:
- Get a reduced workload (objective)? 
- Keep the boss liking them (relationship)? 
- Show their boss they're competent and professional (self-respect)?

All three matter, but not equally in every situation. Sometimes a client must accept that they cannot have all three—they may need to choose.

**Teaching method**: Use worksheets or in-session dialogue to map the situation:
- What is my goal?
- How important is the relationship?
- How important is my self-respect?
- What am I willing to give up if I can't have all three?

### Why Clients Struggle

Even skilled people often fail at interpersonal effectiveness because:

**Lack of ability**: They don't know *how* to ask or say no effectively. Skills must be taught and practiced repeatedly until automatic.

**Emotional interference**: Strong emotion—anger, shame, fear of rejection—overrides known skills. A client knows intellectually how to ask calmly but their voice shakes, they yell, or they freeze.

**Indecision**: They don't know what they want, or they vacillate between extremes (asking for everything vs. nothing, all politeness vs. all aggression).

**Short-term over long-term**: Low distress tolerance makes them prioritize ending discomfort *now* over protecting the relationship *later*. They blow up, leave, or give in impulsively.
