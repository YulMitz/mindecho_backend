## Pros and Cons: Making Wise Decisions Under Pressure

Pros and cons is deceptively simple: write down the positive and negative consequences of two choices—acting on a crisis urge versus resisting it. But its power lies in a subtle psychological principle: **immediate reinforcement is far more powerful than delayed reinforcement**, and pros/cons work by strengthening the salience of delayed consequences.

### The Core Problem It Addresses

When a client is in emotion mind with a strong crisis urge (to drink, to self-harm, to quit their job), they experience an overwhelming pull toward the behavior. Why? Because the short-term reinforcement is immediate and tangible:
- Relief (within seconds)
- Escape from pain (within minutes)
- Mood boost (within an hour)

The long-term consequences are abstract, distant, and easy to discount:
- The hangover is tomorrow
- The scar is permanent but I can't feel it now
- Losing my job is later, not now

This creates a false "calculation" in emotion mind: *The behavior is worth it.*

Pros and cons works by making the long-term consequences *concrete and salient* before the crisis hits, so that when emotion mind takes over, the client has already done the thinking and can access their earlier reasoning.

### The Two Essential Grids

The key insight many therapists miss: **The pros of resisting an urge are NOT simply the opposite of the cons of giving in.**

Example:
- **Con of acting:** I'll lose my job (happens eventually)
- **Pro of resisting:** I won't lose my job (doesn't happen—but this doesn't capture what *does* happen)
- **Pro of resisting:** I stay employed, get my paycheck, keep my apartment, maintain my family relationships (these are active goods, not absences of bad)

Likewise:
- **Pro of acting:** Immediate relief from anxiety
- **Con of resisting:** I have to sit with anxiety (doesn't capture the active goods)
- **Con of resisting:** What good comes from tolerating? (Sometimes clients need help here)

**Create four separate lists:**
1. Pros of acting on the urge (short-term + long-term)
2. Cons of acting on the urge (short-term + long-term)
3. Pros of resisting the urge (short-term + long-term)
4. Cons of resisting the urge (short-term + long-term)

### Teaching the Time Dimension

Many clients only see immediate consequences. Explicitly train them to think in two timescales:

**Short-term (0-24 hours after the behavior):**
- Acting on drinking: "Feel better right now, sleep well, wake up tomorrow"
- NOT drinking: "Stay anxious, stay uncomfortable, lie awake"

**Long-term (days/weeks/months after):**
- Acting on drinking: "Hangover, regret, shame, damage to relationships, health impact"
- NOT drinking: "Anxiety eventually passes, I keep my job, relationships stay intact, I feel proud"

The client's brain is optimized to *feel* short-term consequences vividly. Your job is to make long-term consequences equally vivid by having them write them out.

### How to Assign and Use This Skill

**Preventive (better) approach:**
Have the client fill out pros/cons during a calm moment in session. Write it on the worksheet. Have them *keep a copy with them*. Then, when the urge hits and they're in emotion mind, they review their earlier reasoning instead of trying to think it through in the heat of the moment.

Say: "When you're in emotion mind and the urge is strong, your brain won't generate these reasons. It will only remember 'Drinking feels good.' That's why we do this now, when you can think clearly."

**In-crisis approach:**
If the urge hits and they didn't do it preventively, they can try to do pros/cons in the moment. But this is hard because emotion mind resists—at an implicit level, the person knows that thinking about long-term consequences will weaken the pull toward the behavior. They may say, "I don't have time" or "I don't care about the cons right now."

When they resist, name it: "Your brain is protecting the behavior by saying you don't have time. But you do. Write it down. Ask someone to help you."

### Working with Suicidal Crises

The manual notes that pros/cons for suicide can be powerful but requires specific care. Some clients will find the pros of dying and cons of living to be equal or weighted toward suicide. This is a clinical emergency requiring risk assessment and crisis protocols, not just pros/cons work.

However, *if* the client is exploring rather than decided, and *if* your therapeutic relationship is strong, working through pros/cons with genuine curiosity and validation (not trying to convince them life is worth living) can sometimes be the most powerful motivator. The act of you taking their reasoning seriously, and them writing out even the hard stuff, can shift something.

But only do this within a comprehensive safety plan and crisis framework. Pros/cons alone is insufficient for suicide risk.

### Common Obstacles and Solutions

**"The cons of acting are obvious—so why do this?"**
Because obvious in calm ≠ accessible in crisis. Emotion mind doesn't work on logic; it works on what's salient right now. Writing it out makes it sticky.

**"I know the long-term consequences and I don't care."**
This is the opposite problem: anhedonia, hopelessness, or genuine ambivalence about recovery. This signals a deeper issue (depression, lack of meaning, doubt about whether treatment works). Don't just do more pros/cons. Explore what's underneath.
