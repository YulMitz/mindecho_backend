## Shaping: Reinforcing Small Steps Toward Larger Goals

Shaping is the process of reinforcing successive approximations—small, achievable steps—toward a larger behavioral goal. Each step must be mastered and reinforced before moving to the next one. The key principle is that complex behaviors are difficult to learn all at once, so breaking them into smaller pieces makes change feel possible and maintains motivation through early success.

### Why Use Shaping

Shaping is especially useful for behaviors that are:
- **Complex** and require multiple steps (like finding a job, writing a report, exercising consistently)
- **New** and requiring significant skill development
- **Overwhelming** when viewed as a whole

Without shaping, clients often fail because they set the bar too high initially, experience no reinforcement for partial progress, and lose motivation.

### How Shaping Works in Practice

**Example 1: Job Search**
- Week 1: Reinforce client for applying to one job online
- Week 2: Reinforce for applying to two jobs
- Continue until job searching becomes a significant part of the client's daily routine

**Example 2: Exercise Routine**
- Step 1: Reinforce getting out of bed early
- Step 2: Reinforce exercising for 10 minutes
- Step 3: Reinforce exercising for 20 minutes
- Step 4: Reinforce exercising for 30 minutes

**Example 3: Large Project (e.g., Writing a Report)**
- Step 1: Sit down and outline the report one week before the deadline → reinforce with TV time
- Step 2: Write the introduction → small reinforcer
- Step 3: Write each section → reinforce progression

### Key Principles

**Timing of Reinforcement**
Reinforce *immediately* after the desired behavior occurs. If you wait, the reinforcer won't be connected to the behavior, and learning breaks down. The coach who tells you "Nice swing!" right after you execute correct form teaches much faster than feedback given after 30 swings.

**Progression is Individual**
The size of each step depends on the person and the behavior. Make steps small enough that success is likely, but not so small that they feel trivial. This is why observation and feedback from the client are crucial.

**Don't Skip Steps**
Moving too quickly to advanced steps often leads to failure. It's better to spend extra time solidifying early steps than to rush ahead and lose the client's confidence.
