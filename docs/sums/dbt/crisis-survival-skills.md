## Crisis Survival Skills: Buying Time When Emotions Take Over

Crisis survival skills are rapid, accessible techniques designed to interrupt the escalation of a crisis moment—not to solve the underlying problem, but to prevent secondary crises that compound the original distress. Think of them as emergency stabilization.

### The Core Problem They Address

When a client is in acute emotional crisis (extreme arousal, overwhelming shame, intense urge to engage in destructive behavior), two things happen simultaneously:
1. Their emotional arousal is so high that they cannot access other skills (problem-solving, emotion regulation, interpersonal effectiveness)
2. The urgency of the moment creates a powerful pull toward impulsive action that will make things worse

Crisis survival skills interrupt this trap by reducing arousal rapidly, creating a psychological space where the client can think again.

### What Counts as a Crisis That Needs These Skills

A true crisis in the DBT sense involves:
- **Extreme emotional arousal** making rational thought impossible
- **An immediate threat** of destructive behavior (self-harm, substance use, aggression, suicide attempt)
- **A situation that cannot be solved right now** but requires the person to tolerate intense distress until they can address it

**Not a crisis:** feeling bad about something. Not even: having a major problem. A crisis is when the person's emotional state threatens to create *new* harm on top of the original problem.

### How to Introduce This to Clients

Frame crisis survival skills as **time-buyers**. Say something like: "When emotion mind takes over completely, you can't use problem-solving or any other skill that requires thinking. What you need first is something that brings your arousal down fast so you can get back to wise mind. That's what crisis survival skills do—they buy you time."

Key validation: "You don't have to feel better. You just need to survive the next few hours without making things worse. That's success."

### The Two-Step Structure of Crisis Survival

1. **Use a rapid de-arousal skill** (TIP skills are fastest)
2. **Then engage in a holding activity** (distraction or self-soothing) to fill the time until the crisis passes or can be worked on

If you use only the first step and then immediately return to ruminating about the crisis, arousal will spike again. Clients need something to *do* after calming down.

### When Crisis Survival Skills Fail or Are Overused

**Too much reliance on crisis survival:** Client uses these skills every time they're uncomfortable, avoiding ever sitting with distress. This prevents emotional tolerance from developing. After arousal drops, *redirect to problem-solving or emotion work*.

**Skills not working:** Usually means:
- The skill was too mild for the level of arousal (e.g., distraction won't work when someone is in fight-or-flight; they need TIP skills first)
- No follow-up activity, so arousal re-escalates
- Client is in suicidal crisis or immediate safety risk—escalate to safety planning and crisis protocols

### Clinical Pearls

- **Sequence matters:** Always reduce arousal first (TIP skills), then distract or soothe to fill time. Never lead with distraction when someone is acutely dysregulated.
- **Normalize the temporary nature:** "The effect won't last forever. That's why we pair it with something else to do."
- **Teach pros/cons of tolerance beforehand:** Have clients write out and rehearse why *not* acting on crisis urges has better long-term outcomes. In crisis, emotion mind won't generate these reasons on its own.
- **Anticipate resistance:** When arousal is high, the pull toward destructive behavior is neurobiologically powerful. Clients will resist using these skills because (at an implicit level) they know that thinking of pros/cons or sitting with discomfort will get in the way of the immediately reinforcing behavior. Normalize this. Keep worksheets visible and accessible.
