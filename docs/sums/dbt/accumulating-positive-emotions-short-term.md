## Accumulating Positive Emotions: Short-Term (Building a Buffer)

One of the most evidence-based and straightforward emotion regulation strategies is **increasing pleasant events**. This is not about happiness through denial or forced positivity; it's about the neurobiological and behavioral reality that positive experiences directly reduce sadness and other negative emotions.

### Why Positive Events Matter

Positive events do more than feel good—they actively **reduce vulnerability to painful emotions**. They function as a buffer: when many positive experiences are absent from a life, the person becomes exquisitely sensitive to triggering events. Conversely, accumulated positive experiences build resilience.

This is why depressed clients often say "what's the point?" about small positive actions. The point is that small positive events are **reinforcing**—they make the person want to activate themselves further. One pleasant event is unlikely to transform mood dramatically, but practiced repeatedly, small positives accumulate into noticeable shifts.

### Core Principle: Absence Matters as Much as Presence

Many clients understand that too many negative events create distress. What they miss is that the **absence of positive experiences** is itself a cause of depression and low mood—it's not just that bad things are happening, it's that good things are *not* happening. This is especially true during deprivation (all work and no play, strict dieting, prolonged periods without joy).

### Building Short-Term Positive Experiences

**Do at least one pleasant thing each day** that prompts positive emotions—enjoyment, pleasure, serenity, joy, pride, self-confidence, love, or calmness. This is the behavioral foundation.

**Plan ahead.** When mood is low, motivation won't show up. The client must plan pleasant events in advance—ideally with another person to help mobilize them even when mood says "don't bother."

**Use opposite action if necessary.** Often the client must *act opposite to depression* (inactivity, anhedonia) to engage in the pleasant event at all. This is expected and normal.

**Avoid deserving/not-deserving thinking.** Many clients believe they have to "earn" pleasure or don't "deserve" it. This is judgmental thinking that blocks activation. If the client is someone who thinks this way, they'll need to practice opposite action by engaging in pleasant events *even while feeling they don't deserve them*—possibly combined with mindfulness non-judgment practice.

**Don't avoid avoiding.** A subtle but common mistake: clients in low mood avoid the very steps that lead to pleasant events (making plans, going to the event, initiating contact). They justify this with "why bother?" Use a pros-and-cons analysis to motivate doing opposite action here.

### Be Mindful of Positive Experiences

A pleasant event that the client is not present for will have minimal emotional impact. This is where **mindfulness becomes essential**:

- **Focus attention** on the positive experience as it happens. If the mind is elsewhere, the benefit is lost.
- **Refocus when the mind wanders** to worries or complaints. This is opposite action: redirecting attention to what is present and good, not what is feared.
- **Participate and engage fully** rather than watching from the sidelines. Boredom often comes from passive observation; the antidote is full participation in the flow of the experience.
- **Block worry** about the experience ending, whether they deserve it, or what it means. These worries destroy the present moment.

### Practical Session Work

Have the client **check off 3–7 pleasant events** from a provided list that they could realistically do in the coming week. Have them write these on a worksheet. Discuss barriers (money, schedule, energy, shame, etc.) and problem-solve them. Encourage creativity: even small things like noticing enjoyable sensations while walking can accumulate.

### Be Patient

Emphasize that one or two small pleasant events are unlikely to shift mood dramatically. But practiced consistently over weeks, they compound. The metaphor: positive events are like food. Food does no good if you don't eat it; positive events do no good if you don't experience them. Over time, consistent engagement with pleasant events becomes noticeably mood-elevating.
