## Opposite Action: Changing Emotion Through Behavior

Opposite action is one of the most powerful and empirically supported emotion regulation skills in DBT. The principle is simple: when an emotion doesn't fit the facts or isn't effective for your goals, act opposite to what the emotion urges you to do. Do this *all the way* and *repeatedly*, and the emotion will change.

### The Core Rationale

Most effective psychotherapies for emotional disorders—behavioral activation for depression, exposure therapy for anxiety, anger management for aggression—all ask clients to reverse the expression and action components of problem emotions. This works because emotions are not purely cognitive or physiological; they're action-oriented. By changing the *behavior*, you change the whole emotional system.

### When Opposite Action Is Most Effective

**1. When knowing the facts doesn't work.** You intellectually know the dog isn't dangerous, but you're still terrified. You know your spouse loves you, but jealousy still flares when they look at others. Knowing doesn't always change feeling.

**2. When the emotion (or its intensity/duration) isn't justified.** The facts don't support the emotional response. Fear is high but danger is minimal. Shame is strong but rejection won't happen.

**3. When the emotion fits the facts but isn't effective for your goals.** Your boss criticizes you in front of people you want to impress. Anger is justified, but expressing it will damage your professional standing. Acting on the anger doesn't serve you.

**4. When you're avoiding what needs to be done.** Depression urges you to stay in bed; depression worsens when you isolate. Fear urges you to avoid a treatment that would reduce anxiety. Avoidance behavior perpetuates the problem.

### The Seven Steps: How to Do Opposite Action

**Step 1: Identify and name the emotion.** Use the emotion naming handout if you need it. Be specific.

**Step 2: Check the facts.** Does this emotion fit the situation? Is it justified? If yes, problem solving or acceptance may be better than opposite action.

**Step 3: Identify your action urges.** What does your body want to do? What are you tempted to say or do? This is crucial—opposite action only works when you're truly acting opposite to *your actual urges*, not following a generic handout.

**Step 4: Ask wise mind: Is acting on this emotion effective?** Will it make things better or worse? Will it solve the problem? Will expressing it be wise?

**Step 5: Act opposite to the emotion's urges.** If you've determined the emotion isn't justified or isn't effective, do the opposite. For fear, approach rather than avoid. For anger, gently leave the situation rather than attack. For shame, show yourself to others rather than hide.

**Step 6: Do it ALL the way.** This is critical and often where opposite action fails. Change your posture, facial expression, tone of voice, words, and thinking—not just one or two. 
- Don't say kind words in a sarcastic tone.
- Don't go to a party you're afraid of while standing in corners looking terrified.
- Don't get on a plane while thinking "This will crash."
- Don't reveal your shame while slumping and hiding.

Halfway opposite action doesn't work because your brain still encodes the original emotion signal.

**Step 7: Continue until the emotion goes down.** Practice repeatedly. It rarely works once. Building new emotional associations takes time and repetition, just like exposure therapy. The more you practice, the more your brain learns: "This situation isn't dangerous," or "This behavior doesn't cause the disaster I feared."

### Opposite Actions for Specific Emotions

**Fear**: Approach what you fear rather than avoid it. Do what you're afraid to do. The classic example: if you fall off a horse, get back on.

**Anger**: Gently avoid the person (rather than attack). Don't ruminate. Distract yourself. Do something kind. Try to understand their perspective. Practice half-smiling and willing hands (open, receptive posture).

**Disgust**: Bring the item or person you find disgusting close to you (once you've confirmed it's actually safe). Increased exposure to safe people or things increases liking; your brain habituates.

**Shame**: Stop hiding the behavior or characteristic in people who you know won't reject you. If the behavior violates your values (guilt, not shame), take responsibility and repair. If it doesn't violate your values, repeat the behavior in a safe group until shame diminishes.

**Sadness/Depression**: Get active. Increase rewarding activities and pleasant events. Approach, don't isolate. Do things that make you feel competent.

**Unjustified Love**: Avoid and distract from the person and reminders. Don't feed the love with loving thoughts. Repeatedly remind yourself why loving this person isn't justified (they don't love you back, they harm you, they require dependence).

**Unjustified Jealousy/Envy**: Let go of controlling others. Share what you have. Count your blessings instead of focusing on what others have.

### Critical Teaching Points for Therapists

**Don't suppress emotions; let them come while changing behavior.** Many clients try to suppress emotions while doing opposite action, which undermines the whole strategy. Instead, teach clients to *experience* the emotion while acting opposite—to keep their eyes, ears, and senses open so their brain learns something new. This is how emotional learning happens.

**Validate that halfway opposite action doesn't work.** When clients report that opposite action "isn't working," often they've only done it halfway. Demonstrate or role-play the difference between a client's effort and true opposite action. Ask about their automatic thoughts and tone of voice during the behavior.

**Expect time and repetition.** Children don't overcome fear of monsters under the bed from looking once. Adults don't overcome social anxiety from attending one party. Opposite action works, but it requires consistent practice.

**This is not about willpower or forcing positivity.** Opposite action isn't about "just being positive" or "willing yourself to feel better." It's about providing your nervous system with new evidence through behavior, which then changes emotion.
