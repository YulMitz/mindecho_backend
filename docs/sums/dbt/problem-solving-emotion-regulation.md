## Problem Solving: Changing Emotions by Changing Situations

When an emotion is truly justified—when the facts really are bad—the best way to change the emotion is to change the situation that caused it. Problem solving is the systematic approach to doing that. It's also a foundational life skill for building a life worth living.

### Why Problem Solving Matters

If you're anxious because you have unpaid bills, knowing the facts ("Yes, I really do owe money") won't reduce anxiety. Getting your finances in order will. If you're sad because you're isolated, getting active and connecting with others changes the situation. If you hate your job, finding a new one or changing aspects of the current job solves the core problem.

Problem solving is especially important for:
- **Repeated problems** that keep cycling (repeatedly being misinterpreted by a friend, failing important exams)
- **Avoided situations** that you skip because they cause painful emotions
- **Chronic ongoing stressors** (abusive relationship, hated job, financial strain)
- **Destructive behavior cycles** (self-harm, substance use, angry outbursts, missed therapy)
- **One-time crises** (no ride to an appointment, acute illness)

### Seven Steps to Problem Solving

**Step 1: Observe and describe the problem situation.**

Use the mindfulness "what" skill—just the facts, no interpretation. Then describe what's problematic about it and what consequences it creates for you.

*Example*: "My boss criticizes me in meetings. This creates conflict, makes me irritated, and makes me want to avoid work."

Also describe the obstacles getting in the way of solving it. These might be practical (lack of money, time, resources) or emotional (fear, shame, guilt).

**Step 2: Check the facts.**

Are your interpretations of the situation accurate? Are you catastrophizing? Overestimating obstacles? In emotional distress, it's easy to see more barriers than actually exist. If you can't identify what's driving a repeated problem, consider doing a chain analysis to trace the events leading up to it.

**Step 3: Identify your goal in solving the problem.**

Keep it simple and achievable. The ultimate goal is to reduce painful emotion, but what specifically has to happen for you to feel better? Make this concrete.

*Examples*: "Find a way to get to my appointment." "Get my friend to understand me better." "Find a job I don't hate." "Stop failing exams."

**Step 4: Brainstorm lots of solutions.**

Generate as many ideas as possible *without evaluating them yet*. This is crucial. No idea should be criticized during brainstorming—not even outrageous ones. The goal is to open your mind to possibilities you might not initially consider. Keep going until ideas run out.

*Example ideas for "I'm afraid to fail a cosmetology exam"*: Practice on a friend, do visualizations, buy a doll to practice on, arrange to use the mannequin on weekends, hire a tutor, ask the instructor for extra practice time.

Common blocks to brainstorming: people immediately evaluate feasibility, point out obstacles, or repeat the same ideas. As the therapist, you may need to generate creative or even absurd ideas to model that anything goes at this stage.

**Step 5: Choose a solution that fits the goal and is likely to work.**

Prioritize solutions by likelihood of working and feasibility of implementation. A solution that might work is useless if it's impossible to actually do. Use pros and cons to evaluate one or two of the best options with wise mind.

**Step 6: Put the solution into action.**

This is "where the rubber meets the road." The most common blocks are:
- **Inertia**: "This is too hard. I'm too tired. I don't have time."
- **Fear-generating thoughts**: "If this doesn't work, I'll look like a fool."
- **Willfulness**: "I shouldn't have to solve this; they caused it."
- **Impulsiveness**: Acting on the problem without the planned solution.

When clients know a solution but don't implement it, treat the block *itself* as a new problem to solve.

**Step 7: Evaluate the results.**

Did the solution work? Do you feel better? Were there unexpected obstacles or responses from others? Even failed attempts often improve the situation somewhat. If a solution doesn't fully solve the problem, you may need to try other solutions or refine the approach.

### Clinical Considerations

**Problems are specific to persons and situations.** A commute that's stressful for one person is fine for another. Don't impose your own solutions; help clients identify what's genuinely a problem for *them*.

**Emotions can be understandable and still not justified.** This is subtle but important. If someone tells you there's a snake in your office and there isn't, your fear makes sense given what you believe—but it's not justified by the actual facts. Similarly, a client's fear about losing their job might be understandable given their history, but if the boss has given no indication of dissatisfaction, the intensity might not fit the facts
