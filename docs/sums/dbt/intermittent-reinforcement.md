## Intermittent Reinforcement: Why Some Behaviors Never Go Away

Intermittent reinforcement occurs when a behavior is reinforced only *some* of the time, and especially when the timing is unpredictable. This schedule produces the most durable, persistent behaviors—ones that are "almost indestructible" and extremely resistant to extinction.

### Why Intermittent Reinforcement Creates Unstoppable Patterns

When reinforcement is intermittent and unpredictable, the person (or animal) learns that they *might* be rewarded if they keep trying. This creates persistence. The longer the time between reinforcements, and the more *variable* and unpredictable that timing, the more durable the behavior becomes.

This is the core principle behind:
- **Gambling addiction**: Slot machines are programmed on a variable intermittent schedule. You never know which pull will win, so you keep pulling.
- **Checking compulsions**: If you check your phone and sometimes (unpredictably) get a message, you'll check it compulsively because you never know when the reward will come.
- **Staying in abusive relationships**: An abusive partner may have been loving and attentive early on (continuous reinforcement), then gradually become unpredictable—sometimes loving, often cold or cruel. The unpredictability of occasional warmth keeps the person trying, hoping the good version will return.

### Clinical Examples

**Parenting and tantrums**: A parent who says "no" to a child's demands most of the time, but occasionally gives in (perhaps out of exhaustion), has accidentally put the tantrum behavior on an intermittent reinforcement schedule. The child learns: "If I keep crying, eventually I'll get what I want." This makes tantrums *more* persistent, not less. The occasional "yes" is more powerful than consistent enforcement would have been.

**Pursuing an uninterested romantic interest**: If someone occasionally responds warmly to your advances but usually ignores you, the intermittent reinforcement may keep you pursuing longer than if they consistently rejected you.

**Reassurance-seeking in anxiety**: If you ask for reassurance and sometimes get it (and sometimes don't), you'll keep asking. If someone consistently refuses to reassure you, the behavior may extinguish faster—paradoxically.

### Why This Matters for Treatment

Understanding intermittent reinforcement helps clients see:
1. **Why the behavior is so hard to stop**: It's not personal weakness; it's how conditioning works.
2. **Why inconsistent consequences fail**: If you sometimes give in to a problematic behavior and sometimes don't, you make it *stronger*, not weaker.
3. **Why consistent, predictable limits matter**: Whether you're setting boundaries in your own life or coaching someone else, consistency is crucial.

### The Implication for Extinction

If you're trying to eliminate a behavior that has been intermittently reinforced, you must be prepared for a potentially long process and strong behavioral resistance. The behavior won't extinguish quickly because the person has learned "maybe if I try again, it will work." This is why "riding out" a behavioral burst without giving in (even once) is so critical.
