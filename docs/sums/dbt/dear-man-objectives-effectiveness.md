## DEAR MAN: Skills for Achieving Your Objective

DEAR MAN is the acronym for the core steps in assertive communication when your main goal is to get something specific done. It works for asking, refusing, standing up for rights, resolving conflict, and getting your perspective taken seriously.

### Describe

Start by stating the facts of the situation—what someone with no emotional investment could observe or would need to know. Stick to objective events, not interpretations.

**Why it works**: Grounding the conversation in shared facts gives both of you a reference point and increases the chance you'll at least agree on the basic situation. It also gives you early warning if the other person will fundamentally disagree—before you invest emotional energy in your request.

**Common mistake**: Mixing facts with interpretations ("You always ignore me" vs. "You haven't responded to my last three texts"). When a client describes from emotion or fear, they slip into judgment. Use mindfulness describing skills to help them separate what happened from what they assume it means.

### Express

Clearly state how you feel or what you believe about the situation. Give a brief rationale for your request or refusal. Don't expect the other person to read your mind.

**Why it works**: Your feelings and beliefs are data that help the other person understand why this matters and what makes it worth their cooperation. Vulnerability here—sharing your genuine reaction—can draw their personal interest and increase motivation to help.

**What it's not**: Expressing is not the same as demanding or lecturing. You're sharing your perspective, not overwhelming them with it.

### Assert

Ask for what you want clearly, concisely, and directly. Say no firmly. Don't beat around the bush, hope they guess, or frame it as a suggestion. Use a direct question or statement.

**Examples**: "Will you accept a return on this shirt?" / "I need to say no to this. I can't give you a ride home so often." / "Can you give me the raise?"

**Common mistakes**: 
- Framing requests as questions or hints ("Would you maybe want to...?")
- Softening so much that it's unclear you're actually asking
- Telling someone what to do instead of asking (which prevents you from knowing if they agreed)

### Reinforce

Identify something positive for the other person if they do what you're asking. This might be a benefit to them, something you'll do in return, or simply genuine appreciation.

**Why it works**: People are motivated by positive consequences. If someone never gains anything from complying with requests, they'll eventually stop responding well.

**The carrot versus stick principle**: Positive motivation (carrots) is more effective long-term than threats (sticks). Threats may work while you're watching, but people won't follow through when you're gone. Finding what matters to the other person and linking your request to it is harder work but yields better results.

### (Stay) Mindful

Maintain focus on your goal and don't get sidetracked. Two core techniques:

**Broken Record**: Repeat your request, refusal, or position over and over, using the same calm words and tone. You don't have to invent new arguments each time—just replay what you've already said. The strength is in persistent, emotionless repetition. "Kill them with kindness" in your tone while standing firm on content.

**Ignore Attacks and Diversions**: If the other person attacks you, threatens, insults, or tries to change the subject, don't take the bait. Responding to attacks gives them control of the conversation and reinforces their use of those tactics. Simply continue making your point as if they hadn't attacked. This is harder than it sounds and requires practice.

### Appear Confident

Use a calm, steady voice tone. Make appropriate eye contact. Maintain composed body language and posture. You may feel nervous or scared—that's normal and acceptable—but **appear** confident anyway. The skill is "appear," not "be."

**Why it matters**: Your nonverbal message often overrides your words. Stammering, whispering, staring at the floor, or apologizing conveys that you don't deserve respect for what you want.

### Negotiate

If standard assertiveness isn't working, be willing to give to get. Offer alternative solutions, reduce your request, or ask the other person for ideas. "What can we do here that works for both of us?"

**When to use it**: After breaking record and ignoring haven't moved things, or when it's clear that neither of you will budge on the original terms.

### Using DEAR MAN with Difficult Interactions

When the other person has strong skills too and keeps refusing or won't listen:

1. **Describe the current interaction** (without blaming their motives): "It seems like we keep going in circles here."
2. **Express your feelings about the stuck situation**: "I'm getting frustrated that we can't find a way forward."
3. **Assert a wish in the situation**: "Can we take a break and come back to this later?" or "Will you think about it?"
4. **Reinforce**: "I really want to work this out with you."

### Practice and Homework

DEAR MAN requires behavioral rehearsal. Clients shouldn't wait for a crisis to practice. Low-stakes situations build skill: asking a librarian for help, requesting a menu substitution, asking a coworker for a favor. The more they practice the structure, the more automatic it becomes under stress.
