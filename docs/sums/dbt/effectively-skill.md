## Effectively: Skillful Means and Pragmatism

Effectively, the third "how" skill in DBT mindfulness, means doing what *works* to achieve your goals. It is the opposite of "cutting off your nose to spite your face"—acting in ways that feel righteous but actually sabotage what you want.

### The Core Principle

The goal is to reach your goals, reduce suffering, and increase happiness. Being right feels good in the moment. Getting what you actually want feels even better, and lasts longer. Willfulness (rigid insistence on your way) often blocks effectiveness. Willingness (flexibility and pragmatism) unlocks it.

### How to Teach Effectiveness

**Know your goal.** You cannot act effectively toward a goal you haven't clarified. Clients often confuse emotions with goals:
- "I'm angry at him" is an emotion, not a goal. What do you actually want? (To repair the relationship? To set a boundary? To leave?)
- "I'm afraid of being alone" is an emotion. What's your actual goal? (To build connections? To feel less anxious? To develop self-reliance?)

When emotions cloud judgment, ask: "If you step back, what would actually help you right now?"

**React to reality as it is, not as it should be.** This is crucial. A client wants a raise and believes the supervisor *should* know without being told. Reality: the supervisor doesn't know. The effective move is to ask. A client tailgates a slow driver, flashing headlights, because other drivers *should* follow the rules. Reality: this driver is not following the rule. The effective move is to pass and move on.

**Know what will and won't work.** Sometimes you need to ask for help or instructions. Humble experimentation and learning from mistakes are part of effectiveness. A client wants people to remember their birthday. Effective: call and remind them beforehand. A client wants to make someone happy. More effective: find out what actually makes them happy, then do it.

**Play by the rules when necessary.** If you are in a low-power position (patient in a hospital, prisoner, applicant for a loan, person going through airport security), the people with power make the rules. Being right about the unfairness of a rule does not override the person's power to enforce it. In these situations, working within the rules—even unjust ones—is often the only way to get what you want.

**Be savvy about people.** Different people respond to different approaches. What works with one person may not work with another. Taking people where they are, rather than where they "should" be, is pragmatic. A client who is direct and honest may need to soften their approach with a sensitive supervisor. A client who is people-pleasing may need to be clearer and more direct with a boundary-pushing family member. Adapt your method to the person.

**Sacrifice principles when necessary for urgent goals.** In extreme situations (concentration camp, life-threatening danger), most people sacrifice principles to survive. In everyday life, it may be harder, but sometimes it's necessary. A client wants to keep their job but has rigid views about how authority *should* operate. Effective: follow the (unfair) rules, keep the paycheck, build resources, then leave if possible.

### Common Client Resistance

- **"But that's not fair!"** You: "You're right. It's not fair. And it's also reality. What do you want to do with that reality?"
- **"I shouldn't have to ask; they should just know."** You: "That's a true statement. And people often don't know things we think they should. So if you want them to know, what's the effective way to make that happen?"
- **"I'd rather be right than happy."** You: "I hear that. And there's a cost to that choice. Are you willing to pay it? Or is there a way to get closer to both?"

### In-Session Practice

Identify a real situation where the client is stuck:
- **Client:** "My teenager won't listen to me. I've told him a hundred times to clean his room."
- **You:** "What's your goal here? A clean room, or a better relationship, or something else?"
- **Client:** "Both. But he *should* listen when I ask."
- **You:** "He's not listening. That's the reality. If the goal is a clean room and a better relationship, what might actually work with *this* teenager, even if it's not what should work?"

Help them brainstorm: incentives, natural consequences, involving the teen in the rule-making, backing off on some battles to win trust on others. Effectiveness is creative, adaptive, and focused on outcomes—not on proving that your way is the right way.
