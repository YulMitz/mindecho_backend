## Relationship Generalization: Using Group Dynamics as Skill Practice

Relationship generalization is the practice of helping clients recognize when their everyday relationship problems are showing up *within the skills training group itself*, then using that moment to practice new skills in vivo rather than abstractly.

### Core Principle

The skills training group becomes a microcosm of clients' larger relational world. When a client demonstrates a problematic pattern in group (e.g., dismissing feedback, demanding others change rather than taking responsibility, or reacting defensively to perceived invalidation), that moment offers a powerful opportunity for real-world skill practice. Rather than teaching skills as abstract techniques to use "out there," the therapist can say: "This is happening right now. This is exactly the kind of situation we're learning skills for."

### How to Recognize It

Watch for clients who:
- Repeatedly insist the only solution to their problem is someone else changing (not themselves)
- React with tears, anger, or shutdown when offered alternative perspectives
- Blame the group format or leaders for their distress rather than looking inward
- Avoid accepting responsibility or problem-solving collaboratively
- Show the same relational stuckness in group that they report struggling with outside

### The Therapeutic Approach

**Sensitivity is essential.** Clients with histories of invalidation or criticism are hypersensitive to feeling blamed or publicly shamed. Linehan emphasizes:

1. **Make individual problems universal.** Rather than singling out one person ("You keep refusing to solve your own part"), frame it as a general group observation: "I'm noticing something that comes up for a lot of us..." This allows the person to see themselves in the pattern without being publicly identified.

2. **Connect the in-group moment to out-of-group patterns.** Point out explicitly: "The thing that just happened here—where it felt hard to accept a suggestion—that's the same thing that happens with your son, isn't it? This is a chance to practice something different."

3. **Plan for generalization; don't assume it.** Simply noticing the pattern is not enough. The therapist must:
   - Discuss it explicitly with the client (ideally at the end of the session, in private)
   - Develop alternative response patterns the client can try
   - Assign homework where the client practices the new skill in real-life situations
   - Review what happened and refine the approach

4. **Use it as teaching, not punishment.** The moment a client feels humiliated or attacked, the learning opportunity evaporates. The tone should be collaborative: "Here's what I'm seeing, and here's how the skills we're learning could help."

### When It Goes Wrong

If a group dynamic is genuinely rooted in the group format rather than the client's patterns—poor group fit, a personality conflict with a co-leader, or legitimate structural problems—relationship generalization is inappropriate. Leaders must distinguish between:
- **Within-client patterns** (how they handle feedback, solve problems, relate to authority) → use for generalization
- **Actual group problems** (inappropriate behavior from others, group norms that aren't working, logistical issues) → fix the problem directly

If unsure, pause the skills content, address the issue, and discuss it openly rather than force it into a teaching moment.
