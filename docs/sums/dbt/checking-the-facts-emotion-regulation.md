## Checking the Facts: Reality-Testing for Emotional Responses

Checking the facts is a foundational emotion regulation skill that asks: Does this emotion match the actual situation? It's the first step before deciding whether to change your emotion through opposite action or problem solving.

### Why This Matters in Session

Emotions evolved to help us respond to real dangers and opportunities. But our minds can misinterpret situations, catastrophize, or predict disasters that may never happen. When fear, anger, or shame is triggered by *thoughts about* what might happen rather than what *is* happening, checking facts can often reduce emotional intensity directly—without needing to change behavior or the situation itself.

### The Core Process

**Step 1: Identify your interpretation.** What story are you telling yourself about this situation? What do you believe will happen?

**Step 2: Separate facts from interpretation.** What actually happened (or is happening)? What did you *assume* or *predict*?

**Step 3: Test the accuracy of your thoughts.** Use one or more of these methods:
- **Review past outcomes**: Has this worry come true before? Last time you had a headache, did you have a terrible disease? Probably not.
- **Observe the actual situation**: Check for evidence. Is the person actually carrying a weapon? Are others around, suggesting this is safe?
- **Seek information**: Ask directly instead of assuming. If a coworker ignored you at the mall, ask if they saw you rather than concluding they dislike you.
- **Gather facts**: Look at the car after your son's accident instead of assuming it's destroyed. Apply for the jobs you think you can't get.
- **Perform experiments**: Test your prediction in the real world to see if it comes true.

**Step 4: Generate alternative outcomes.** Even if your feared event *could* happen, what else might happen instead? When your boss asks to meet, might they ask for a project update, or offer a bonus, rather than firing you? Simply generating alternatives can increase your belief that other outcomes are possible.

### Common Distortions to Watch For

**Catastrophizing**: Exaggerating the negative and focusing only on the worst possible outcome. ("My car is destroyed." "I'm going to die.") Catastrophizing amplifies both physical and emotional pain and is often the real problem, not the facts themselves.

**Unjustified emotion intensity**: The emotion might fit the facts somewhat, but its *intensity* or *duration* doesn't match the actual threat level. You lose a credit card and react as though you're facing lifelong poverty. Someone cuts in front of you in line, and you react as if they physically attacked you.

**Reasonable but unjustified**: An emotion can be understandable—given what you believe to be true—but still not justified by the actual facts. If you're told a poisonous snake is in your office, fear makes sense. But if the snake was removed overnight and nobody told you, your fear is now understandable but *not justified by the facts*. This distinction is critical and often difficult for clients to grasp.

### When Checking Facts Might Not Be Enough

If the emotion *is* justified—the facts really are bad—then checking facts won't reduce it. In these cases, move to **opposite action** (if the emotion isn't effective for your goals) or **problem solving** (if you need to change the situation itself).

Also, intensely emotional clients may have narrowed attention and heightened threat sensitivity due to the emotion itself. In these moments, suggest **crisis survival skills** (TIP skills, distraction, self-soothing, or pros/cons of staying emotional) before or alongside fact-checking.

### A Clinical Note: "Middle-of-the-Night Thinking"

Clients often report catastrophic, ruminating thoughts at night that seem ridiculous in the morning. Teaching them to recognize these as "middle-of-the-night thoughts" and defer evaluation until morning can be validating and practical. Pair this with distress tolerance skills like cold water on the face or paced breathing to get through the night.
