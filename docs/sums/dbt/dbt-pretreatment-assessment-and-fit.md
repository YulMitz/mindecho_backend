## Pretreatment Assessment and Treatment Matching

Before a client begins DBT skills training, comprehensive assessment determines whether DBT is appropriate and what configuration of care will best serve them.

### Core Assessment Elements

**Clinical presentation:**
- Presenting problems and life goals
- Current and past life-threatening behaviors (suicide attempts, self-harm, dangerous acts)
- Diagnostic evaluation as needed
- Severity, pervasiveness, and complexity of any mental disorder
- Impact on daily functioning and imminent risk to life

**Functional assessment:**
- Reading level and language comprehension (important for using workbooks)
- Ability to understand and follow written materials
- Previous experience with DBT or DBT skills training

**Screening interview:** Phone screening can be valuable, especially for specialized groups (substance use treatment, suicidal individuals, eating disorder groups). This allows early identification of clients whose needs may not be suited to the particular program.

### Treatment Intensity Decision Framework

The client's "level of disorder" determines appropriate intensity. This is defined by current mental illness, severity, scope, and complexity of dysfunction, plus the disability and imminent threat to life the disorder creates. Four stages guide matching:

**Stage 1: Severe disorder, behavioral dyscontrol, life threat**
- Life-threatening behaviors: suicide attempts, crisis behaviors, deliberate self-harm, other imminent danger
- Severe therapy-interfering behaviors: noncollaboration, noncompliance, absenteeism, behaviors that disrupt treatment or other patients
- Severe quality-of-life interference: incapacitating mental illness, homelessness, criminal behavior with high jail risk, domestic violence, serious consequences from behavioral dyscontrol

**Appropriate for Stage 1:**
- Standard outpatient DBT (skills training + individual therapy + coaching + consultation team)
- Intensive case management plus DBT suicide protocol and crisis planning
- Inpatient/residential/day treatment DBT
- Skills training while on waiting list for individual therapy

**Stage 2: PTSD, residual mood/anxiety disorders, emotion dysregulation**
- Moderate-severity disorders not adequately addressed in Stage 1
- Significant emotional pain or restricted emotional experience
- Difficulty tolerating emotions; shame, guilt, anger, emptiness, fear

**Appropriate for Stage 2:**
- Skills training for emotion dysregulation
- Skills training for eating disorders
- Skills training for treatment-resistant depression
- DBT PTSD protocol with evidence-based exposure therapy

**Stage 3: Problems in living, mild disorders, quality-of-life limitations**
- Mild-to-moderate disorders
- Difficulty setting or achieving goals
- Problem-solving deficits
- Low self-efficacy or self-esteem
- Relationship or employment difficulties
- Mild emotion dysregulation

**Appropriate for Stage 3:**
- Skills training plus DBT team plus as-needed individual therapy or coaching

**Stage 4: Sense of incompleteness, search for meaning**
- Desire for spiritual fulfillment or spiritual direction
- Seeking peak experiences or deeper understanding of reality
- End-of-life concerns

**Appropriate for Stage 4:**
- Advanced mindfulness and reality-acceptance skills (under development)

### Curriculum and Skills Selection

Once level of care is determined, the next decision is which skills curriculum fits this client's problems. Selection should be based on:
- Published research on what skills address what problems (though research often lacks specificity)
- The client's specific presenting problems
- Available resources in your setting

Standard DBT core skills (mindfulness, interpersonal effectiveness, emotion regulation, distress tolerance) are taught in most programs. Specialized modules are added based on client need:
- Addiction/substance use skills for those with SUD
- Eating disorder-specific skills for eating disorders
- Emotion dysregulation skills for severe affective dyscontrol

### Progress Tracking and Outcome Measurement

**DBT Diary Card:** Clients and therapists use this weekly to track:
- Problem behaviors (frequency/intensity)
- Mood and self-efficacy changes
- Skill use and perceived helpfulness

The diary card informs weekly therapy but requires data entry and graphing for outcome analysis.

**Standardized measures:** Consider using validated instruments with acceptable psychometrics. Administer at pretreatment, then at regular intervals (every 3–4 months for 6-month programs; at 4, 8, 12 months for annual programs). Useful measures include:
- Borderline Symptom Checklist–23 (BSL-23)
- Difficulties in Emotion Regulation Scale (DERS)
- Depression and anxiety rating scales (PHQ-9, HAM-A, HAM-D)
- PTSD symptom checklists
- Suicidal ideation and behavior inventories (SBQ, Reasons for Living Inventory)
- Dissociative experiences measures (DES)

Data tracking allows you to measure change over time, identify which clients benefit most, and refine treatment protocols.
