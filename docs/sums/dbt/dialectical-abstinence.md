## Dialectical Abstinence: Both Commitment and Realism

Dialectical abstinence synthesizes two seemingly opposite approaches to addiction: absolute abstinence (never engaging in the behavior again) and harm reduction (minimizing damage if a lapse occurs). This both-and approach is more effective than choosing either one alone.

### The Problem It Solves

Clients face a dilemma: They want to stop their addictive behavior completely. Yet they fear that if they lapse, they'll be unable to recommit. Two traditional approaches each have drawbacks:

- **Abstinence-only commitment:** People who commit to 100% abstinence tend to stay abstinent longer, but when they slip, they often struggle longer to recommit. They may feel like total failures.
- **Harm reduction focus:** People working on harm reduction often get back "on the wagon" faster after a slip, but they also relapse quicker overall.

Dialectical abstinence offers a third path.

### The Dialectical Synthesis

Dialectical abstinence is a both-and approach:
- **While abstinent:** Maintain 100% commitment to never engaging in the behavior again. Think like an "abstentarian"—someone who never says "just this once."
- **If a lapse occurs:** Immediately activate a predetermined harm reduction plan to minimize damage and get back to abstinence quickly.

This is not accepting lapses before they happen (that would undermine commitment). It's acknowledging they're possible while committing fully to preventing them, and being prepared to respond skillfully if they occur.

### The Core Commitment: Making It 100%

When setting abstinence, the commitment must be absolute. Anything less than 100% sets clients up for failure because the urge to engage in addictive behavior is extraordinarily strong.

**Key teaching:** A useful comparison is to vegetarians. They don't say "just this one time" and eat meat. They're committed not to eat certain foods, full stop. When committing to abstinence, adopt the same mindset: become an "abstentarian" who never says "just this once."

**Thinking like an Olympic athlete:** Imagine you're like Olympic athletes preparing for competition. Before the race, nothing is discussed except winning—going for the gold. If athletes thought "a bronze medal would be fine," their training, performance, and push would all weaken. Athletes also don't think about falling or twisting an ankle. Those thoughts stay out of their minds
