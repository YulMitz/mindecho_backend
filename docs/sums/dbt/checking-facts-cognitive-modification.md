## Checking the Facts: Separating Thoughts from Reality

Emotions are often not about what actually happened—they're about what we *think* happened. This simple insight is one of the most powerful levers for emotion change.

### Why Thoughts Shape Emotions

**The core mechanism:** We react to our interpretations and thoughts about events, not to the events themselves. A person who believes their loved one died in a car accident will feel intense grief—even if the information was wrong and the loved one is alive. The *thought* created the emotion, not the fact.

**What gets distorted:**
1. **Beliefs about reality** ("She didn't text me back because she hates me")
2. **Beliefs about what we need** ("I need drugs to handle my pain" vs. "I need to find healthy coping")
3. **Beliefs about other people's obligations** ("You *should* be different; you *owe* me")
4. **Absolute, all-or-nothing thinking** ("If I'm not perfect, I'm a failure")

All of these create or intensify painful emotions, even when the factual basis is thin or absent.

### How Emotions Affect Thinking (The Vicious Cycle)

It's bidirectional. Moods color interpretation:

- **Angry/annoyed?** The cheerful friend calling seems like a pest, not a loyal supporter
- **Anxious?** The wind rattling your window sounds like a break-in
- **Ashamed?** Two coworkers laughing in the hallway are clearly mocking *your* mistake
- **Sad?** A bad grade means you'll never graduate

The emotion narrows what you notice and how you interpret it. This is why emotions "love themselves"—they perpetuate by making you hypervigilant to confirming evidence while filtering out disconfirming evidence.

### The Steps: Checking the Facts

#### 1. Name the emotion clearly
Before fact-checking, be precise about which emotion you're trying to change. Review Handout 6 if needed. Different emotions fit different facts, so clarity matters.

#### 2. Describe the prompting event (facts only)
Describe only what you observed through your senses. Strip out judgments, absolute language, and interpretations.

**Poor:** "My boss was terrible to me and completely unreasonable."
**Better:** "My boss said my report had three errors and asked me to fix them by tomorrow."

The first is loaded with judgment; the second is observable fact.

**Common errors:** Clients often include the entire history ("And then he said... and before that she..."). Redirect: the history goes in vulnerability factors. The prompting event is just the immediate trigger.

#### 3. Identify your interpretations, thoughts, and assumptions
What did you add to the facts? What story did you construct?

**Examples of fact vs. interpretation:**

| Fact | Interpretation |
|------|---|
| Friend walking down mall with another friend | Friend likes the other person more than me |
| No response to text after 2 hours | Person is angry at me |
| Coworker quiet in meeting | I did something wrong |
| Partner looking at someone else | They're attracted to that person |

Generate *alternative interpretations* that also fit the facts:
- "My friend didn't see me because it was crowded"
- "They're busy and haven't checked their phone"
- "They're tired today"
- "They were just being polite to someone who approached them"

#### 4. Ask: What threat am I imagining?
Painful emotions are always tied to some perceived threat. Often it's implicit—not even in words, just a felt sense of danger. The threat might be:

- **Anger:** "I'm being attacked; my goals are blocked"
- **Fear:** "Something bad will happen to me"
- **Sadness:** "I've lost something permanently"
- **Shame:** "I'll be kicked out; I don't belong"
- **Guilt:** "I've violated my own values"
- **Jealousy:** "Someone is taking away what's mine"
