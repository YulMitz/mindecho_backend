## Describing: Translating Observation into Accurate Language

**What It Is**

Describing is the second "what" skill—putting words on observations in a way that sticks to the facts. It's distinguishing what you actually observed from what you *think*, *feel*, *assume*, or *concluded* about it.

The core principle: **If it wasn't observed, it can't be described.**

Many of our emotional problems come from describing unobserved things (other people's thoughts, intentions, emotions, causes, or meanings) as if they were facts.

**Why This Matters Clinically**

A client observes their partner not being dressed when they get home. Their mind *describes* this as "You don't love me" or "You're being lazy." The actual observation: a person wearing pajamas. The conclusion: a statement about love and character. These are not the same thing.

When clients treat their thoughts about events as facts, they:
- Respond emotionally to stories instead of reality
- Make ineffective decisions based on assumptions
- Damage relationships by accusing others of things they never observed
- Ruminate endlessly on interpretations

**Research shows**: Describing and labeling emotions neurologically regulates them. Brain imaging reveals that when people accurately label their emotional responses ("I notice sadness," not "Everything is terrible"), the brain regions involved in emotional reactivity quiet down.

**What Cannot Be Observed (and Therefore Cannot Be Described)**

Help clients distinguish these:

1. **Thoughts of others**: "You think I'm lying" ≠ observing thoughts. Accurate: "I think you think I'm lying" or "I'm having the thought that you believe I'm lying."

2. **Intentions of others**: "You're trying to manipulate me" ≠ observation. You can observe: actions, words, effects. You cannot observe intention. Better: "When you said X, I felt afraid and concluded you were trying to hurt me."

3. **Emotions of others**: "You're angry" is an inference. You can observe: tight jaw, raised voice, body posture. Different emotions can look similar. Someone withdrawing might be ashamed, not angry. Better: "You're not talking to me, and I'm thinking you're upset."

4. **Concepts, meanings, causes, changes**: "You've changed" ≠ observation (it's a comparison/conclusion). "You're a bad person" ≠ observation. These require the mind to put observations together into an interpretation.

**How to Coach Describing Accuracy**

When a client says, "He doesn't want to talk to me," ask:
- What did he actually do or say?
- How do you know he doesn't want to talk?
- Could other explanations fit the facts?

Use the **"instruction to a designer"** test: If you had to instruct someone to paint or film what you observed, what details would you give? "His eyebrows were drawn together, he didn't make eye contact, he gave one-word answers" is describing. "He's angry and rejecting me" is interpreting.

Frame it as: "**When you [specific observed behavior], I think/feel [your response].**"
- "When you don't answer my texts for hours, I start thinking you're upset with me."
- "When you raise your voice, I feel afraid."

This format clarifies what you observed vs. what you're concluding.

**Describing Your Own Mind**

This is tricky because clients ARE observing their own thoughts and feelings. The describing part is recognizing they're observing *thoughts about events*, not the events themselves.

"I'm having the thought that I'm a failure" ≠ "I am a failure"  
"I notice sadness" = observing your own emotional experience (valid)  
"Everything is hopeless" = thought, not observation of events

**In Session: When Describing Fails**

A client says: "My therapist hates working with me."

You might respond: "I hear you think that. Let me describe what I actually observe: I show up for our appointments, I ask questions about your experience, I remember things you've told me. Those are facts. The thought 'she hates working with me' is coming from your mind, likely based on something—maybe your own feeling that you're hard to help, or an exchange that hurt. Can you separate what you directly observed from what your mind concluded?"

This is not dismissing their experience; it's helping them see the difference between thought and reality, which is precisely where their power lies.

**Between-Session Practice**

Have them:
- Describe one interaction that bothered them, sticking only to observable facts (what was said, what was done, facial expressions)
- Write down the thought/interpretation they added
- Notice: Does describing the facts alone create less emotional distress than the full story?
