## Three Goals in Interpersonal Effectiveness

In any interpersonal interaction with a goal, three things are simultaneously at stake. Learning to clarify which matters most—and why—is the foundation of effective communication in DBT.

### The Three Competing Priorities

**Objectives Effectiveness**: Getting the specific result you want—a raise, a refusal that sticks, a boundary honored, your viewpoint taken seriously. This is the concrete outcome: what the other person should do, stop doing, agree to, or understand.

**Relationship Effectiveness**: Maintaining or improving how the other person feels about you *after the interaction ends*—regardless of whether you got what you wanted. It's the difference between getting your way while the person resents you, versus getting your way (or not) while they respect you more.

**Self-Respect Effectiveness**: Acting in ways that align with your values, maintain your sense of competence and integrity, and let you respect yourself when the conversation is over. This includes not compromising your morals for approval, not acting helpless when you're capable, and standing up for what matters to you.

### Why This Matters in Session

Clients often operate as if one goal always trumps the others. Some sacrifice every objective to preserve relationships, then explode in frustration months later. Others prioritize standing firm on principle and burn bridges unnecessarily. Still others chase approval so desperately they violate their own values.

The real skill is **context-dependent prioritization**. In one situation, getting the raise (objective) might matter most; in another, preserving the friendship (relationship) is worth more; in a third, standing up for your belief (self-respect) is non-negotiable.

### The Cost of Extreme Approaches

**Always prioritizing relationship** at the expense of your needs leads to resentment, unmet needs accumulating, and eventual relationship breakdown—often through blowups or by leaving. Relationships cannot sustain indefinite sacrifice without reciprocity.

**Always pursuing objectives** without regard for relationship can get short-term wins but at the cost of future cooperation, goodwill, and social approval.

**Always making self-respect the main issue** can mean never compromising, never letting others "win," and creating rigid, conflict-prone interactions.

The therapeutic task is helping clients see that all three matter in every situation—they just weight differently depending on context.
