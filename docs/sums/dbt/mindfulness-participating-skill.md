## Participating: Full Engagement in the Present Moment

**What It Is**

Participating is throwing yourself completely into an activity, entering wholly into life with full awareness. It's the state psychologists call "flow"—where the separation between you and what you're doing dissolves, and you become merged with the activity itself.

When participating: thinking about doing the activity stops. You just do it. There is no self-conscious narration. A dancer becomes the dance. A runner becomes the running.

**Why It Matters**

This is often the goal state clients are seeking without knowing it. When people describe their best moments—times they felt fully alive, unafraid, connected—they're describing flow states of participation.

**Key Benefits:**

1. **Incompatible with depression/numbness**: Full engagement naturally brings aliveness.
2. **Incompatible with self-consciousness and social anxiety**: When you're fully absorbed in an activity, you're not watching yourself. No audience exists.
3. **Incompatible with boredom**: True participation is incompatible with boredom and always involves a sense of enjoyment and control.
4. **Effort becomes effortless**: Great exertion feels easy in flow; actions are smooth and spontaneous.
5. **Presence with others**: Participating requires genuine presence. You don't miss your own life, and you don't miss being part of others' lives. This is where real compassion and intimacy live.
6. **Skill mastery requires it**: You can only excel at any activity by participating in it fully. The piano player who thinks about finger positions during a performance plays poorly. The expert has practiced so much that participating *is* the performance.

**The Three "What" Skills Balanced**

Clients naturally tend toward one skill:

- **Natural participators** (often diagnosed as ADHD or impulsive) don't step back. They participate in everything, often without noticing when they're participating in ways that hurt themselves or others. They need to practice observing and describing to gain awareness and make better choices.

- **Natural observers** (often anxious or avoidant) stay on the sidelines. They watch life but don't live it. They're afraid of failing, of being judged. They need to practice participating to build confidence and experience safety in engagement.

- **Natural describers** (analytical, ruminating) are constantly narrating. Their mind is a running commentary. They analyze relationships, their performance, everything—which keeps them disconnected from living. They need to practice participating to quiet the constant internal dialogue.

The goal is balance: observe to understand, describe to communicate clearly, participate to live.

**Common Blocks to Participating**

*"I'm too self-conscious"*  
Self-consciousness is the opposite of participation. In true participation, there's no "self" separate from the action. Practice participating in low-stakes activities first: clapping along to music, walking in nature, doing a simple craft. Notice: when you're fully absorbed, does the self-doubt still exist?

*"What if I fail / look stupid?"*  
This is mind-reading about others' judgments. In participation, there's no audience in your awareness. You're inside the activity, not watching yourself from outside. The fear quiets when you actually do the activity fully.

*"I can't relax / have fun"*  
Often these clients have a very active observing or describing mind. They're watching themselves have fun, which blocks actual fun. Suggest: try a structured activity (dance, game, sport) where the rules or rhythm do some of the "thinking" for them, freeing them to participate.

**In Session**

When a client says: "I was at the party but I wasn't really there"  
Explore: Were you observing yourself from outside, thinking about what others thought? Or were you so anxious you were narrating everything? Help them see the difference between participating and watching themselves participate.

When someone is stuck in rumination or analysis:  
"What's one thing you could do right now where you'd have to put full attention into it—not thinking about it, just doing it?"

When depression or numbness is the issue:  
Participating *is* often what rebuilds the felt sense of aliveness. It's not about forcing happiness; it's about actually being present in an activity rather than numb to it.

**Practice Ideas**

Some participate well through:
- Physical activity (dance, sports, yoga)
- Creative work (art, music, writing)
- Social play (games, improvisation, group activities)
- Nature (walking barefoot, gardening, swimming)
- Skilled work (cooking, building, crafting)

The activity itself matters less than the quality of attention—full, unselfconscious, engaged.
