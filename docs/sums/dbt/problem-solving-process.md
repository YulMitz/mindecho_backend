## Problem-Solving for Justified Emotions

When a client's emotional distress makes sense given their actual circumstances, the therapeutic task shifts from emotion regulation to **problem-solving**—changing the situation itself rather than just changing how they feel about it.

### Core Principle: Problems Take Time

The most important frame to establish is that effective problem-solving is a **process that takes time and patience**. Many clients expect simple, one-shot solutions and give up when the first attempt fails. This expectation itself becomes a barrier to the focused, difficult, and time-consuming work real problems require. Share examples—yours or others'—of problems that required multiple attempts before resolution. This normalizes persistence as part of competent problem-solving, not a sign of failure.

### Four Problem-Solving Strategies

Once you've confirmed the emotion is justified (it fits the facts), guide the client through these four approaches in sequence:

**1. Act on the Emotion**
The action urges tied to basic emotions often align with solutions. Fear prompts us to escape danger; sadness prompts us to grieve and process loss; anger prompts us to address boundary violations. Sometimes the emotional action urge *is* the solution. Example: If someone you love withdraws, the love urge—to spend time with them, pursue reconnection—may be exactly what's needed.

**2. Change the Situation**
When the emotional action alone gives only temporary relief, use problem-solving to permanently alter the situation. Example: Running from a rat and jumping on a chair reduces fear momentarily but doesn't solve the problem. Problem-solving means getting a trap, baiting it, or calling an exterminator.

**3. Avoid or Leave the Situation**
Sometimes the situation is more powerful than the client is. When change is impossible, leaving becomes the viable option. Example: A student being bullied may need to change schools rather than keep trying to stop the bullying. An employee in a role where they consistently fail may need to find a different job.

**4. Change Thoughts and Interpretations**
When the situation can't be changed and can't be left, cognitive reframing becomes the tool. This is not about denying reality, but adjusting perspective. Example: Cheerleading yourself through a scary but necessary situation ("I can do this; I will be OK") even when real danger exists. Or cultivating a different relationship to envy by valuing simplicity rather than material wealth.

### Teaching Notes

Be prepared to clarify which strategy applies to different emotions. For example:
- **Fear** often benefits from building a sense of control and mastery through gradual exposure, not just escape
- **Anger** should be "fought back" only when it's genuinely effective (defending yourself from an attacker) not reflexively (giving away your wallet may be safer than fighting)
- **Disgust** may require practicing understanding or empathy for the disgusting person/event, especially if you can't avoid them
- **Shame** solutions (hiding behaviors, avoiding groups, finding new groups) should only be used if the client actually wants to avoid rejection—otherwise, activist change to group values is valid
- **Guilt** requires figuring out what harm occurred, then repairing it—which may take time, especially when trust has been broken or something irreplaceable was lost

The key is helping clients see that the problem and the solution are often _specific and concrete_, not abstract. This makes problem-solving feel achievable.
