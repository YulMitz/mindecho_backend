## Skills Training Behavioral Targets: Priority Hierarchy

DBT skills training has three clear behavioral targets, ranked by importance. Unlike individual psychotherapy, skills training subordinates crisis and quality-of-life work to skill acquisition—with one exception: behaviors that would destroy the therapy itself must be stopped first.

### Priority 1: Stopping Behaviors Likely to Destroy Skills Training

This is the only behavior that ranks above skill teaching. **Therapy-destroying behaviors** are those that, if unchecked, would end skills training for the individual or the group.

**Included behaviors:**
- Violent acts: throwing objects, pounding destructively, hitting or verbally attacking other clients (note: attacking trainers does *not* count as therapy-destroying)
- Self-injurious acts during sessions: cutting, scratching, picking scabs, excessive medication
- Suicide crisis behaviors: credible suicidal threats followed by storming out
- Behaviors that make concentration impossible: yelling, hysterical crying, loud moaning, constant out-of-turn talking
- Serious interpersonal ruptures among group members or between members and leaders that would cause someone to leave therapy

**What trainers do:**
Use a modified therapy-interfering behavior protocol (see Table 4.1): behaviorally define what is happening, analyze it briefly, make consequences clear, adopt a problem-solving plan. For clients in standard DBT, deeper work on the destructive behavior goes to the individual therapist. Contingency management (both positive and aversive consequences) may be necessary to bring behavior under control quickly.

**Key example from Linehan's clinical experience**: When a group member was raped on her way to a skills training session, the group discussed it—because the trauma was extreme and would have destroyed the therapeutic relationship if ignored. But even then, the frame was skills-based: "How can you use distress tolerance, emotion regulation, and social support skills to cope?"

### Priority 2: Skill Acquisition, Strengthening, and Generalization

This is the **core work** of skills training. Most session time goes to teaching, practicing, and applying mindfulness, distress tolerance, emotion regulation, and interpersonal effectiveness skills.

**Why this matters for trainers:**
Clients with emotion dysregulation find active practice effortful and contrary to their passive coping style. If a trainer shifts attention away from a client who is being passive or dysregulated—by moving to another group member or asking how the client is feeling—this **reinforces passivity**. Instead, trainers sometimes need to "drag clients through difficult moments" strategically, ensuring they stay engaged in skill practice even when uncomfortable.

**Exception**: For brand-new group members, those with social anxiety, or those who have assertively stated they intend to be willful, excessive pushing may backfire. Leave such clients alone initially; meet with them during break if needed to problem-solve.

### Priority 3: Reducing Therapy-Interfering Behaviors

Behaviors that slow progress (but don't destroy therapy) are **lowest priority**. These include mood-dependent passivity, restlessness, pacing, doodling, sitting oddly, wanting to discuss weekly crises, oversensitivity to criticism, anger at other clients.

**Trainer strategy:**
- **Ignore briefly disruptive behavior** and act as if the client is cooperating (extinction schedule)
- **Use a matter-of-fact tone** to ask the client to stop, then resolutely focus on skill teaching
- **Opportunistically apply current skills**: A client getting angry can be asked to try opposite action or distress tolerance; a withdrawn client can be offered cold gel packs or paced breathing (TIP skills); a passively resistant client can be asked, "Are you being willful? When will you be willing to practice willingness?"

**Important distinction**: Skills trainers do *not* become process therapists. They do not allow skills training sessions to become focused on life crises, suicidal behaviors, or quality-of-life problems. These belong in individual therapy. When skills seem ineffective for a presenting crisis, clients should be referred to their individual therapist.

**The wind-down period** at session's end (discussed in Chapter 3) is an appropriate time to observe and comment on therapy-interfering behaviors or improvements in previous interfering patterns.

### Why This Hierarchy Matters: The Risk of Drift

Many trainers feel pulled to address clients' crises, process group dynamics, or resolve personal distress—especially in Stage 1 of DBT when clients are in acute danger. Ignoring the target hierarchy is **not doing DBT skills training**. In DBT, *what* is discussed is as important as *how* it is discussed.

If a trainer struggles to follow the hierarchy (common!), this should be brought to the DBT consultation team. The team can help clarify priorities and prevent trainer drift into individual therapy mode.

### Managing Trainer Emotions: Preventing Attack and Withdrawal

When a difficult client emerges, trainers often experience anxiety about the group and may become controlling or angry. Two risks emerge:

1. **Desire to remove the client**: Linehan notes that in 30+ years of groups, she has never kicked out a member, and she's never seen a group improve by doing so. When the difficult member leaves, someone else becomes the problem.

2. **Desire to attack**: Anger and judgmentalness can rise when control efforts fail. This is when trainers need **their own DBT skills**:
   - Observe and describe what the client is doing without judgment
   - Practice radical acceptance of corrigible but repetitively annoying clients
   - Use opposite action (matter-of-fact or light tone) when withdrawing urges arise
   - Use humor and irreverence to lower tension
   - Use DEAR MAN, GIVE, FAST to ask the client skillfully to stop distracting behaviors
   - Put problem behaviors on extinction and act as if the client is cooperating
