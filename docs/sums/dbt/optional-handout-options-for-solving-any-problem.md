## Options for Solving Any Problem

This optional teaching tool addresses a common impasse: clients who respond to every suggestion with "Yes, but..." and convince themselves (and sometimes the therapist) that nothing can be done. The core message is liberating: there are only four possible responses to pain, and the client is more resourceful than they think.

### The Four Options

**1. Solve the Problem**
End or change the problem situation directly, or find a way to escape it.
- Example: If your marriage is causing constant conflict, you could seek couples counseling to resolve the conflict, or you could leave the relationship.
- Example: If flying makes you anxious, you could pursue exposure therapy, or you could travel by other means.
- Skills that help: Problem-solving skills, walking the middle path, negotiation

**2. Feel Better About the Problem**
Change your emotional relationship to the problem—not by denying it, but by shifting perspective, finding meaning, or reducing the emotional weight.
- Example: Remind yourself that conflict is normal in marriage; it doesn't mean the relationship is broken.
- Example: Develop a strong support network outside the marriage so the conflict matters less.
- Skills that help: Emotion regulation skills, mindfulness, building positive relationships

**3. Tolerate the Problem**
Accept that the problem exists and may not change, but reduce the suffering it causes through acceptance.
- Example: You can't get divorced and you can't feel good about the conflict. But you can practice radical acceptance and willingness to live with it as it is.
- Example: You can't eliminate your flying phobia and you can't talk yourself into liking it. But you can accept it and fly anyway.
- Skills that help: Distress tolerance skills, radical acceptance, mindfulness

**4. Stay Miserable (or Make It Worse)**
Use no skills; ruminate, blame, or engage in crisis behaviors that add to the problem.
- Example: Stay in the marriage, obsess about the conflict, avoid any solution, and drink to cope.
- This is always an option—but it's the only one that requires no skill.

### Why This Teaching Works

Many clients operate from a false belief: "Either I solve this problem perfectly, or it's unsolvable and I'm stuck." This teaching collapses that black-and-white thinking. It says:
- Not every problem is solvable in the way you want
- But you *always* have options
- The skills you're learning apply to each of these options
- Your job is to choose which option(s) fit your situation and practice the corresponding skills

It also normalizes acceptance and tolerance as *active skills*, not passive resignation. Accepting a problem is not giving up; it's a skilled response that reduces suffering.

### How to Present It

1. **Introduce the concept:** "There are only four things you can do with pain. Let's map them out."
2. **Go through each option with examples** relevant to the client's life
3. **Identify which skills help with each** so the client sees the whole training as a toolbox for all four responses
4. **Ask the client:** "Which of these options is possible for your situation? What skills do you need to practice?"
5. **Return to it when the client gets stuck:** "You've been trying to convince me your situation is hopeless. Let's look at these four options again. What's actually possible here?"
