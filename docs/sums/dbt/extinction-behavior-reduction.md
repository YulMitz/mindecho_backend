## Extinction: Removing the Reinforcer to Reduce Behavior

Extinction is the reduction or elimination of a behavior by *removing the reinforcer that maintains it*. It's one of the most straightforward behavior change strategies, but it's also one where clients are most likely to fail—not because the strategy doesn't work, but because they give up in the face of the extinction burst.

### How Extinction Works

Identify what is reinforcing the unwanted behavior, then stop providing that reinforcer. Common examples:

- **Tantrums reinforced by giving in**: Stop giving in to the demand when a tantrum occurs.
- **Attention-seeking reinforced by scolding**: Ignore the behavior instead of commenting on it.
- **Demand avoidance reinforced by escape**: Don't let the person leave the task when they protest.

### The Critical Issue: The Extinction Burst

**When reinforcement is suddenly withdrawn, the behavior typically increases sharply at first.** This is called a behavioral burst, and it's a normal, expected part of extinction. If reinforcement doesn't resume during this burst, the behavior will eventually decrease—but many people give up during the burst, inadvertently teaching the person that escalation works.

**Real-world example:**
A child throws a tantrum in a supermarket to get Cocoa Puffs cereal. The parent has reinforced this many times by giving in. Now the parent decides to extinguish the behavior by no longer giving in. What happens?

- The first time: The tantrum *escalates*—the child cries harder, screams louder.
- If the parent gives in now (even "just this once"), the message sent is: "Tantrums work, but only if you escalate."
- If the parent holds firm and the tantrum eventually ends without the cereal, the child learns: "Tantrums don't work anymore."
- Over subsequent shopping trips, if the parent remains consistent, the tantrum frequency decreases.

### Preventing Intermittent Reinforcement During Extinction

This is crucial: **If you stop reinforcing a behavior most of the time, but occasionally give in, you've created an intermittent reinforcement schedule.** This makes the behavior much more persistent and harder to extinguish.

**Clinical example:**
A parent says no to candy three times in a row, then gives in on the fourth request. The child is now on a variable intermittent schedule and will persist even more strongly. The parent has created the opposite of the intended effect.

### Making Extinction Work: Pair It with Reinforcement of Alternatives

Extinction alone doesn't teach new behavior—it only stops old behavior. For extinction to be effective:

1. **Withhold the reinforcer** for the unwanted behavior
2. **Actively reinforce an alternative, desired behavior** at the same time

**Example:** Instead of just ignoring a tantrum, acknowledge the child's distress while holding the boundary ("I know you're upset, and you can stay home while I take a walk. I'll be back soon"), and then reinforce calm behavior ("I noticed you calmed down—thank you").

### The Orientation Conversation

If you're helping someone reduce another person's behavior through extinction, *explain the plan first*. Otherwise, it feels arbitrary or punitive, and the person may resent the strategy. Say something like:

"I'm going to stop responding to the tantrum because I've realized that my giving in is actually teaching you to keep having tantrums. It's not punishment—it's just that this approach isn't working for either of us, so we're trying something different."

### When Extinction Is Harder

Extinction can be very difficult if:
- The behavior has a long history of reinforcement
- The reinforcer is extremely powerful
- The person trying to extinguish the behavior has been inconsistent in the past
- The person is in a state of high emotional arousal (where behavior may feel less controllable)

In these cases, a therapist or coach can help clients prepare for and survive the extinction burst.
