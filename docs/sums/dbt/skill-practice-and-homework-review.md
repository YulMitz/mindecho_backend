## Skill Practice and Homework Review

Homework practice is not optional in DBT skills training—it is the bridge between learning a skill in session and being able to use it when it matters. The weekly homework review and practice cycle is essential to skill development and generalization.

### Why Homework Review Matters

Without regular homework review, several things happen:
- Skills taught in earlier weeks drop off clients' radar
- Inconsistent practice means inconsistent skill development
- Clients don't see the connection between practicing and real-world change
- The therapist doesn't know whether the client understands how to use the skill

The homework review is your opportunity to reinforce which skills clients are using, troubleshoot what's not working, and remind people why they're doing this.

### How to Structure Homework Review

**At the start of each session:**
1. Ask specifically about assigned practice from the previous week
2. Ask which skills clients *chose* to practice (even if not assigned)
3. Ask about successes—when did they use a skill and it helped?
4. Ask about barriers—what got in the way?

**Keep it brief but consistent.** You don't need 15 minutes per person, but you do need to ask everyone and notice patterns (e.g., "I notice nobody practiced this week—let's talk about what happened").

### Using the Diary Card

The diary card (introduced in individual therapy or in orientation) is a tool for tracking practice across all relevant skills. If clients are not in individual therapy:
- Introduce the diary card in orientation
- Have them bring it to each session or keep their own practice log
- Review the skills portion at least weekly, not just new content

**Critical:** If you only review this week's assigned homework, skills taught 3+ weeks ago disappear from practice. The diary card prevents this—it's a running log of all active skills.

### Connecting Practice to Personal Goals

Homework is more likely to happen when clients see it as *their* goal, not the therapist's requirement. When assigning practice:
- Link it explicitly to the client's stated goals from orientation
- "You said you wanted to feel less overwhelmed by conflict. Practicing opposite action when you feel the urge to yell will help you stay in the relationship without giving in completely."
- Ask the client how they plan to practice, where, and when
- Troubleshoot barriers *before* they miss the homework

### When Practice Isn't Happening

Common barriers:
- **Misunderstanding the skill.** Client doesn't see how to use it. Clarify, role-play, or simplify the assignment.
- **Competing priorities.** Other crises or commitments took time. Acknowledge this and adjust the assignment.
- **Lack of opportunity.** The skill requires a specific situation that hasn't come up. Suggest practice during lower-stakes moments or create a practice scenario.
- **Resistance or willfulness.** Client doesn't believe the skill will help or resents being told what to do. Return to the pros and cons worksheet. Discuss what would make the skill worth trying.
- **Shame about not doing it.** Client feels bad and then avoids coming to group. Normalize effort over perfection. "Practice isn't about doing it perfectly—it's about trying."

### Building Group Norms Around Practice

Consistent practice is contagious. When some group members practice regularly and others don't, the practicing members feel isolated or guilty. To build a norm of practice:
- Celebrate practice attempts publicly (without shaming non-practitioners)
- Normalize that practice is hard and imperfect
- Have group members share what made practice possible for them
- Discuss this explicitly in orientation and at the start of each new module
