## GIVE: Skills for Maintaining and Improving Relationships

GIVE skills are about how you pursue your goals in a way that leaves the other person liking you, respecting you, or at least feeling heard—even if they don't give you what you want.

### (Be) Gentle

Gentleness means approaching without harshness, disrespect, or judgment. It has four components:

**No Attacks**: Don't threaten, yell, or express anger directly. People respond better to gentleness than hostility.

**No Threats**: Avoid statements like "I'll kill myself if you leave me" or hidden threats. Tolerate "no" to requests without escalating. Stay in the conversation even when it gets painful, and exit gracefully if you need to.

*Important distinction*: Communicating a desire to leave a relationship or expressing a serious struggle (suicidal thoughts, urges to use) is not itself a threat. It becomes a threat when you frame it as *conditional on the other person's behavior*. "I'm having thoughts of suicide and I need help" is different from "If you don't come back to me, I'll kill myself."

Better framing: Take responsibility for your experience ("My suicidal urges are getting stronger") rather than placing responsibility on the other person ("You're making me feel this way").

**No Judging**: Don't use "shoulds," name-calling, or implied put-downs. Avoid guilt trips and voice tone that communicates condemnation.

**No Disrespect**: No sneering, contempt, scorn, or walking out on conversations. Remember that nonverbal communication often carries more weight than words. A client can say "I hear you" while their facial expression says "I think you're ridiculous."

### (Act) Interested

Listen to the other person's perspective, reasons, and point of view. Don't interrupt, mind-read their motives, or assume you know what they're thinking. Ask gently if you're unclear about their motivation, and actually listen to the answer.

**The skill is to "act" interested**, not necessarily "be" interested. There may be times when you want the interaction to go well but aren't genuinely interested in the topic. Deliberately choosing to appear interested is a form of relationship effectiveness.

**Why this works**: People respond to interest. They feel better about you when you give them time and space. When someone stops listening—looks away, picks up their phone, files their nails—conversation becomes painful and derails.

### Validate

Communicate that the other person's feelings, thoughts, and actions make sense given their situation or history. Validation is not agreement; you can validate someone's reason for feeling something while disagreeing with their conclusion.

**Example**: "I know that when you feel unheard, you raise your voice because you believe that's the only way to be taken seriously. I understand why you do that. At the same time, I'm asking you to try telling me what you want without yelling, because I listen better when I'm not defensive."

**Ways to validate**:
- Acknowledge their feelings and perspective with words, without judgment
- Read their nonverbal signals (body language, facial expression) and name what you see: "I can see this is really important to you" or "I know you're busy with this project"
- Notice and admit when they're right, even if it's painful for you
- Validate with action when appropriate—don't just say "I understand you need help" while standing still; actually help

**Common mistake**: Validating only with words while your actions contradict ("I know you're in pain" while keeping your foot on their foot—validation is empty without action).

### (Use an) Easy Manner

Be lighthearted. Use humor appropriately. Smile. Ease the other person along. This is the "soft sell" rather than the "hard sell."

**Why it matters**: An easy manner creates a comfortable atmosphere and signals that the conversation is safe. It conveys that the other person can relax without worrying about an explosion or emotional catastrophe.

The goal is to make the other person *like* giving you what you want, not feel bullied or guilty into it. Good interpersonal skill means inducing others to do what you want while they actually *want* to do it.

### Integration with DEAR MAN

A skilled interaction often uses both. DEAR MAN gets your objective, GIVE keeps the relationship intact. A person asking for a raise might use DEAR MAN to clearly state the request and broken-record through objections, while using GIVE to acknowledge the boss's budget constraints, validate that the company has had a tough year, and maintain warmth throughout.
