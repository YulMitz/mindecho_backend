## Checking In and Tracking Mood Across Sessions

**Purpose**: The mood check serves multiple functions at once. You gather data about progress, identify which specific symptoms are improving, and—most importantly—you reinforce the cognitive model by helping clients see that their thoughts and activities directly influence their mood.

### What to Check

Begin by asking clients how they're feeling overall compared to last week (better, worse, or about the same). Then follow up with specific questions tailored to their presenting problem. For depression, you might ask about:
- Overall sense of well-being (on a 0–10 scale)
- Energy levels
- Ability to enjoy activities
- Sleep, appetite, motivation

For other conditions, ask about symptoms relevant to their diagnosis: frequency and severity of panic attacks, time spent in rituals (OCD), binge episodes, substance use, angry outbursts, self-harm, or destructive behavior.

### Eliciting Attribution for Change

Once you've identified that mood has improved, ask clients *why* they think it improved. This is critical. You want them to recognize that positive changes in their thinking and behavior are associated with feeling better. For example:

- "Why do you think your mood improved this week?"
- If they say "I felt more hopeful," respond: "So your thought was 'Maybe therapy is working,' and that thought made you feel less depressed?"
- If they mention activities, reply: "So changing your thinking and changing your behavior is affecting your mood."

By doing this, you subtly reinforce the cognitive model—not as theory, but as their lived experience.

### Addressing Discrepancies and Difficulties

**When mood has worsened**: Ask what they think contributed. Gently suggest thinking or behavior as factors ("Could it have anything to do with your thinking, or with things you did or didn't do?") to imply clients have some control.

**When progress is external**: If clients attribute mood changes to medication or outside events, acknowledge this but probe further: "I'm sure that helped. But did you also find yourself thinking differently or doing anything different?"

**When clients struggle to express mood**: Use gentle interruption and concrete categories: "I need to know if you've generally felt better, worse, or the same. Are you more anxious? More sad? More angry?"

### Safety Screening

Always ask about suicidal ideation, hopelessness, and aggressive or homicidal impulses. These must be addressed first on the agenda if present.
