## Reframing Self-Comparisons

Clients in depression often make automatic comparisons that fuel their dysphoria. They compare their current functioning to others without depression, to their pre-disorder self, or to an idealized future self they "should" be. Each of these comparisons is unhelpful because it sets an unrealistic standard and highlights deficit rather than progress.

### The Problem with Unhelpful Comparisons

A depressed client struggling to organize their living room might think, "Other people can do this easily. Look at me—I can barely do basic stuff." Or: "I used to organize my entire apartment without thinking. Now I can barely handle this room. I'm pathetic."

These comparisons have a common feature: they ignore the fact that depression itself is a legitimate cause of reduced energy, motivation, and concentration. Comparing yourself to non-depressed people (or to yourself before depression) is like comparing yourself while sick with pneumonia to someone healthy and then feeling worse about your fatigue.

### How to Intervene

**Normalize the impact of the disorder:** Help clients see that the symptoms they're experiencing—tiredness, low energy, trouble concentrating, low motivation—are *legitimate reasons* to struggle with tasks. Frame it the same way you'd frame pneumonia: "If you had pneumonia, would you expect to do everything you normally do? Depression affects your brain chemistry and energy the same way pneumonia affects your lungs."

**Introduce the functional comparison:** Teach clients to ask themselves: "How am I doing compared to my worst point?" This is the only fair comparison because it acknowledges that they're still recovering or managing a real condition.

In session, you might say:
> "What would it look like if you compared yourself to how you were when your depression was at its worst—when you spent all day in bed or on the couch and your whole apartment was a mess? What can you do *now* that you couldn't do then?"

Most clients can identify clear progress when they make this comparison. They've managed to get out of bed more days, get ready, maybe do some activities. This builds hope and self-efficacy.

**Make it actionable:** Help the client write down two things for their Action Plan:
1. *Notice* when you're making an unhelpful comparison (to others, to your pre-illness self, to where you "should" be)
2. *Replace it* with a comparison to your worst point

**Special considerations at the lowest point:** If a client is severely depressed and can't see any progress even from their worst point, modify the approach. Remind them that you're working as a team with specific goals, and that progress will come. Frame self-therapy and goal-work as future hope rather than current achievement.
