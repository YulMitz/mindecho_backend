## Eliciting and Responding to Client Feedback

In CBT, actively seeking feedback—and responding well when you receive it—is essential to maintaining a strong working relationship and preventing premature dropout. Many clients will not volunteer concerns unless you explicitly invite them to do so.

### Why Feedback Matters

When you don't address clients' negative reactions, they will likely struggle to focus on the work of therapy and may decide not to return. Even when your alliance feels strong, regular feedback helps catch misunderstandings early. Clients often feel honored and respected when a therapist genuinely asks for their reactions.

### When and How to Elicit Feedback

**Early in treatment**, use open-ended questions to invite honest feedback:
- "What did you think about the session? Was there anything that bothered you, or that you thought I misunderstood? Is there anything you want to do differently next time?"

**After several sessions**, when clients are more comfortable giving candid answers, you can simplify:
- "What did you think of the session?"

Ask about your conceptualization, treatment plan, session structure, and specific interventions. Make it routine—not a special occasion that signals something is wrong.

### Reading Indirect Signals

Not all clients will tell you directly. Watch for:
- Nonverbal cues (eye-rolling, sighing, affect shifts)
- Indirect statements ("Maybe I'm not expressing myself clearly" when they mean you're not understanding)
- Avoidance of certain topics
- Expressions of hopelessness or skepticism about treatment

You don't need to address every minor sign. Use clinical judgment—you can overlook a teenager's first eye-roll, for example—but when a pattern emerges or you sense genuine discomfort, bring it up.

### Responding to Negative Feedback

When a client gives you negative feedback (e.g., "This isn't helping" or "You don't understand me"):

1. **Provide positive reinforcement first**: "It's good you told me that."
2. **Ask whether you've actually made a mistake**. Did you introduce a confusing worksheet? Propose an action plan that's too difficult? Misunderstand what they said? Interrupt too much? Apologize genuinely and collaborate on a solution.
3. **If you haven't made an error**, the problem likely involves the client's inaccurate cognitions. Use the cognitive model:
   - Express empathy
   - Ask for more information in the context of their beliefs
   - Seek agreement to test whether the thought is accurate
   - Gather evidence for and against the thought
   - Explore alternative explanations

### Example: Addressing a Rupture

When a client says "You don't care about me," you might respond:
- "It's good you told me. That's an interesting thought. How much do you believe it right now?"
- "What evidence do you have that I don't care? And what evidence is there on the other side—that maybe I do care?"
- "Could there be another explanation for why I mentioned [the issue]? For example, is it possible I brought it up because I want to help you build skills so you won't feel so distressed?"

This approach validates their experience while gently introducing doubt about the accuracy of their interpretation.

### Generalizing Insights Beyond Therapy

Once a client recognizes that an inaccurate thought about you (the therapist) was tested and revised, you can help them apply the same process to their relationships outside therapy. For example: "Have you had this idea about anyone else lately?" Then use the same questions to help them evaluate whether their assumption is true.

## Tailoring the Therapeutic Relationship to the Individual

While core counseling skills—warmth, empathy, genuineness, and collaboration—are essential, applying them rigidly can backfire. The degree to which you use these behaviors must be calibrated for each client based on their culture, age, gender, ethnicity, socioeconomic status, disability status, sexual orientation, and individual personality.

### The Risk of Mismatching

Some clients may experience high warmth and empathy as patronizing or manipulative, leaving them feeling suspicious or uncomfortable. Others may interpret low warmth as dislike or devaluation. Watch for clients' emotional reactions in session—they alert you to relational problems so you can adjust your presentation.

### Cultural Considerations

Clients from different cultural backgrounds have different expectations about:
- **Your role**: Some prefer you as an expert who takes charge; others expect genuine collaboration
- **Directiveness**: Some value your suggestions of alternative perspectives; others feel this contradicts their cultural beliefs and practices
- **Formality**: How you introduce yourself, address the client, maintain eye contact, and express respect vary significantly across cultures
- **Self-disclosure**: Some cultures view therapist transparency as trustworthy; others see it as unprofessional

### Your Own Cultural Lens

Recognize that your own background and culture shape your beliefs, values, and how you perceive and interact with clients. Understanding your cultural biases helps you respond with sensitivity. You may need to vary:
- How you introduce and address yourself
- Eye contact patterns
- Word choice
- How you express respect
- Amount and type of self-disclosure

### Balancing Flexibility with Authenticity

Despite significant cultural differences, any given client may respond well without you adapting your general style. Develop an individualized conceptualization and treatment plan for each person. Your goal is genuine flexibility—not performed insincerity—that honors both the client and your authentic self.

## Using Self-Disclosure in CBT

In CBT, you are not a "blank screen" as in psychoanalysis. Clients benefit from perceiving you as a warm, authentic person who genuinely wants to help them. Judicious self-disclosure can strengthen this perception significantly.

### Purpose-Driven Self-Disclosure

Self-disclosure should always have a clear therapeutic purpose:
- Strengthening the therapeutic relationship
- Normalizing the client's difficulties
- Demonstrating how CBT techniques work
- Modeling a skill or coping strategy
- Serving as a role model

Never disclose simply to meet your own needs or to make the session about you.

### What to Share and What Not to Share

**Acceptable topics** (where appropriate):
- Your age, marital status, children, grandchildren
- Your education, training, and experience
- Personal struggles you've overcome that relate to the client's current work (e.g., perfectionism, difficulty saying no)
- Mild, relatable experiences (frustration with a task, enjoying a hobby)

**Avoid sharing about**:
- Your dating life
- Alcohol or substance use
- Financial struggles
- Deep personal crises (unless long resolved and clearly relevant)
- Anything that might worry the client or shift focus to your wellbeing

### When a Client Asks About You

It's normal for clients to be curious. You can answer straightforward questions about your professional background and life. If a client continues asking personal questions beyond the therapeutic purpose, gently redirect:

"We could keep on talking about me, but then we won't have as much time to talk about what's important to you and how you can have a better week. Is it okay if we get back to you?"

For inappropriate questions, you might say: "I'm sorry not to answer your question, but I want to focus on how I can help you."

### Examples of Effective Self-Disclosure

**When a client is perfectionistic**: "I keep a sticky note on my desk that says, 'Good enough.' It helps me remember that done is better than perfect."

**When a client struggles with overcommitment**: "I have another sticky note that says, 'Just say no.' I had to work on that skill myself."

**When a client shares a positive experience**: Respond conversationally with genuine interest—ask about details, show you're listening, perhaps draw a brief parallel to your own life ("My kids loved that amusement park too").

**When a client faces a problem you've solved**: "I sometimes have that problem too. Would it help if I told you what I do? Instead of two piles, I make three..."

### Monitoring Your Client's Response

Pay attention to verbal and nonverbal reactions. Some clients—particularly those with narcissistic personality disorder—may not appreciate hearing about your life. Adjust accordingly.

### Timing Matters

Expressing your own emotional reactions (e.g., "It makes me feel sad when I hear about what your father did") should wait until you've established genuine trust. Early in treatment, "I'm so sorry that happened to you" is more appropriate. Later, when the client knows you're authentic, expressing your real sadness can deeply strengthen your bond.

Similarly, giving clients feedback about their unhelpful behaviors works best after you've established safety. When an angry client calms down, you might say: "When you get really passionate and yell, it makes it harder for us to figure out what to do about the problem." This is nonjudgmental and focused on the outcome, not their character.

## Repairing Therapeutic Ruptures

Difficulties in the therapeutic relationship don't happen randomly. Understanding why ruptures occur helps you prevent and repair them.

### Why Some Clients Are More Vulnerable to Ruptures

Clients bring their general beliefs about themselves, others, and relationships into the therapy session, along with their characteristic coping strategies.

**Clients with secure relational beliefs** typically enter treatment believing:
- "Other people are generally trustworthy and helpful"
- "Problems in relationships can usually be resolved"

These clients assume you'll understand, empathize with, and accept them. They reveal their difficulties, faults, weaknesses, and fears openly and express their preferences. It's relatively easy to form a collaborative team.

**Clients with insecure relational beliefs** often believe:
- "Other people will hurt me"
- "Problems in relationships can't be solved"

These clients feel vulnerable and guard themselves, assuming you may be critical, uncaring, manipulative, or controlling. They may resist revealing what they see as negative qualities, avoid certain topics, or try to control or dominate the session. Ruptures are more likely—and require explicit repair.

### Recognizing Ruptures: Direct and Indirect

**Direct ruptures** are easier to spot:
- "I don't think you understand what I'm saying"
- "You're treating me like everyone else"
- "This isn't helping"

**Indirect ruptures** are more subtle. The client may take responsibility themselves:
- "Maybe I'm not expressing myself clearly" (meaning: you're not understanding me)
- "I guess I'm just too hard to help" (meaning: you don't believe in me)

You'll need to ask questions to discover whether a problem genuinely exists and how much it's affecting the alliance.

### The Repair Process

**Step 1: Notice and positively reinforce.**
When a client gives feedback (direct or after you've elicited it), start with: "It's good you told me that." This reinforces that expressing concerns is safe and welcome.

**Step 2: Determine if you made an actual mistake.**
Ask yourself: "Is the client right?" Common therapist mistakes include:
- Introducing a confusing worksheet
- Offering an inappropriate suggestion
- Proposing action plan items that are too difficult
- Misunderstanding what the client said
- Being too directive or too nondirective
- Interrupting excessively

If you made a mistake, apologize genuinely (model good apologizing), acknowledge the impact, and collaborate on a solution.

**Step 3: If no mistake, use the cognitive model.**
The rupture likely reflects the client's inaccurate thoughts or beliefs. Proceed as follows:

- **Express empathy**: "I understand why that would upset you."
- **Ask for more information**: "What does it mean to you that I brought this up? What went through your mind?"
- **Assess belief strength**: "How much do you believe that thought right now?"
- **Explore evidence**: "What evidence do you have for that thought? What evidence is there on the other side?"
- **Generate alternative explanations**: "Could there be another reason I [did this thing]? For example, is it possible I did it because [positive intent]?"
- **Test the thought**: "Would it be helpful to look for evidence that might support or disprove this idea?"

### Example: Client Belief "You Don't Care About Me"

Client: "You don't care about me."

Therapist: "It's good you told me. That's an interesting thought. How much do you believe it?"

Client: "100%."

Therapist: "What makes you think I don't care? What evidence do you have?"

Client: "Well, you brought up those phone calls and they bothered you."

Therapist: "Could there be another explanation? Is it possible I brought up the phone calls because I care about helping you feel less distressed? Because I want to teach you skills so you won't have to rely on calling in crisis?"

Client: "I guess... maybe."

Therapist: "Let me ask: Do I start sessions on time? Do I seem glad to see you? Do I seem sorry when you're upset? Do I work hard to help you? These are signs I do care."

This approach validates the client's experience while gently testing the accuracy of their interpretation.

### Prevent Future Ruptures

After repairing a rupture, ask the client:
- "Are there other things you think I don't understand?"
- "If I make another mistake, do you think you could let me know right away?"

This invitation makes it safe to raise concerns early, preventing small misunderstandings from festering.

### Generalizing the Insight

Once a client recognizes that a thought about you was inaccurate (tested and revised), help them apply the same process to other relationships:

"Have you had this idea about anyone else lately? Should we ask the same questions about whether that thought is accurate?"

This moves the therapeutic work from the session to the client's real-world relationships.
