## Identifying Automatic Thoughts

**What They Are**: Automatic thoughts are the rapid, involuntary stream of interpretations that run through our minds. They're not the event itself, but our interpretation of it. Most people are barely aware of them—we notice the emotion or behavior that follows instead. In CBT, learning to catch and examine these thoughts is the first step toward change.

### Key Characteristics

- **Usually brief and verbal**, though sometimes images
- **Mostly unnoticed** until trained to look for them
- **Logically connected to emotion**: If you think "I'm failing," you feel sad. If you think "What if something bad happens?", you feel anxious.
- **Often distort reality**: People in distress see situations as more negative than they are
- **Clients are usually more aware of emotion or behavior** than the thought that triggered it

For example: Abe avoids calling friends because he thinks "They might be critical of me for not working." He notices the avoidance more than the thought. Maria feels sad and doesn't immediately know why until you ask—then discovers she was thinking "I'll never have a life like my sister's."

### When to Ask

Ask about automatic thoughts when:
- A client describes a problematic situation or emotion from the past week
- They express difficulty with a goal they want to pursue in the coming week
- You notice a sudden shift in their affect or behavior *during the session itself*
- They describe avoidance, a behavioral lapse, or symptoms worsening

The basic questions are simple:
- "What [is/was/will be] going through your mind?"
- "What [are/were/will you be] thinking?"
- "What was the worst part about that for you?" (often surfaces the core thought)

### How to Elicit Thoroughly

Don't stop after the first automatic thought. Ask follow-up questions:

- **"What else went through your mind?"** There's usually a stream of thoughts, each connected to different emotions.
- **"Did you feel any other emotion besides sadness?"** If yes, there's usually another thought: "You felt anxious too—what was going through your mind that made you feel anxious?"
- **"What were you thinking right before you did that?"** This surfaces the thought that triggered behavior.

### Forms Thoughts Take

Automatic thoughts may come as:
- **Direct statements**: "I can't do anything right"
- **Images or flashbacks**: A memory of failure
- **Questions**: "What if I mess this up?"
- **Embedded in description**: "I just knew she was upset with me" (the automatic thought is "She's upset with me")
- **Short phrases**: "So lazy," "Total failure," "Nobody likes me"

Don't insist on formal phrasing. If a client says "I just knew they were judging me," that's the automatic thought.

### Explaining to Clients

When introducing automatic thoughts, use their own example:

"You just identified what we call an automatic thought—'I'll never be able to have a life like hers.' Everyone has these. They just pop into our heads, especially when we're stressed or down. We're usually more aware of the emotion—in this case, you felt sad—than the thought itself. When people are depressed, these thoughts often aren't true or not completely true. But we react as if they are. That's what we're going to work on."

Then draw the cognitive model:
- Situation: Thinking about sister
- Automatic thought: "I'll never have a life like hers"
- Emotion: Sadness

This makes the connection concrete and teachable.

### Extended Scenarios

Sometimes clients experience a chain of thoughts and reactions, especially before impulsive behavior (binge eating, drinking, self-harm). Map the whole sequence:
1. Initial trigger/situation
2. First automatic thought → emotion
3. Second situation (realization, physical sensation)
4. Second automatic thought → additional emotion
5. Third situation and thoughts → behavior

Once mapped, show the client all the places where they can *intervene* before the final unhelpful behavior. This creates hope: "We have multiple places where you can catch this and change course."

### Common Difficulties and Solutions

**Client can't identify a thought**: Ask more concrete questions. "What was the worst part about that?" "What does it mean about you?" "What were you afraid would happen?"

**Client describes multiple emotions but won't name thoughts**: Note the emotions and ask about each: "You felt sad and angry. These usually come from different thoughts. What thought made you sad? What made you angry?"

**Client is aware of behavior but not the thought**: Use the behavior as a clue: "You avoided calling them. Usually when we avoid something, there's a thought about what might happen. What were you thinking might happen?"

**Thought seems obvious but client resists**: Don't impose. Keep asking gently: "Help me understand what you were thinking."
