## Posing a Hypothesis About the Problem

Once you've identified and discussed a client's core belief with them, it's useful to offer them a two-part hypothesis about what the actual problem might be. This framing is collaborative and opens space for exploration.

### The Two Possibilities

**Possibility 1:** The belief is true, and the client actually lacks competence (or is actually unlovable, etc.). If this is the case, your work together will focus on building skills, gathering evidence of capability, and developing the actual competence.

**Possibility 2:** The belief is not true—or not completely true. The client has a powerful, distorted belief that prevents them from recognizing their actual abilities or from attempting things they could do well. The depression strengthens this false belief so much that it becomes a self-fulfilling prophecy: they don't try because they "know" they'll fail.

### Why This Matters

Often, the real problem is not actual incompetence but rather a strong belief in incompetence that prevents the person from *trying*. Because they believe they'll fail, they avoid situations, don't gather evidence of success, and miss opportunities to disconfirm the belief.

### Clinical Delivery

Present this hypothesis in a curious, collaborative tone: "I'm wondering if the problem is that you really are incompetent, or if the problem is that you have a belief that you're incompetent when you're actually not—and that belief is so strong it keeps you from even finding out whether you could do something well."

This gentle reframing helps clients see there's another possibility worth exploring, which naturally motivates the work of belief change.
