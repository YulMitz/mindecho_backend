## Contextualizing Māori Mental Health: The Impact of Colonization

A foundational aspect of providing culturally competent therapy to Māori clients is understanding the impact of New Zealand's colonial history on their collective and individual wellbeing. The link between this history and current Māori mental health problems is well-documented and provides essential context for case conceptualization.

The foundational event of modern New Zealand was the signing of the Treaty of Waitangi in 1840 between the British Crown and Māori. While the Treaty promised Māori the rights of British citizens and undisturbed possession of their lands, what followed was a period of systematic colonization throughout the late nineteenth and early twentieth centuries. This era was marked by substantial loss of Māori land, a rapid deterioration in Māori health, and a significant population decline. These historical trends are consistent with the experiences of other indigenous populations worldwide who have been impacted by European colonization.

This history is not merely a distant event; its consequences are directly linked to the higher rates of mental health disorders (including depression, anxiety, and substance use) observed in the Māori population today. When working with a Māori client, it is crucial to recognize that their presenting issues may be influenced by this legacy of historical trauma, loss, and systemic disadvantage. A purely individualistic CBT model that ignores this context risks being invalidating and ineffective. Acknowledging these historical factors helps the therapist to frame the client's struggles in a way that is de-pathologizing and recognizes the role of external, systemic forces.
