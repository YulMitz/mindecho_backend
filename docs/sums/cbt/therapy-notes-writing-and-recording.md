## Writing and Recording Therapy Notes

Once you've evaluated an important automatic thought with a client in session, ask them to summarize what you discovered together. Pose questions like: "What do you want to remind yourself of this week if that thought comes up again?" or "Can you summarize what's important about what we just talked through?" This verbal summary step is essential—it lets you catch confusion, add missing nuances, and ensure the response is genuinely theirs before it gets written down.

Ask the client whether they'd prefer to write the notes themselves or have you write them. Either way, the notes should capture:
- The triggering thought (e.g., "I can't do it")
- The evidence against it or the more realistic thinking (e.g., "My concentration was good enough to fill out job applications before; I can at least start")
- Action steps if relevant (e.g., "I could ask my son for help")
- Any context that helps (e.g., "Getting started is usually the hardest part")

**Format matters.** Short, plain-language notes work best—no jargon, no lengthy paragraphs. Many clients find index cards, a therapy notebook, or even a smartphone note easier to access throughout the week than long written sheets.

**Recording is an alternative.** Some clients don't like reading, or they respond better to hearing your voice or their own voice replaying the session insight. You can record audio notes at the end of a session. However, recording the entire session is often counterproductive; clients rarely listen to full recordings repeatedly. Instead, record just the key responses and reminders.

**Frequency of use matters most.** Clients who read their therapy notes every morning and pull them out during the day as needed tend to integrate the new thinking much faster than those who only read them when distressed. Morning reading is rehearsal—it primes the brain before situations arise.

**Practical motivation tips:** If clients say they don't have time to read notes, ask them to read aloud in session while you time them. Most therapy notes take only 20–60 seconds. Linking note-reading to their stated values ("You want to feel less stuck at work—this reminder helps you get started on tasks") strengthens compliance. Also ask about obstacles: Do they forget where they put the notes? Help them choose a visible, accessible location.

Keep a copy of each client's therapy notes in your file. You'll use them to plan the next session, to reinforce progress, and to provide replacements if clients lose theirs. This simple practice dramatically improves the therapeutic work that happens between sessions.
