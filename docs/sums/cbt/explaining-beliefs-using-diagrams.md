## Explaining Beliefs Using a Diagram

After you've explored a client's core belief, its origins, and how it currently manifests, drawing a simple diagram helps consolidate this understanding and makes the formulation tangible and memorable.

### Basic Structure

A typical belief diagram shows:
1. **Childhood/Historical experiences** (the origins)
2. **Core belief developed** (the conclusion the client drew)
3. **Current triggering event** (what activates the belief now)
4. **Automatic thought** (the specific thought in the moment)
5. **Emotion and behavior** (the client's response)

### Drawing It Together

Rather than explaining the whole model at once, build the diagram collaboratively:
- Start with the historical origin: "It sounds like when your mother yelled at you about the messy house, you felt incompetent. Is that right?"
- Add the core belief: "So you developed the idea, 'I'm incompetent.'"
- Move to the present: "Now, when you see bills on the table and haven't paid them, what happens?"
- Capture the automatic thought: "And you think, 'I can't believe I haven't paid them yet,' which means to you...?"
- Complete the connection: "That you're incompetent. So now you can see why you had that automatic thought. The core belief underneath it comes from way back."

### Why Diagrams Help

Seeing the formulation drawn out helps clients:
- Understand the logic of their responses (not random or "crazy")
- See the connection between past and present
- Recognize that changing the belief or its activation is possible
- Refer back to the diagram throughout treatment
- Share the diagram with others to help them understand the client's struggles

### Referencing Throughout Treatment

Keep the diagram visible during sessions. As new automatic thoughts or situations arise, you can point to the diagram: "See, that thought is coming from the same core belief we identified. That's the belief trying to interpret this situation."
