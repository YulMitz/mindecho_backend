## Inducing Positive Imagery

While most therapists focus on identifying and modifying negative images, inducing positive imagery is equally important and often underused. Mental imagery affects emotion more powerfully than verbal thought alone, and deliberately creating positive images can increase confidence, motivation, and positive emotion.

### Five Approaches to Positive Imagery

**1. Focusing on Positive Memories of Competence**

Help clients recall specific memories in which they solved problems, coped with difficulty, or succeeded despite challenges. The goal is to make the memory vivid and emotionally present, not just a verbal recall.

In session, guide them to see the memory in detail: What do you see? What sensations do you notice? How are you feeling in that moment? The sensory richness matters—temperature, sounds, physical sensations all make the image more real and emotionally impactful.

For example, a depressed client who lost confidence might recall a summer job in high school where they learned a difficult skill through persistence. Guide them to re-experience that hot day, the physical effort, and the growing competence. Then help them connect: "You're still that same person who can do hard things. You have depression, but you haven't given up. You do hard things every day now—getting up, staying active, volunteering."

This technique is powerful because it's not about empty reassurance; it's about reconnecting with lived experience of competence.

**2. Rehearsing Adaptive Coping Techniques in Imagination**

When clients are anxious about an upcoming situation, have them imagine using their coping skills in detail. This rehearsal usually boosts confidence and motivation to actually use the skills during the real event.

Walk through a realistic scenario step by step. When will anxiety first appear? What will you do? What will you say to yourself? What coping technique will you use? Then imagine moving through the situation, including realistic ups and downs, until you've gotten through it.

The key is *realistic* coping, not perfect outcomes. The client should imagine some anxiety remaining, some difficulty, but managing it anyway. This builds true confidence—not false confidence that they won't be anxious, but confidence that they can handle anxiety.

**3. Distancing Through Time**

When clients feel hopeless or trapped, have them imagine themselves at some point in the future (a year from now, 5 years from now) when they've gotten through the current difficulty. The image should be concrete and sensory: Where do you wake up? When? What are you doing? What does your day look like?

This technique combats the depressive sense that "this will always be like this." It creates hope and perspective. Even a vague image of "things are better" can shift mood.

**4. Substituting Positive Images**

When a client reports a recurring distressing image, teach them to deliberately replace it with a pleasant scene. Some clients find it helpful to imagine the distressing image as a TV channel they can change. Practice switching from the distressing image to a detailed pleasant memory or imagined scene (a beach, forest, cherished memory).

This technique requires practice to be effective, and it works best for occasional spontaneous images, not intrusive thought patterns (use mindfulness for those).

**5. Focusing on Positive Aspects of a Feared Situation**

Rather than only imagining worst-case outcomes, have clients identify and vividly imagine positive or meaningful aspects of an upcoming situation. A client feared a Cesarean section but was able to imagine the excitement on her partner's face, the kindness of the medical team, and holding her newborn. This doesn't deny the fear but provides emotional balance.

### How to Guide Imagery Effectively

- **Use sensory detail.** Ask about sights, sounds, physical sensations, temperature, emotions. The more vivid, the more emotionally impactful.
- **Go slowly.** Don't rush through the image. Pause and let the client immerse themselves.
- **Connect back to goals.** After positive imagery, help the client see how it relates to their values and what they can do this week to move toward it.
- **Record it.** Have clients write down what they imagined so they can revisit it during the week, especially before a challenging situation.
