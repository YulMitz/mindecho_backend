## Identifying Values and Aspirations

Values are long-standing beliefs about what is most important in life. They shape choices and behavior. When depressed clients perceive they're not living up to their values, distress deepens. The therapeutic task is to help them reconnect with what truly matters, then build a vision of a future aligned with those values.

### Eliciting Values

Ask in a conversational tone: "What's really important to you in life?" or "What used to be really important to you before depression set in?"

If the client draws a blank or says "nothing," offer concrete domains to explore:
- **Relationships**: family, friends, intimate partnerships
- **Productivity**: work, managing the home
- **Health**: fitness, eating well, sleep, substance use
- **Self-improvement**: education, skills, appearance, self-control
- **Community**: local or broader civic engagement
- **Spirituality**: religious practice or spiritual meaning
- **Recreation**: hobbies, sports, entertainment
- **Creativity and nature**
- **Relaxation and rest**

Many depressed clients will identify at least some values even when they feel they've lost everything. Common ones include family, work, helping others, health, and friendships.

### Eliciting Aspirations

Once values are named, ask what the client wants for their life:
- "What do you want for your life?"
- "What are your hopes for the future?"
- "What do you want your future to look like?"
- "When you were growing up, what did you hope your life would be like?"

Aspirations are more specific than values—they're the concrete ways the client wants to live out what matters. An aspiration might be "have a good job," "better relationships with my family," "feel useful and helpful," or "take care of myself better."

### Drawing Conclusions About Achieving Aspirations

It's not enough to identify aspirations—help the client feel the meaning of achieving them. Ask:
- "What would be especially good about achieving this?"
- "How would you feel about yourself? What would it say about you?"
- "How might other people view you or treat you differently?"
- "What would it suggest about your future?"
- "How would you feel emotionally if all this came true? Can you get that feeling right now?"

These questions help the client connect aspirations to identity, relationships, and emotional wellbeing. This is powerful motivational work.

### Creating an Image of Achievement

Use guided imagery to make aspirations tangible and emotionally real. Ask the client to imagine a day in the future (e.g., a year from now) when they've recovered and their aspirations have come true. Guide them through sensory details:
- Where do they wake up? What does the room look like?
- How are they feeling as they wake up?
- What are they thinking about?
- What do they do next?

As the client describes this future, their affect often brightens. This brief emotional experience—feeling what recovery could feel like—can be deeply motivating for the work ahead. The image becomes an anchor that the client can return to when treatment feels difficult.
