## Setting an Initial Agenda

**Purpose**: The agenda identifies what the client wants to accomplish in this session, helps you prioritize when time is limited, and ensures the client feels heard and respected in the decision-making process.

### Basic Steps

1. **Ask the client's goals**: "What would you like to work on today?" or "What's your goal for this session?" Listen for the first response, but don't stop there.

2. **Label, don't elaborate**: When the client begins describing an issue, gently interrupt and name it briefly rather than allowing a long description. This keeps them from losing track of what they actually want to prioritize. For example:
   - Client: "Well, I've been trying to clean up my apartment but there's so much stuff and some of it is really hard to throw away because…"
   - Therapist: "Okay [writing], 'Cleaning hard stuff in the apartment.' Anything else?"

3. **Check for additional items**: Continue asking "Anything else?" or "Is there anything coming up this week that's important?" This may surface items they didn't initially mention—like a difficult phone call or upcoming obligation.

4. **Review notes from last session**: Check your records for items you didn't finish. Probe gently: "We didn't get to talk about volunteering—should that be on today's agenda?"

5. **Propose your own topics**: If relevant, suggest things you think should be addressed: "I'd like to teach you more about evaluating your thoughts, if that's okay."

6. **Prioritize together**: Near the end of agenda-setting, list all items and ask which one the client wants to start with. Agree on time allocation: "Should we spend about 50–50 on these two items?"

### Why Gentle Interruption Matters

Without interruption, clients may:
- Spend 10 minutes describing one issue in detail and never get to their real priority
- Feel so heard in talking that they don't mentally prepare to reflect on what they actually want to tackle
- Miss the chance to problem-solve multiple issues in one session

Gentle interruption—done respectfully—actually gives clients *more* control, not less, because they decide what's most important rather than just following the path of what comes to mind.

### Handling Common Difficulties

**Client rambles or provides excessive detail**: Interrupt kindly with a summary. "It sounds like you have two goals: reconnecting with your dad and handling work stress. Which one feels most important?" This refocuses without making them feel cut off.

**Client doesn't know what to put on the agenda**: Offer options:
- "Is there something on your goal list [from earlier sessions] you'd like to work on?"
- "How would you like the next few days to be better?"
- "When was the past week most difficult for you?"

**Client feels hopeless and overwhelmed**: Avoid trying to tackle everything. Frame it positively: "I know you have real problems. If we could work on just one thing today that would help most, what would it be?" Make a forced choice to orient them toward problem-solving.

**Client becomes irritated with the structure**: If they say "You're interrupting me too much," stop and apologize. Negotiate: "How about you tell me what's most important for me to know for the next 10 minutes, and I won't interrupt. Then I'll summarize so I understand correctly." Show you're willing to adapt.
