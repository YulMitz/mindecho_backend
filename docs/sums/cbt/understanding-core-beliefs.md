## Understanding Core Beliefs in Depression

Core beliefs are deeply held ideas about oneself that form the foundation of a client's worldview. Unlike automatic thoughts, which are fleeting and situational, core beliefs are more stable and pervasive—yet they become dramatically more powerful and distorted when a client is in a depressed or anxious mood state.

### What Makes Core Beliefs Different

Core beliefs may not be believed strongly when the client is well, but during depression they become almost completely believable, even when substantial evidence contradicts them. A client might reasonably believe "I'm reasonably competent" during stable periods, but when depression activates, the belief "I'm incompetent" takes over their entire perception.

### The Screen Metaphor

A useful way to explain this to clients is the screen metaphor: imagine a screen around the person's head with their negative belief written on it repeatedly. Information that fits the belief passes straight through the screen and registers consciously. But information that contradicts the belief doesn't fit through the screen—the person either doesn't notice it or actively discounts it.

For example, a client might fix their grandson's toy but then think, "It took me a long time to figure it out," rather than concluding, "I figured it out—that shows competence." The screen filtered out the success.

### Core Belief Categories

Core beliefs typically fall into three domains:
- **Helpless/incompetent** ("I can't do anything right")
- **Unlovable** ("No one would want to be close to me")
- **Worthless** ("I have no value")

### When Core Beliefs Originated

Understanding when and how a core belief developed helps clients see it has a history and context. Ask about childhood experiences, early jobs, or other formative times when the belief felt true. Often, the belief originated in a specific painful period (harsh parenting, early failure, rejection) but has persisted even though the client's actual competence or lovability changed.

### Working with Core Beliefs in Session

When you observe a client's automatic thoughts, emotional reactions, and behavioral patterns, you can form a hypothesis about their underlying core belief. The goal is to educate clients about how these beliefs work—that they are not facts, but rather long-held patterns that become stronger during depression. This normalization reduces shame and opens space for change work.
