## Collaborative Action Plan Setting

Rather than unilaterally assigning homework, invite client input and agreement at every step. This collaborative approach builds ownership and increases follow-through.

**How to frame it:** Instead of "You should try calling your boss," say: "What would you think about asking your boss to change your work schedule?" or "Do you want to practice this technique this week?" Acknowledge the rationale explicitly: "I think if you get in the shower as soon as you get up, you'll show yourself you can take more control of your day. What do you think? Is this something you want to try?"

**Why it matters:** Depressed or anxious clients often lack energy and motivation. Cognitive and executive function may be impaired. If you assign something too difficult without their buy-in, they'll likely fail, reinforcing beliefs about their incompetence. Collaborative agreement increases commitment.

**Key principle—err on the easy side:** Break large tasks into manageable parts. Instead of "Complete a daily thought record," suggest "Spend 10 minutes doing one thought record" or "Read one chapter of this book." Start small to build momentum and self-efficacy.

**Provide explicit instructions:** Help clients decide when, where, how long, and with whom they'll do the task. Vague assignments fail. Specific timing anchors the plan: "Go to the bank right after our session, then spend 15 minutes filling out the application at home."
