## Identifying Spontaneous Negative Images

Many clients experience automatic thoughts not as words but as mental pictures, sensory impressions, or felt sense in the body. A client might not report "My husband will blame me" as words; instead, they'll have a vivid image of his angry face and harsh tone. These images often cause more distress than verbal thoughts alone, yet many therapists don't systematically identify or address them.

### How Images Present

Images can be:
- **Visual:** A clear mental picture (e.g., seeing yourself sitting alone, feeling sad; seeing a loved one in a hospital bed)
- **Sensory:** A tone of voice, a facial expression, a smell that triggers distress
- **Somatic:** A feeling in the body (heaviness, tightness, trembling) associated with an anticipated outcome

Importantly, images can flash through awareness in milliseconds and clients may not consciously register them. Yet these brief images significantly influence emotion.

### Teaching Clients About Images

Use multiple words to help clients identify what you mean: mental picture, daydream, fantasy, imagining, movie in your mind, visualization, memory. If one word doesn't land, try others.

You might say: "As you were describing walking into that situation, I got a picture in my head of what it would look like. Have *you* been imagining what will happen?"

### Persistent Questioning Without Pressure

Many clients need multiple attempts to identify and report images. Common responses to "Did you imagine what would happen?" include:
- "I'm not sure."
- "I don't think so."
- "I don't know what you mean."

Rather than giving up, ask more specifically:
- "Where would this happen? In the living room or kitchen?"
- "Can you picture it now? What do you see?"
- "What does her face look like when she says that?"

Use your own image as a cue: "As you were describing this, I pictured [scene]. Were you imagining something like that?"

### Normalizing Distressing Images

Some clients identify images but hesitate to share them because the images are graphic or seem "strange." Normalize the experience:

> "Lots of people have visual images either instead of or along with their thoughts. Usually we don't realize it. Sometimes images seem pretty strange, but actually it's common to have all kinds of images—sad, scary, even violent ones. The only problem is if you think you're strange for having an image."

### Clinical Tips

- If you form a visual image yourself as the client describes a situation, use that as a cue to probe: "I'm getting a picture of this. Are you?"
- Don't assume a client won't report images. Some images are graphic or distressing, and clients may need permission and reassurance.
- A few clients initially identify images but don't report them due to shame or fear you'll see them as disturbed. Persistent, gentle inquiry with strong normalization helps.
