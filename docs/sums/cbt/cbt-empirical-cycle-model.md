## The Empirical Cycle: CBT as a Scientific Process

A characteristic of Dutch CBT practice is the conceptualization of the therapeutic process as an "empirical cycle," mirroring the scientific method. This approach views therapy not as the simple application of techniques or protocols, but as an integrated, systematic process of hypothesis testing tailored to the individual client. The core of this model is the "holistic theory" (akin to a case conceptualization) and functional analyses, which together form a personalized understanding of the client's problems and their maintaining factors.

This therapeutic cycle consists of five distinct steps:

1.  **Defining the Problem:** Collaboratively establish a clear, concrete definition of the client's presenting issues.
2.  **Formulating a Holistic Theory and Functional Analyses:** Develop a comprehensive case conceptualization ("holistic theory") that explains the development and maintenance of the client's problems. This includes detailed functional analyses of specific behaviors, emotions, and cognitions. This theory is treated as a set of predictions or hypotheses to be tested.
3.  **Defining a Treatment Plan:** Based on the holistic theory, create a structured treatment plan. The interventions chosen are directly derived from the hypotheses about what is maintaining the problem.
4.  **Applying Therapeutic Interventions:** Implement the planned interventions. Crucially, these interventions are framed as experiments designed to test the validity of the holistic theory and functional analyses.
5.  **Evaluating Effects:** Systematically evaluate the outcomes of the interventions. This evaluation provides feedback that either confirms or disconfirms the initial hypotheses, leading to a refinement of the holistic theory and subsequent treatment steps.

This model provides a powerful framework for highly individualized therapy. While standardized, evidence-based protocols are also widely used, the empirical cycle ensures that even protocol-based work can be adapted and personalized. It positions the therapist and client as collaborative researchers, working together to understand and solve the client's problems in a structured, empirical way.
