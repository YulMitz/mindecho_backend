## Using Thought Record Worksheets

After you've helped a client evaluate an important automatic thought verbally and they feel better, you can introduce a worksheet as a way to organize their thinking at home. Two main options exist: the **Testing Your Thoughts Worksheet** (simpler, more readable format with 13 questions) and the **Thought Record** (more comprehensive, sometimes taught in stages).

**Preparation is key.** Do not pull out a worksheet immediately after identifying an automatic thought. If the verbal evaluation hasn't been effective, clients will assume the worksheet won't help either and may abandon it. Instead, work through the Socratic questions first, let the client experience relief, and *then* say, "I'd like to show you a way to organize this same work when you're at home."

**Introducing the worksheet:** Walk through the form together. Explain that it's just a written version of what you just did. Point out the helpful reminders at the top (e.g., "Thoughts are ideas, not necessarily truths"; "It's worth doing if your mood improves 10%"; "Spelling and grammar don't count"). Make it clear that *not every question applies to every thought*—that's fine. The goal is progress, not perfection.

**Practice in session first.** Before assigning it as homework, have the client complete one worksheet with you watching and guiding. This is not optional. Seeing how they fill it out tells you whether they understand the questions and whether their responses are superficial, confused, or robust. If responses are weak, coach them toward deeper thinking.

**Common pitfalls:** Clients sometimes feel overwhelmed by the number of questions, rush through answers, or produce responses that are too brief ("Yeah, I guess so"). If you notice shallow responses, pause and ask: "Can you say more about that?" or "Does that answer really address the worry, or is it just a quick fix?" Sometimes a worksheet isn't the right next step—some clients do better with simpler two-column formats (automatic thought and response) or verbal rehearsal alone.

**Modifying the approach:** For clients with lower literacy, reading difficulties, or low motivation, worksheets may not be the right tool. A simpler format (two columns: thought and response) or audio reminders may work better. If a client completes a worksheet and reports it didn't help much, this is important diagnostic information—not a failure. It may mean:
- The thought they evaluated wasn't their most distressing thought
- The thought is a core belief (needs deeper work)
- Their evaluation was superficial (coach them on depth)
- They need a different technique altogether (problem-solving, acceptance, behavioral change)

**When worksheets aren't enough:** If a client struggles with a worksheet or finds it unhelpful, don't shame them or push harder. Instead, predict obstacles collaboratively: "What do you think might get in your way?" Help them plan responses if they get frustrated ("Would it help to remember this is a skill you're learning?"). You can also make the homework a "no-lose" scenario: Either they complete it successfully, or they jot down any difficult thoughts so you can work on them together at the next session.
