## Core Beliefs: The Foundation of Interpretation

Core beliefs are the deepest, most fundamental level of cognition. They are central, enduring ideas about oneself, other people, and the world—often so fundamental that clients rarely articulate them, even to themselves. Individuals regard these beliefs as absolute truths: just the way things "are."

### How Core Beliefs Develop

People develop core beliefs from childhood onward as they attempt to make sense of their environment. Interactions with family, peers, and the wider world—filtered through genetic predisposition—lead to certain understandings that may or may not be accurate or adaptive. These beliefs then organize how the person interprets future experiences.

**Important**: Dysfunctional beliefs can be unlearned, and more reality-based, functional beliefs can be developed and strengthened through treatment.

### Adaptive vs. Dysfunctional Core Beliefs

#### Adaptive Beliefs

People who have had stable, generally safe childhoods and positive relationships tend to hold realistically positive, flexible core beliefs:

- **About the self**: "I'm reasonably competent, effective, likeable, and worthwhile."
- **About others**: "Most people are benign or neutral; some are potentially hurtful, but I can reasonably protect myself."
- **About the world**: "It's a mixture of predictability and unpredictability, safety and danger—but I can cope with most things."
- **About the future**: "It will have positive, neutral, and negative experiences. I can cope, sometimes with help from others, and I'll be okay in the end."

Well-adjusted individuals may have *latent* negative beliefs that become temporarily activated during stress or setback, but they typically revert to adaptive beliefs after a short period.

#### Dysfunctional Core Beliefs

Negative core beliefs typically fall into three categories:

1. **Helplessness**: "I'm incompetent, ineffective, helpless, unable to cope, or inferior."
2. **Unlovability**: "I'm unlovable, unlikeable, undesirable, or bound to be rejected and alone."
3. **Worthlessness**: "I'm immoral, dangerous, or don't deserve to live."

A client may hold beliefs in one, two, or all three categories. When these beliefs become activated (often by relevant stressors or vulnerabilities), they become extreme, unrealistic, and highly maladaptive.

### How Beliefs Maintain Themselves: Biased Information Processing

Once a core belief is active, the mind automatically processes information in a way that confirms it. This is not conscious distortion; it happens automatically.

#### The Schema as Filter

Think of a core belief as a mental structure (a schema) with a specific opening. Information that matches the shape of that opening is immediately processed and strengthens the belief. But information that doesn't fit is either:

1. **Discounted** ("Yes, I did well in that situation, but that's only because the client was eager to please me")
2. **Not noticed** at all—it "bounces off" the schema

**Example**: A client with the core belief "I'm incompetent" has a difficult time reading a complex text. She interprets this as proof of stupidity. When she later completes a difficult task successfully, she discounts it ("It took me so long" or "The task was easier than I thought"). Positive evidence simply doesn't register as relevant to her belief. Over time, the belief becomes stronger and stronger.

#### The Maintenance Cycle

This biased processing helps explain why clients' negative beliefs persist despite evidence to the contrary. Without intervention, the belief system becomes self-reinforcing.

### Intermediate Beliefs: Rules, Attitudes, and Assumptions

Between core beliefs and automatic thoughts sits a middle layer: intermediate beliefs. These consist of attitudes, rules, and conditional assumptions that arise from core beliefs and guide behavior.

- **Attitudes**: "It's terrible to fail."
- **Rules**: "I should give up if a challenge seems too great."
- **Assumptions**: "If I try something difficult, I'll fail. If I avoid it, I'll be okay."

These intermediate beliefs often reflect the client's values. They may also serve as coping strategies—ways the person tries to manage or prevent the activation of their core belief. For example, someone who believes "I'm incompetent" might adopt the rule "I should never ask for help" to avoid revealing their incompetence.

### The Belief Hierarchy

The relationship flows from deep to surface:

**Core beliefs → Intermediate beliefs (rules, attitudes, assumptions) → Situation → Automatic thoughts → Emotional, behavioral, physiological reaction**

Understanding this hierarchy helps explain why changing a single automatic thought may not be sufficient for lasting change. Lasting progress often requires identifying and modifying the core beliefs and intermediate beliefs that generate the automatic thoughts.
