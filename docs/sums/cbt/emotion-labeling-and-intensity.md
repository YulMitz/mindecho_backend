## Emotion Labeling and Intensity Rating

Clients often have limited emotional vocabulary and may mislabel their feelings or confuse thoughts with emotions. Your role is to help them accurately identify what they're feeling and quantify its intensity—both to understand their experience better and to measure progress in treatment.

### Why Emotion Labeling Matters

Many clients, especially those with depression or anxiety, have an impoverished vocabulary for emotions. They may report feeling "bad" or "upset" without further differentiation. Yet sadness, anxiety, and anger call for different therapeutic responses. Additionally, when clients can name their emotions precisely, they gain some distance from them and feel more understood.

### Building Emotional Vocabulary

**Offer multiple-choice options.** Instead of asking the open-ended "How did you feel?", try "Were you feeling happy, sad, anxious, angry...?" This jogs their recall.

**Provide lists.** Create or give clients a list of positive and negative emotions. The positive list might include: accepted, adventurous, affectionate, agreeable, amazed, amused, appreciative, awesome, benevolent, blessed, bold, calm, capable, centered, cheerful, confident, content, creative, curious, delighted, dynamic, eager, elated, empowered, energized, enthusiastic, excited, fortunate, free, friendly, fulfilled, generous, grateful, happy, helpful, hopeful, insightful, inspired, intelligent, interested, joyful, kindly, light, love, motivated, open, optimistic, passionate, peaceful, playful, pleasantly surprised, pleased, proud, reassured, relieved, resilient, respected, reverent, safe, satisfied, secure, serene, sincere, stimulated, supported, tender, thankful, thrilled, tranquil, valuable, vibrant, virtuous, vital, wise, worthy, youthful, zany.

The negative list might include: anxious, worried, fearful, scared, tense, afraid, suspicious, panicky; sad, down, lonely, unhappy, depressed; ashamed, embarrassed, humiliated; disappointed, discouraged, in despair; jealous, envious; guilty; angry, mad, irritated, annoyed, frustrated, misunderstood, resentful, wronged.

**Create an emotion chart.** Have the client list situations in which they felt each key emotion (anger, sadness, anxiety). This exercise builds their ability to differentiate and recognize emotions in the future.

### Rating Intensity

Quantifying emotion helps you and the client track whether interventions are working and prevents premature moving forward.

**Use a 0–10 scale.** Ask: "If 10 is the saddest you've ever felt and 0 is not sad at all, how sad did you feel?" Most clients can work with this.

**Offer alternatives if numbers don't fit.** Some clients balk at numerical scales. Offer descriptive labels: "mild," "moderate," or "intense." Or draw a visual scale with anchors (A little — A medium amount — Very — Completely).

**Rate before and after.** Ask for intensity before evaluating a thought, and again after. This helps you decide whether to spend more time on this cognition or move forward. For example:
- Client reports sadness at 8 after an automatic thought surfaces
- You help evaluate the thought via Socratic questioning
- Client re-rates sadness at 3 or 4
- Relief suggests the intervention worked; if still high, you may need a different approach

### Distinguishing Thoughts from Emotions

At the beginning of treatment, many clients use the word "feel" to describe both thoughts and emotions:
- "I feel like I can't do it" (thought)
- "I feel anxious" (emotion)

**When to address the confusion:** If it won't disrupt the session flow or damage collaboration, gently correct: "So when you had the thought 'He doesn't want to talk to me,' how did you feel emotionally? Sad? Angry? Anxious?" Reframe: "Feelings are usually one word—sadness, anger, anxiety. Thoughts are ideas you have, like 'It's no use.'"

**When to let it pass:** In early sessions, if the mislabeling isn't clinically important to your conceptualization, don't interrupt. Save the distinction for when you'll teach thought records or when understanding the difference is essential to progress.

**When to teach explicitly:** If you plan to have the client use Thought Records or other structured worksheets, take time to clearly explain: "Feelings are emotions. Thoughts are ideas. Let's make sure we separate them so we can evaluate the thoughts and see if they're true."

### Matching Thought Content to Emotion

The relationship between what a client thinks and what they feel should make logical sense. If there's a mismatch, investigate further—there's likely an unidentified central thought or image.

Example: A client reports feeling *sad* when you'd expect *anxiety* based on their described thoughts. Ask: "I'm a little confused. Those thoughts sound more like anxious thoughts. What else was going through your mind?" They may then reveal a memory or belief ("I remembered when I lost my job and felt like such a failure") that better explains the sadness.
