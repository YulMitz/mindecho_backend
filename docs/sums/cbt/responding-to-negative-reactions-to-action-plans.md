## Responding to Negative Reactions to Action Plans

When you notice a client's mood shift or receive negative feedback about an Action Plan, respond immediately and directly.

**First, positively reinforce the feedback:** "I'm glad you told me. This gives me the chance to understand what's not working."

**Specify the problem:** Ask what's going through their mind. Are they overwhelmed by the number of tasks? Do they believe CBT isn't addressing their "real" problems? Do they feel pressure or shame?

**Establish meaning:** Understand what the negative reaction means to them. One client said, "You see, I've got real-life problems. It's not just my thinking." This revealed a misunderstanding of the cognitive model and concern that you were minimizing her struggles.

**Intervene at the alliance level:** Clarify your position. For example: "I do believe you have real-life problems. The problems with your mother, your loneliness—those are all real. I'm not saying we only need to look at your thoughts. We'll work on solving these problems together. I'm sorry if I gave you the wrong impression."

**Make reasonable adjustments:** If an Action Plan feels too burdensome, modify it. Make items optional, reduce the number, or decrease frequency. This demonstrates understanding and flexibility. A client might say, "The Action Plan is contributing to how overwhelmed I feel." Response: "Okay, let's mark the thought record as optional, and we'll cross off calling your friends for now."

**Repair and refine:** Use this moment to strengthen the therapeutic relationship and refine your conceptualization. Your willingness to listen and adjust shows you're genuinely tailoring treatment to them—not rigidly imposing a protocol.
