## Italian CBT Case Formulation Framework

In Italian CBT, a structured case formulation is a critical first step that guides the entire therapeutic process. This model organizes the client's narrative into three key components, moving from the past to the present to understand the complete picture of their psychopathology. This formulation is typically shared with the client to foster a collaborative understanding and provide a clear rationale for the treatment plan.

### 1. Historical Factors of Psychopathology

This component explores the client's past to identify vulnerabilities. The therapist investigates the client's life history to understand what experiences, relationships, or learning made them more susceptible to developing specific irrational beliefs and problematic themes. This is the "why you?" question. It looks at developmental history, family dynamics, and significant life events that laid the groundwork for the current problem.

### 2. Collapse Factors of Psychopathology

This part focuses on the onset of the symptoms. The therapist and client work to pinpoint what specific event or set of circumstances "broke the balance" in the client's life. This is the precipitating factor—the trigger that caused the underlying vulnerability to manifest as active psychopathology. Identifying this "collapse" helps clarify when the problem started and what stressors overwhelmed the client's coping mechanisms at that time.

### 3. Maintenance Factors of Psychopathology

This is the most crucial component for treatment planning, as it addresses what is happening *now* to keep the problem going. The therapist analyzes the current internal and external factors that block the resolution of the problem. This includes:
- **Irrational Beliefs (IBs):** Demands, awfulizing, frustration intolerance, self/other-downing.
- **Automatic Thoughts (ATs):** Over-generalizing, emotional reasoning, etc.
- **Dysfunctional Emotions & Behaviors:** Avoidance, safety behaviors, compulsions.
- **Vicious Circles:** How thoughts, feelings, and behaviors interact to create a self-perpetuating cycle of distress.

By clearly defining these three areas, the therapist can create a comprehensive and individualized map of the client's difficulties, which serves as the foundation for targeted and effective interventions.
