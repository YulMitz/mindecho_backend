## Determining When the Belief Originated or Became Maintained

Understanding the historical roots of a core belief helps clients develop compassion for themselves and see that the belief, while currently unhelpful, once made sense given their experiences.

### Gathering the History

Ask open-ended questions about when the client first felt or believed the core belief:
- "Do you remember feeling incompetent/unlovable/worthless at other times in your life? As a child?"
- "When did you first start believing this about yourself?"
- "Were there specific events or relationships that made you feel this way?"

### Common Origins

Core beliefs often develop from:
- **Parental messages**: "You're doing it wrong," critical feedback, or conditional regard
- **Early failures or rejections**: Not making a sports team, being bullied, losing a job
- **Traumatic or painful experiences**: Abuse, abandonment, significant loss
- **Repeated patterns**: Multiple similar failures that seemed to confirm the belief

### Seeing the Pattern Across Time

Clients often find it helpful to see that the belief has been present throughout their life but wasn't always activated. For example: "It sounds like 'I'm incompetent' is a core belief that started when you were a kid and your mother criticized you. But you didn't believe it all the time. For most of your life until the depression set in, you probably believed you were reasonably competent. The depression reactivated and strengthened that old belief."

### Therapeutic Value

This historical perspective serves several functions:
- Reduces shame ("I developed this belief for a reason")
- Normalizes the belief ("Many people with similar experiences have this belief")
- Suggests the belief is not permanent ("It became stronger with depression, so it can change")
- Opens possibility ("I was different before depression; I can be different again")
