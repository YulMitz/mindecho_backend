## Goal-Setting in CBT: First Session & Beyond

Goals are the specific, actionable flip side of problems and aspirations. Where aspirations paint the broader picture ("I want to feel useful"), goals are concrete steps ("Get a job," "See my friends more," "Clean my apartment," "Exercise three times a week").

### Setting Goals Collaboratively

Ask the client: "Could we talk about some specific goals you might have? How would you like your life to be different?"

The client may start with vague goals ("I want to be like I was before") or overly broad ones ("I want to be happy"). Help them become more specific: "If you weren't depressed anymore, what would you be doing differently?"

Write goals down. This signals to the client that you take their aspirations seriously and that they're committing to specific, traceable steps.

### Addressing Automatic Thoughts That Interfere

Almost inevitably, as soon as clients state a goal, they'll follow with a thought that undermines it: "I want to get a job, but I don't know how I'm going to be able to do these things. I haven't been able to so far."

**This is not the time to ignore the thought.** Respond by:

1. **Acknowledging the truth in their experience**: Yes, depression has made it very hard to do these things. That's the illness.

2. **Reframing their coming to therapy as evidence of change**: "You were willing to come for an evaluation even though you were skeptical. That shows you're willing to try. You did the evaluation well. We made an action plan together, and you actually did it [if relevant]. That tells me you're capable of change."

3. **Using the pneumonia analogy again**: "If you had pneumonia, would you try to cure yourself? No, you'd see a doctor. I'm like your doctor—except instead of medication, I'm teaching you skills that research shows help people get better."

4. **Emphasizing teamwork**: "You're not in this alone. We'll work as a team, step by step, so it won't feel overwhelming. I have good reason to expect treatment will help."

5. **Reframing seeking help as strength**: "I think it's a sign of strength that you're willing to do something that goes against your grain—asking for help instead of trying to solve everything yourself."

### Three Common Goal-Setting Difficulties

**1. Client has difficulty coming up with goals:**
Use the "miracle question" from solution-focused therapy: "If a miracle happened and you weren't depressed when you woke up tomorrow, what would be different? How would someone know you weren't depressed?"

Or explore whether the client has beliefs that goals are futile or that they don't deserve a better life.

**2. Goals are too broad:**
Redirection: "I want to be happier" becomes "If you were happier, what would you be doing differently?" Break down the broad goal into smaller, observable behaviors.

**3. Goals focus on changing others:**
Redirect to what the client can control. If the client says "I want my partner to be nicer," reframe: "What do you think of phrasing it this way: 'Learn new ways of talking to my partner'? If you change what you're doing, it might influence how they respond."

### Avoiding Goal Overwhelm

Don't set too many goals at once. For a depressed client, three to five goals is reasonable. Assure the client that goals can be added as therapy progresses. Quality matters more than quantity; achieving one goal builds self-efficacy and hope.

### The Importance of Goal Diversity

Help clients set goals across multiple life domains (work, relationships, health, self-care, recreation, spirituality). This balanced approach prevents obsessive focus on one area and ensures the client is rebuilding their life in multiple meaningful ways.
