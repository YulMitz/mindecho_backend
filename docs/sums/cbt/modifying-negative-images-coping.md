## Modifying Spontaneous Negative Images

When clients experience distressing spontaneous images, three main strategies help them respond: changing the image to a more functional one, following the image to its realistic conclusion (which often reduces catastrophic thinking), and reality-testing the image against what's actually happening.

### Strategy 1: Changing the "Movie"

Frame this as the client being the director. They don't have to be at the mercy of their spontaneous images—they can deliberately create different ones.

**How to apply it:**

Start by acknowledging the original image: "You saw yourself sitting alone on the couch, feeling really sad." Then ask: "What would you *want* to happen instead? What's a better ending?"

The client might imagine their daughter calling to invite them over, or calling a friend who's available. Help them make this new image vivid and detailed: "Can you see yourself picking up the phone? How do you feel when she invites you over?"

**Important next step:** Don't leave it at fantasy. Address the reality gap. Ask: "How do you know this will actually happen?" The answer is usually "I don't." Then problem-solve: "What could *you* do to make it more likely that you actually *will* get together with your daughter or friend this weekend?"

This moves the work from pure imagery into behavioral activation—the client can actually make a phone call, which increases the likelihood of the positive outcome they imagined. The imagery becomes motivational for action rather than disconnected fantasy.

**When to use this approach:** Use it intermittently for spontaneous negative images, not as the primary strategy for intrusive images. If images keep recurring, mindfulness techniques are more appropriate because they teach disengagement rather than substitution.
