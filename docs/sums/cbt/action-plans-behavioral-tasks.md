## Action Plans: Translating Insight into Behavior Change

An Action Plan is a concrete assignment—something specific the client agrees to do between sessions. It flows naturally from the discussion of each goal or issue on the agenda. Action Plans bridge the gap between what clients understand intellectually in session and what they do in the real world.

### Designing Effective Action Plans

Craft each plan carefully, taking into account:

- **The nature of the issue**: Is this about avoidance, behavioral activation, skill-building, or data-gathering?
- **Your conceptualization**: What will most directly target the maintaining factors?
- **Practical constraints**: How much time, energy, and opportunity does the client realistically have?
- **Client variables**: Their current level of motivation, concentration, depression severity, and preferences

### The Most Common Mistake: Tasks That Are Too Difficult

A frequent error is suggesting Action Plans that are much too ambitious. A severely depressed client who cannot get out of bed should not be asked to reorganize their entire apartment or return to full-time work immediately. Tasks that are too difficult will be abandoned, reinforcing the client's sense of failure and helplessness.

Instead, calibrate tasks to the client's current functioning:
- **Very depressed clients** might start with getting out of bed for one hour, making one phone call, or taking a walk around the block
- **Moderately depressed clients** might tackle one household task, attend a social event, or practice one new skill
- **Clients with higher functioning** can take on more ambitious projects

### Elements of a Clear Action Plan

A good Action Plan specifies:
- **What** the client will do (concrete, observable behavior)
- **When** and **how often** (e.g., "three times this week," "every morning")
- **Where** (if relevant)
- **How long** (if relevant)
- **The purpose** (why this will help—to test a thought, build a skill, gather evidence, or reduce avoidance)

### Examples of Action Plans

- "This week, I'll clean up the kitchen counter for 15 minutes while listening to music I enjoy. This will help you see that you *can* do things, even when you don't feel like it."
