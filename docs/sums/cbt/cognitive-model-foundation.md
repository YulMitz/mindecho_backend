## The Cognitive Model: Situation, Thought, Reaction

The core principle of CBT is that people's emotional, behavioral, and physiological reactions are not determined by situations themselves, but by *how they interpret* those situations. This is the cognitive model—the foundation upon which all CBT technique rests.

### The Basic Formula

**Situation/Event → Automatic Thoughts → Reaction (emotional, behavioral, physiological)**

The same objective event will produce different reactions in different people—or even in the same person at different times—depending on what thoughts pass through their mind. 

#### Example: Reading a CBT Textbook

Five people read the same introductory CBT book and have vastly different reactions:

- **Reader A** thinks "This makes sense. Finally, a book that will teach me to be a good therapist!" → feels mildly excited → keeps reading
- **Reader B** thinks "This approach is too simplistic. It will never work." → feels disappointed → closes the book
- **Reader C** thinks "This book isn't what I expected. What a waste of money." → feels disgusted → discards it
- **Reader D** thinks "I really need to learn this. What if I don't understand? What if I never get good at it?" → feels anxious → reads the same pages over and over
- **Reader E** thinks "This is too hard. I'm so dumb. I'll never master this." → feels sad → turns on the television

Same stimulus. Completely different emotional and behavioral outcomes. The difference lies in what each reader was *thinking*.

### Automatic Thoughts: The Layer Beneath Awareness

Automatic thoughts are the quick, evaluative cognitions that arise spontaneously—often so rapidly that people are barely aware of them. They're not the result of deliberate reasoning; they *feel* true in the moment. Clients are often far more aware of the *emotion* or *behavior* that follows than of the thought itself.

In session, when you notice a shift in your client's affect, body language, or behavior, you can ask: "What was just going through your mind?" This simple question anchors the cognitive model concretely. It teaches clients that emotions don't arise in a vacuum; they follow thought.

### Why This Matters for Therapy

Understanding this model means you can help clients see that they have more agency than they realize. While they cannot always control events, they *can* learn to notice and modify their thinking. When dysfunctional thoughts are subjected to objective reflection and reality-testing, mood, behavior, and physiological arousal typically improve.
