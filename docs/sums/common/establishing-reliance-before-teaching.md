## Establishing Reliance: The Foundation for Guidance and Teaching

Many therapists avoid offering direct explanation or guidance, fearing it will disrupt the therapeutic process or collapse into a prescriptive, non-analytic stance. But this caution can leave clients without crucial knowledge about how they work—knowledge that accelerates change.

The key is **timing and relationship state**. Explanation and guidance become powerful only when the client experiences you as someone who understands them and genuinely wants their wellbeing. Kohut called this the positive transference; others call it reliance.

**Signs that reliance is in place:**
- The client shows insight and uses your reflections to understand themselves better
- They make genuine emotional contact (tears, direct eye contact, softness in tone)
- They ask you for guidance or perspective, rather than you offering it unprompted
- They show curiosity about their own psychology rather than defensiveness
- They express appreciation for your understanding

**What reliance allows you to do:**

Once reliance is established, you can:
- Explain psychological development and how affects function
- Teach coping strategies and emotional skills
- Offer analogies that connect their familiar competencies to unfamiliar emotional territory
- Make interpretations that the client can hear as clarification rather than blame
- Validate their achievements and efforts

**The mechanism:** When a client trusts you and feels you have their interests at heart, your words carry a different weight. An explanation of affective development isn't abstract theory—it's a demonstration of care. It says: *I understand how you work, and here's what's possible.* It narrows the gap between expert and patient and suggests there is nothing mysterious or shameful about their experience.

**Important nuance:** This is not about being warm and fuzzy or offering "unconditional positive regard." It is about clear-eyed understanding, genuine interest in the client's competence and growth, and a consistent message that you believe in their ability to change.
