## The Affective Titration Deficit: Too Much or Too Little

Human problem-solving depends on a fundamental capacity: the ability to experience emotions without being overwhelmed by them. When affect is appropriate to the situation and well-regulated, a person can orient themselves, develop coping strategies, recruit support from others, reflect on plans, and seek helpful input.

When affect regulation is impaired, one of two extremes typically emerges:

**Affective lability (too much):**
The client floods easily. A small setback triggers intense anxiety or rage. They lose access to their coping skills and become temporarily incompetent. Their emotions drive reactive behavior rather than informing thoughtful response.

**Affective restriction (too little):**
The client describes major life events—abandonment, loss, potential divorce, significant conflict with loved ones—in a bland, matter-of-fact voice. No sadness, no fear, no visible emotion. They seem oddly detached from experiences that should matter deeply.

**The developmental root:**

Both patterns typically stem from an early relational environment in which emotional attunement was absent or unsafe. Children whose caregivers did not respond to their emotional signals, or who responded unpredictably or with anger, learn to protect themselves by either:
- Suppressing emotional experience (restriction), or
- Never learning to modulate it in the first place (lability)

In either case, emotions were not named, reflected back, or validated. The child never internalized the experience of being understood emotionally.

**Why this matters for brief therapy:**

You cannot dismantle a strong defensive structure against affect in a short time frame. However, you can often help the client *reacquaint themselves* with feeling in the safety of the therapeutic relationship. If the capacity for appropriate affect was once present (i.e., it is defended against rather than developmentally missing), there is hope.

**The clinical indicator:**

Notice whether the client shows any emotional resonance in the room with you. When you reflect something deeply true about their experience, do they become tearful, thoughtful, or visibly moved? If yes, the affective capacity is there—it's being defended against. If the client remains entirely unmoved even when describing profound longing or loss, the deficit may be more foundational.
