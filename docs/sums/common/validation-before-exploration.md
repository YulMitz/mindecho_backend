## Validation Before Exploration

One of the most misunderstood interventions in psychotherapy is positive regard or validation. Therapists sometimes worry that praising a client or acknowledging their strengths will "seal over" the problem, allow the client to avoid discomfort, or short-circuit the therapeutic process. This concern is partly valid in long-term psychoanalysis with neurotic patients, where transference material needs to emerge. But in many clinical situations, validation is not a shortcut—it's a foundation.

### Why Validation Matters

Clients who carry shame or low self-esteem are preoccupied with self-defense. They are searching for evidence of their worthlessness and braced for judgment. When you offer genuine recognition of their accomplishments or strengths *before* you fully understand the problem, you do something crucial: you interrupt the script. You signal that you see them as more than a bundle of pathology.

This shift is not about making them feel better in the moment. It's about creating the psychological safety required for genuine change. A patient dominated by the threat to the self does not have enough flexibility left over to do the work of therapy. They're using all their energy to ward off the threat.

### The Paradox of the Vicious Circle

Many shame-prone clients are caught in a bind:
- They need validation and connection to heal.
- But revealing the source of their shame feels like inviting humiliation.
- So they protect themselves by staying isolated or defensive.
- This isolation and defensiveness push away the very people who might validate them.

The therapist, by offering validation *without requiring disclosure first*, can break this circle. You're saying: "I see worth in you already. You don't have to earn it by being perfect or proving yourself."

### When Validation Opens the Door

Once a client experiences genuine validation—not empty reassurance, but therapist recognition of real strengths or efforts—they often feel safe enough to reveal the shame they've been hiding. The validation becomes the condition that makes disclosure possible. The client thinks: "If this person can see my worth even without knowing my secret, maybe the secret won't destroy me."

This is not manipulation. It's the basic condition of being heard: you have to feel safe before you can be honest about the hard things.

### The Difference Between Validation and Collusion

Validate the client's strengths, effort, and worth. Do *not* validate self-defeating behavior or the shame-driven stories they tell about themselves. If a highly accomplished professional says "I'm a fake," you might say:

- "You've built a successful career. I can see why you feel anxious about being exposed, and I also see evidence that you know your field."

Not: "Of course you're not a fake."

Validation acknowledges what's real and true. The client's fear is real; their accomplishments are also real. Holding both is the therapist's job.

### The Therapist's Genuine Interest

Validation that matters is not performed. It comes from genuine interest in the client as a person. When you read a client's history and recognize the genuine effort, intelligence, or resilience it took to get where they are, that recognition is real. You're not faking it to build rapport. You're noticing what's actually there.

This matters because clients can sense the difference. A shame-prone person is exquisitely attuned to insincerity. If you offer validation that doesn't ring true, it feels like pity or condescension, which deepens shame. But if your words come from a place of genuine respect for what the client has done, despite the obstacles, that lands differently.

### How Validation Changes the Work

Early validation of strengths and accomplishments does something important: it shifts the therapeutic orientation from pathology to development. Instead of focusing only on what's broken, you're saying: "You have real capacities. Let's understand what's blocking them."

This reframe often allows the client to work with more hope and less defensiveness. They're not trying to hide their badness; they're trying to understand why their genuine strengths don't translate into self-respect.
