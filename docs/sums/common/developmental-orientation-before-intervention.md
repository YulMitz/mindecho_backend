## Orientation Must Come Before Intervention

There is a powerful temptation in therapy to move quickly to insight and guidance. You observe something troubling—a parent-child dynamic that mirrors the client's own childhood, a pattern of self-sabotage, an affect management problem—and you want to point it out. Your insights feel urgent and helpful.

Resist this urge until you have established **mutual orientation**: a shared understanding of what the actual problem is.

**Why premature interpretation fails:**

Until a client trusts you enough to rely on your perspective, interpretations—however accurate—land as criticism. They hear: *You're doing it wrong.* The client becomes defensive, feels misunderstood, or experiences shame. Resistance builds, and the therapeutic relationship strain worsens the very problem you were trying to address.

**The process of orientation:**

1. **Listen and gather facts.** Get the full picture of the client's life, competencies, history, and present situation.

2. **Notice discrepancies.** Where is the client's internal experience at odds with observable reality?

3. **Reflect back what you're puzzled by, without judgment.** Example: "I'm a bit confused. From what you've described, you handled that negotiation skillfully. Yet you told me you can't do conflict. Help me understand that."

4. **Wait for the client to move toward insight.** Often, when the discrepancy is gently highlighted, the client will begin to see it themselves. Their own reflection is far more powerful than your interpretation.

5. **Only when there is reliance in place**—when the client has experienced you as understanding and non-shaming—can you offer guidance, explanation, or deeper interpretation.

**The payoff:** Once orientation is established and the client agrees with you about what the real problem is, your subsequent interventions are far more effective. The client can hear you without defensiveness. Education becomes possible. Behavioral change follows more readily.
