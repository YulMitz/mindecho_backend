## Working with Shame in Therapy

Shame is the affect that makes us feel utterly alone. Unlike guilt (which is about something we did), shame is about what we *are*—a fundamental defect in the self. When a client is shame-prone, no amount of accurate interpretation will land until they first feel safe from the anticipated attack or humiliation they expect.

### Recognizing Shame-Driven Behavior

Shame typically reveals itself through several defensive patterns:

- **Pomposity or grandiose speech**: Clients use inflated language or self-importance to convince others (and themselves) of their value, often when feeling vulnerable beneath the surface.
- **Attack-other defense**: Critical, harsh, or dominating behavior toward family or colleagues—an attempt to shame others before being shamed.
- **Avoidance/withdrawal**: The client sidesteps confrontation or becomes evasive rather than standing up for themselves directly.
- **Isolation**: Shame-prone individuals cut themselves off from the very people whose validation they desperately need, creating a vicious circle.
- **Disorganization when threatened**: A client who is otherwise intelligent and capable becomes paralyzed or incoherent in situations where they feel exposed.

### The Shame Spiral

A shame-prone person is caught in a trap: they need validation and connection to feel whole, but revealing the reason for their shame feels catastrophic. So they defend harder, which alienates others or triggers the very rejection they fear. Their defensive maneuvers (pomposity, aggression, evasion) then backfire in real relationships, reinforcing their sense of worthlessness.

### Therapeutic Stance: Build Before You Confront

The critical insight is that **you cannot confront defensive behavior effectively until the client feels safe**. If you point out the client's avoidance, grandiosity, or counterproductive patterns too early, they will either:

- Flee treatment
- Ignore what you say
- Redouble the defensive maneuver
- Become more ashamed

Until the client has a viable alternative—a sense that you see their worth and won't humiliate them—they cannot afford to relinquish their defenses. They're like someone on crutches: pointing out that crutches are a poor substitute for legs is useless until the leg is healed.

### Practical Interventions

**1. Validate Accomplishments and Strengths First**

Before you understand the source of the shame, recognize what the client has actually achieved. This is not flattery or false reassurance; it's genuine acknowledgment that builds the therapeutic alliance. The shame-prone client is preoccupied with self-defense and lacks the psychological flexibility for deeper work. Your validation begins to construct the "floor of deserved self-respect" they need to proceed.

**2. Don't Confront the Defense Too Early**

If the client presents with family conflict rooted in their shame-driven behavior, don't focus initially on what they're doing wrong. This will feel like confirmation that you side with whoever is complaining (spouse, boss, colleague). Instead, use that moment to explore other areas of their life, looking for clues about what's really driving the behavior.

**3. Wait for the Right Moment to Name the Pattern**

As the alliance strengthens and the client feels safer, they may bring up a situation where their defensive behavior is actively sabotaging a goal they care about (money, respect, a relationship). That's when confrontation becomes therapeutically useful. By then, your relationship gives you the authority to say hard things—and the client's wish for your approval can motivate change.

**4. When You Do Confront, Do It Like the Good Mother**

The good mother disapproves of the behavior but makes clear she still values the child. You can face the client with uncomfortable truth while holding that tension: "I don't accept what you're doing in this instance, AND I believe you're capable of something different. I think you want my approval, and I think you'll find that asking directly for what's yours feels better than this evasion."

**5. Help Them Grieve the Unmet Need**

Often the shame originates in early lack of validation or recognition. A child who was never praised for genuine accomplishments grows up unable to internalize self-respect, no matter what they achieve. Once the client feels safe, help them see that they needed to hear "I'm proud of you" and never did. This doesn't excuse the behavior, but it makes sense of it and opens the possibility of reparation—in the therapy relationship itself.

### When Confrontation Works

Once the shame begins to lift—once the client starts to trust that you see their worth—they gain the psychological freedom to behave differently. They can then look back at their previous patterns with more objectivity and understand how they inadvertently undermined themselves. The shame no longer dominates their mental life, and the defensive maneuvers become optional rather than compulsory.
