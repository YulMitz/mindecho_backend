## Validating Change and Effort

Validation in therapy is not generic reassurance or empty cheerleading. It is the therapist's emotionally genuine response to concrete evidence of the client's competence, growth, or changed behavior. The power lies in specificity and sincerity.

### When Validation Matters Most

Clients who grew up in emotionally deprived, shaming, or chaotic families often lack internalized permission to feel proud. When they achieve something real—handle a difficult conversation, reverse a pattern, get external recognition—their first move is often to undo it: "I should have known that already," "It was an accident," "Anyone could have done that." This defensive reversal protects them from the vulnerability of mattering.

The therapist's task here is **not** to argue the client out of shame, but to name what actually happened and name what it means. This is especially important after behavioral interventions succeed. When a client follows concrete guidance from the therapist (a communication strategy, a reframing, a technique) and the real world responds positively, the therapist must pause and allow that success to register.

### How to Validate Effectively

1. **Be specific about what changed.** Don't say, "You did great." Say what the client actually did differently: "You put yourself in his shoes instead of defending yourself. When you apologized without making excuses, he relaxed and became generous with you."

2. **Connect it to the client's struggle.** Acknowledge that this was difficult for them: "I think you would have figured this out yourself if shame hadn't stood in your way. When you grew up being shamed for mistakes, it's natural to get defensive instead of taking responsibility."

3. **Tie it to competence, not personality.** The client needs to know their action worked in the world, not that they are a good person: "You ended up getting what you wanted because you changed how you approached the problem—not because he changed."

4. **Use your own genuine emotion.** Clients sense the difference between earned praise and hollow comfort. If you believe the client did something that required real growth, let that be audible in your voice.

5. **Expect minimization and sit with it.** When the client says "It seems too easy" or "Maybe we're making too big a deal out of this," resist the urge to argue. Instead, offer perspective: "Self-esteem is always being put on the line in everyday interactions. How you manage these moments shapes your sense of competence. This one was a significant shift."

### The Role of Validation in Building Reliance

Early in therapy, especially with clients who cannot easily bond, validation of *performance* (what they did right) can build reliance more effectively than validation of *feeling* ("You're okay"). When you validate effort and show the client their own competence reflected back to them, they begin to trust that you see them accurately. This becomes the foundation of the therapeutic relationship itself.

Validation also works developmentally. A child learns what is good and real in the world partly through the mirrored feedback of a trusted adult. Adults with developmental deficits in attachment or self-worth still need this function—not to be talked to like children, but to have their achievements witnessed and named by someone they are coming to rely on.
