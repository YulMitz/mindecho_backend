## Moving from Abstraction to Concrete Example

Clients often arrive with a presenting complaint that sounds reasonable on the surface but doesn't hold up under scrutiny. A client may say, "I have no confidence and can't speak publicly," while their actual job requires them to address dozens of people regularly and do it well. The complaint feels true to them, but the facts contradict it.

**Why this matters:** Abstract complaints can mislead you into the wrong formulation. If you accept the client's self-description at face value, you'll miss the *real* problem—which may lie in a completely different developmental sector.

**The clinical move:**

Ask: "Tell me about a specific time when this happened. Give me an example." Then follow up with details:
- What was the actual situation?
- What specifically did you do or say?
- What was the outcome?
- How did others respond?

**What to watch for:** Often the client's description of their competence contradicts their stated incompetence. A client afraid of conflict may, in the details, describe handling complex negotiations skillfully every day. A client convinced they can't form relationships may reveal a pattern of attracting others who pursue them.

This discrepancy between narrative and evidence is *not* a lie. It reflects how the client's internal emotional experience (shame, longing, fear) overrides their actual observable functioning. The affect tells them they're failing; the facts say otherwise.

**Clinical use:** This discrepancy is your diagnostic gold. It tells you the real problem lies elsewhere—often in the *affective* rather than *behavioral* domain. A competent professional who doesn't feel competent needs help with self-worth and internalized validation, not skills training.
