## Formulation Using Developmental Theory

A formulation is your working hypothesis about what's actually going on with a client. It's more specific than a diagnosis and should guide your choice of interventions. One useful way to build a formulation is to think developmentally: across different sectors of psychological growth, which ones are working well, and which have stalled or failed?

### The Developmental Spiral

The developmental sectors typically include:

- **Attachment**: The capacity to trust, rely on, and feel safe with others.
- **Autonomy**: The ability to make decisions, take action, and feel competent as an individual.
- **Affect and reason**: The capacity to recognize, tolerate, and reflect on feelings; to think clearly even under stress.
- **Sexuality and intimacy**: The ability to integrate desire and tenderness with another person.
- **Creativity**: The capacity to initiate, imagine, and produce something new.

A client may be strong in some sectors and weak in others. The presenting problem often points to a sector, but it may not be the *primary* sector where development has failed.

### The Value of Asking "Which Sector?"

For example, a man comes in with marital conflict. He's critical, controlling, and his wife is fed up. The obvious diagnosis is "problems with attachment" or "relationship dysfunction." But if you ask developmentally:

- **Autonomy**: Is he able to make decisions? Take action? Achieve goals? (Yes, often excellently.)
- **Affect**: Can he recognize his emotions? (Sometimes, under pressure.)
- **Attachment**: Does he trust others? Allow himself to be vulnerable? (No—this is clearly the problem.)

But maybe the real issue isn't attachment per se. Maybe the issue is that his autonomy sector is *over*-functioning in a way that blocks attachment. He's so focused on asserting control and proving competence that he can't let anyone else lead. He can't rely on his wife because relying on someone means admitting he needs something he can't provide for himself.

Now you have a different entry point for treatment. Instead of focusing on his marital conflict (which will only entrench his defensiveness), you might focus on the *function* of his controlling behavior. What does the autonomy-driven control protect him from? Often it's the terror of being dependent, which may trace back to early experiences of unpredictability or betrayal.

### Using Formulation to Prioritize Interventions

A good formulation helps you decide what to work on first. If a client has:

- Strong autonomy and affect regulation, but fragile attachment
- You might initially avoid challenging their behavior (which will feel like judgment) and instead focus on building the alliance and safety.

If a client has:

- Good relational skills and attachment capacity, but struggles with autonomy
- You might directly explore what's blocking their ability to act or decide, because they have the emotional safety to tolerate that kind of inquiry.

### The Mismatch Between Presenting Problem and Primary Issue

One of the most important moves a formulation allows is recognizing when the presenting problem is *secondary* to the real issue. A client comes in saying their spouse is angry and threatens divorce. The therapist might focus on the marital conflict. But if the formulation reveals that the primary issue is shame (a failure in the autonomy sector—the inability to feel respect for oneself despite real accomplishments), then the marital conflict is just a symptom.

Treating the symptom (giving marital advice) won't work because the real problem (shame-driven behavior) remains. But addressing the shame—validating accomplishments, helping the client understand what happened to prevent self-respect from developing—can change the whole picture. The marital behavior often improves once the underlying developmental arrest is addressed.

### Building the Formulation Over Time

You don't need to have the formulation figured out in the first session. In fact, it's better if you don't. Listen to the client's history, the way they talk about relationships, their accomplishments, their regrets. Notice which sectors light up and which ones they minimize or avoid. Over a few sessions, a pattern usually emerges.

### Using the Formulation With the Client

Eventually, the formulation becomes something you can share with the client in language that makes sense to them:

"It sounds like you're someone who has real competence and drive. You've built a career, you've managed a family, you make good decisions. But somewhere along the way, you came to believe you're not worthy of respect—including your own. So you try to force other people to see your worth, which backfires. Does that land?"

When the formulation is right, clients recognize themselves in it. And when they do, they often feel relief: "That's it. That's what I've been doing." The formulation becomes the shared map for the work ahead.
