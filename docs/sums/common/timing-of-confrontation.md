## Timing of Confrontation

One of the most delicate skills in therapy is knowing when to name what you see without destroying the alliance. Too early, and the client experiences it as judgment and retreats. Too late, and you've colluded with the self-defeating pattern. The key is using the alliance as your guide.

### The Early-Confrontation Problem

When a therapist confronts a client's self-defeating behavior too early—before the client feels genuinely understood and valued—the confrontation lands as attack. The client hears: "You're doing it wrong." Even if you phrase it carefully, the underlying message is judgment.

For a shame-prone client, early confrontation confirms their worst fear: that if someone really knew them, they'd be seen as bad or wrong. The client will then either:

- Leave therapy
- Comply superficially while becoming more defended
- Increase the very behavior you've confronted (because the shame has intensified)
- Attack you or become hostile

### The Late-Confrontation Problem

If you never confront, you're essentially saying: "I see what you're doing, and I'm okay with it." This can feel like collusion or enabling. The client may even unconsciously test the alliance to see if you'll eventually call them out. When you don't, some clients lose respect for you or assume you don't believe they're capable of change
