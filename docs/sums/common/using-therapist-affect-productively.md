## Using Your Own Affect as Clinical Data

Therapists often receive training that labels any emotional reaction to a client as "countertransference"—something to eliminate or manage away. This misunderstanding can blind you to valuable information about the client's relational patterns.

**The distinction:** True countertransference is *unconscious* conflict stirred up in you by the client's transference. The moment you become *aware* of an emotional reaction, by definition it is no longer countertransference. Instead, it becomes useful data.

When you notice feeling resentment, confusion, sadness, or discomfort in response to something a client says or does, that conscious reaction tells you something important:

1. **About your own sensitivities**: What themes trigger you? These can reveal areas where you need awareness or further personal work.

2. **About the client's relational patterns**: Your emotional response often mirrors what the client unconsciously tries to evoke in others. If you feel a flash of shame, irritation, or a desire to withdraw when the client speaks, that may reflect exactly what the client's social world experiences.

**The clinical move:** File away the reaction without acting on it immediately. Wonder about it. Does the pattern fit what you're learning about the client's history? Does it illuminate a defensive strategy (e.g., "attack other before being shamed," "please the authority to avoid abandonment")?

The goal is *controlled use* of your affect—noticing it, thinking about it, and using it in the client's interest rather than reacting impulsively (as you might in a social situation). This requires you to step back from the feeling and use it as an observational tool.
