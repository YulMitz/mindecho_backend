## Reconnecting With Avoided Emotion: Navigating Re-Entry to the Body

Many clients who have experienced depression, trauma, or intense emotional pain have developed a protective strategy: **living in their heads rather than their bodies.** They may have learned that thinking about emotion is "safer" than experiencing it somatically. This strategy is wholly understandable but leaves emotional processing incomplete, requiring ongoing mental effort to keep emotion-related bodily sensations out of awareness.

### Why Clients Avoid the Body

**Common patterns:**
- Physical or sexual abuse survivors whose bodies hold traumatic memory
- Clients with medical emergencies or serious illness tied to intense bodily fear
- Depression survivors who learned that "thinking it through" feels safer than feeling it through
- Anyone for whom emotions historically overwhelmed them

When the body scan practice asks clients to *intentionally reconnect with awareness of bodily sensations*, they may experience being quite overwhelmed by previously avoided emotions. This can feel dangerous or re-traumatizing.

### How to Support Safe Re-Engagement

**Be vigilant for warning signs** in clients' feedback. Listen for:
- "I got too upset and had to stop"
- "All my past came back to haunt me"
- "I felt panicked when I focused on my [body part]"
- Avoidance of the practice altogether

**Guide them to walk the fine line.** The instruction is to remain connected to bodily experience *without* being blown away by its intensity. This is a skill, not a matter of willpower. Teach them to:

1. **Return to the body scan instructions intentionally.** When overwhelmed, refocus on the *instructions themselves*—follow the specified region of the body step by step, as best they can. This provides structure and a "place to stand."

2. **Use the breath as an anchor.** Teach them to "breathe into" an intense sensation, gathering themselves in awareness, rather than bracing against it or dissociating.

3. **Acknowledge the progress.** If a client can now feel something they previously numbed, this is often a sign of healing in motion. Help them see the discomfort as the *work of processing*, not a sign of failure.

### What Healing Looks Like

A client reflected seven sessions into the program: *"I was quite alarmed when I started doing the body scan. It was as if all my past came back to haunt me. I got very, very upset. Now, I don't feel half as bad. I don't feel so stirred up about it all... it's all been filed away now."*

This is the goal: **completion of emotional processing**. The intense feelings that were stuck in the body can move through and be integrated, often with dramatic results. In your role, normalize the initial difficult phase and help clients understand it as temporary and part of healing.
