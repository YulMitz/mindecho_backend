## Working Through the Body: The Path of Least Resistance

### Why the Body Matters

Much of MBCT's effectiveness comes from its integration of body awareness. While cognitive therapy focuses on the content of thoughts, and standard mindfulness can sometimes remain in the "head," MBCT deliberately uses bodily sensation as a gateway to changing one's relationship to difficult experience.

This is strategic. When someone is caught in rumination or overwhelmed by negative thoughts, thinking about thoughts—even observing them—can be difficult or ineffective. But the body is always available. It's always communicating. And unlike thoughts, which can be abstract and slippery, bodily sensations are concrete and "truthful" in a way that makes them easier to work with.

Additionally, for people with a history of trauma or depression, the body often carries the memory and reaction even when the person can rationally "challenge" their thoughts. Approaching through the body respects this reality and gives another route in.

### The Two-Step Process

**Step 1: Locate the Sensation**
When a difficult thought or worry arises, the first move is not to analyze or challenge it, but to ask: "Where do I feel this in my body?" Anxiety might live in the chest or belly. Shame might be a tightness in the face or throat. Grief might be a heaviness in the center of the chest.

The instruction is to bring deliberate, friendly awareness to that place. Not to tense around it or brace against it, but to gently focus attention there using the breath as a vehicle. "Breathing into" that region on the inbreath, creating awareness of the sensations.

**Step 2: Open and Soften**
Once you have awareness of the sensation, the second step is to notice your relationship to it. Often there's an automatic bracing, tightening, pushing-away response. The instruction is to release that resistance on the outbreath, with phrases like:
- "It's OK, whatever it is, it's OK. Let me feel it."
- "Soften. Open."

This doesn't mean the sensation goes away. It means the sensation (tightness, ache, trembling, heat) is held in a larger space now. The person learns: "The feeling is still here, but I'm not fighting it, and now there's room to breathe around it."

### A Powerful Example from Practice

The text includes a moving account of a woman whose son had been in serious trouble with police. She brought this worry to mind during meditation and was initially devastated by the physical sensation—a solid, suffocating mass in her chest. When the instructor invited her to "breathe into" the sensation, something shifted. She described it: "It was like the whole of my body around there was really tight and knotted. And then you said, 'Breathe into it,' and it suddenly became like a great big empty space... having doors and windows open, and with curtains blowing and air coming in and out."

The anxiety didn't vanish, but its relationship to her changed fundamentally. By the end of the meditation, she was able to say, "Oh, you're still there, but never mind, the wind's blowing through and that's alright." She had made space for the difficult feeling, rather than being consumed by it.

This is not about *getting rid of* the problem. It's about no longer letting the problem fill 100% of your internal space. The person becomes larger than the problem.
