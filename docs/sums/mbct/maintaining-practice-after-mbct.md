## Maintaining Practice After MBCT Ends

One of the most important practical challenges in MBCT is bridging the gap between structured group sessions and independent practice. The program ends; the instructor is no longer present. How do people sustain what they've learned?

### The "Everydayness" Principle

Experience shows that **regular, daily, brief practice is preferable to longer but infrequent practice**. Someone who sits for 10 minutes every day will maintain skills better than someone who sits for 60 minutes once a week.

There is something about the *everydayness* of practice—no matter how short—that builds continuity and sustains both motivation and momentum. This is particularly important because "relating to our experience mindfully is not part of the territory of the depressed mind." The depressed mind naturally reverts to automaticity, rumination, and avoidance. Brief, daily practice acts as an anchor—a small, repeated act that reconnects you to the mindful stance.

Think of it like learning a foreign language: you don't get better speaking it once a week for two hours. You improve by speaking it every day, even briefly.

### Designing Realistic Maintenance Plans

Participants need to decide on a specific, realistic practice plan *before* the program ends. Research from cognitive therapy shows that the people who benefit most are those who do the homework. If people let their practice lapse entirely, it becomes much harder to access it when stress hits.

Plans vary widely. Some participants commit to 30 minutes of daily sitting. Others alternate between regular sitting practice and yoga. Most commonly, participants plan to:
- Use the breathing space (a 3-minute practice) multiple times throughout the day
- Do more formal practice on weekends or when they feel they need to "refresh"
- Return to a favorite guided meditation recording when needed

**The important thing is that the plan should be realistic**—one that the person can actually sustain given their life circumstances.

### Choosing Your Tools

Participants benefit from having specific supports for their practice:
- A favorite guided meditation recording (many return to Series 2 recordings, particularly the mountain meditation, which uses an extended metaphor about observing storms moving around a mountain)
- A recording that simply contains the sound of bells ringing—enough structure to sit without instructions
- Meaningful quotes or reminders
- A memento from the program (often a marble, stone, or bead) that serves as a physical reminder of the commitment

### Why Stress Is Unpredictable

A core reason instructors spend significant time on this topic is that **stress is unpredictable—nobody knows when and where depression will hit**. But when it does, the person who has kept their practice "oiled" (maintained it regularly) will have it available. Those who let it lapse entirely find it much harder to resurrect when needed.

The analogy is useful: "Keep your tools oiled, so that they are ready for use when you actually need them." Or as Jon Kabat-Zinn advised: "Weave your parachute every day, rather than leave it to the time you have to jump from the plane."
