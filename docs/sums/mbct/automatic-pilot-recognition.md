## Automatic Pilot: The Silent Relapse Risk

Most people experience frequent moments of automatic pilot—states where the body is doing one thing while the mind is elsewhere, or the mind is passive, "caught" by thoughts, memories, or plans without conscious intention. These moments feel normal and harmless. But for someone with a history of depression, automatic pilot is a vulnerability.

### What Automatic Pilot Looks Like

Common examples include:
- Reading a full page of text and retaining nothing
- Driving a familiar route and arriving with no memory of the journey
- Intending to stop at a friend's house and driving past it on automatic
- Beginning an activity and suddenly realizing time has passed without awareness

In automatic pilot, the mind is passive, allowing itself to be "caught" by whatever thoughts or feelings drift through it. There's no intentional direction of attention.

### Why Automatic Pilot Matters for Depression

When someone is on automatic pilot, early warning signs of mood shift go unnoticed. Fragments of negative thinking—the initial sparks that could be managed if caught early—coalesce into patterns before conscious awareness registers them.

The sequence looks like this:
1. **Unnoticed**: Small mood dips and early negative thoughts emerge
2. **Uninterrupted**: Without awareness, they activate associated thought patterns
3. **Amplified**: The self-perpetuating loops of rumination build momentum
4. **Too late**: By the time the person consciously notices their mood has dropped significantly, the depressive state is already established and difficult to interrupt

### The First Step: Recognizing Automatic Pilot

An important first step in relapse prevention is helping clients recognize those times when their minds are running on automatic pilot—not as a sign of failure or laziness, but as evidence that the default mode of consciousness is operating.

This recognition is itself a form of mindfulness: noticing that attention has drifted, that the body and mind are operating in parallel, that intention is absent. **The moment this recognition occurs, the person has already stepped partially out of automatic pilot.**

### Practical Starting Point

Given that clients in recovery from depression typically don't experience strong depressive symptoms, using automatic pilot recognition in everyday activities provides the training ground. Clients learn to:
- Notice when their minds have drifted during routine activities
- Intentionally return their awareness to the present moment
- Recognize the difference between automatic and intentional experience

This daily practice builds the skill that will later be deployed when actual mood shifts occur.
