## Relapse Prevention Planning in MBCT

Relapse prevention in MBCT involves two complementary components: recognizing early warning signs (relapse signatures) and having concrete action plans ready to deploy when those signs appear.

### Identifying Relapse Signatures

The first step is helping clients recognize *their own* specific early warning signs of depression. These are highly individual. One participant identified: sluggishness, disturbed sleep (waking throughout the night), feelings of futility and hopelessness, lowering of morale, and a physical slowness pervading things.

The key point is that these signs are often **not dramatic**—they're subtle shifts in mood, energy, or sleep. During Session 7, clients develop their personal list by reflecting on what preceded their previous depressive episodes. This awareness transforms mood changes from something that "happens to you" into something you can *observe happening*, creating space for choice.

### The Antidepressant Activity List

A practical format is the "Antidepressant Activity List"—written down in advance, filed away, and consulted when mood begins to drop. The list includes activities across two categories:

**Pleasure-based activities**: phone a friend, rent a video you enjoy, have a hot bath, take a nap, treat yourself to favorite food, go for a walk, visit a friend, listen to music that makes you feel good.

**Mastery/achievement activities**: clean the house, clear out a cupboard, catch up with letter writing, do some work, pay a bill, do gardening, tackle something you've been postponing, take exercise.

The instruction attached to this list is critical: *"Although you may not feel like doing any of these, select at least one and do it anyway."* This bypasses the motivational barrier that depression creates.

### The Power of Early Action

The most important principle: **act early in the process**, before the downward spiral deepens. A participant described how, before the program, he would "simply be dragged along, dragged down by it" with no sense of control. Afterward, recognizing his warning signs became "a prompt to do something"—a visible difference between being a helpless victim of depression and having an available strategy.

He noted an important caveat: the strategy doesn't cure depression, but it "gives you some control over what is happening to you."

### Maintaining Motivation Through Personal Values

Experience shows that relapse prevention plans alone—no matter how good—are difficult to sustain unless **linked to a positive reason for maintaining the practice**. This means connecting the prevention work to something the person deeply cares about.

One participant, Joanne, discovered she had more time and availability for her children when she maintained a mindfulness practice. Though she initially worried that formal practice would take time *away* from them, she found the opposite. She linked her commitment to ongoing practice directly to her wish to remain emotionally available to her children.

At the deepest level, the core reason for maintaining practice is: "Because I care for myself." For those with depression, this is often the hardest reason to access, as depression brings attitudes of self-criticism and self-denigration. MBCT invites a different relationship—one informed by *trugaredd* (a Welsh term meaning loving-kindness and mercy)—toward oneself and one's difficulties.
