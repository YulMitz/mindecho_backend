## The Thoughts-Feelings Connection: ABC Model Made Concrete

A powerful way to help clients understand why MBCT works is to show them—experientially—how their interpretations of events *cause* their emotional reactions, not the events themselves. This is the foundation of the cognitive model, but it lands much more strongly when clients discover it themselves.

### The Exercise: Imaginal Scenario

**Setup:** Ask clients to settle into a comfortable position, close their eyes, and imagine:

*"You are walking down the street. On the other side of the street, you see somebody you know. You smile and wave. The person just doesn't seem to notice and walks by."*

**Debrief:** Invite them to notice what thoughts, feelings, and bodily sensations came up. Write them all on the board as they report them.

**Typical responses vary widely:**
- *"I was upset at them for not acknowledging me"* (feelings + thoughts mixed)
- *"They must have seen me. Do what you want"* (thought + resigned feeling)
- *"I felt quite isolated"* (feeling → likely thought: "Nobody likes me")
- *"I would go over this in my head for quite some time. My thinking would be really negative, and every time I went down one branch, there would be three to five negative thoughts coming off that"* (meta-observation of rumination pattern)

### The Key Teaching Point

**Same situation, many different interpretations = many different feelings.** This observation directly teaches the ABC model:

- **A** = Activating Event (the person not acknowledging you)
- **B** = Belief/Thought (your interpretation: rejection? preoccupation? hostility?)
- **C** = Consequence (emotional reaction and bodily sensation)

### Surfacing Mood-Dependent Thinking

After reviewing the variety of reactions, ask:

*"Which of these ways of thinking would be most likely to come to mind if you were depressed? What thoughts would you have if you were still depressed?"*

Most people will recognize that depressed thinking involves interpretations like *"Nobody likes me"* or *"I'm not worth noticing."* This is crucial: it shows that **thoughts are not facts**—they reflect mood state as much as objective reality. When depression is present, the same neutral event gets filtered through a distorted lens.

### Application to Rumination Prevention

The exercise demonstrates why checking thoughts *early* is so important. In MBCT, the goal is not to challenge each negative thought (standard cognitive therapy), but to **become aware of the thought stream itself**—to notice that interpretation is happening—*before* the cascading rumination takes hold.

The client who realizes *"Oh, I'm having depressed thinking again"* has already stepped partway out of its grip. They can then use the breath or body as an anchor to disengage from the interpretive spiral, rather than fighting each thought.

### Why This Exercise Is Superior to Explanation

Telling clients "your thoughts cause your feelings" is abstract. Having them *experience* how their own minds generated different emotions from the same event is memorable and motivating. They see the power of interpretation directly, and it becomes self-evident why learning to relate differently to thoughts (rather than accepting them as truth) matters for preventing relapse.
