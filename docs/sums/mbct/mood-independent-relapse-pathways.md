## Progressive Autonomy of Depressive Episodes

Robert Post's research reveals an important pattern: the relationship between psychological stress and depressive relapse is not fixed—it changes over time. Early episodes of depression are typically preceded by significant negative life events; however, as someone experiences more episodes, stressful events play a progressively smaller role. Later episodes become more easily triggered and may appear spontaneous.

### The Threshold-Lowering Mechanism

Post proposed that each new depressive episode contributes to small neurobiological changes that gradually lower the threshold at which depression can be triggered. Early in someone's history, they might need a major loss or significant stress to become depressed. Over time, the trigger becomes smaller—relationship frustrations, minor work stressors, or even routine life transitions can precipitate an episode. Eventually, the threshold may lower so far that episodes seem to occur almost independently of external circumstances.

### Implications for Relapse Prevention

This model suggests why preventive work is critical for people with recurrent depression. The pattern means:

- **Vigilance alone is insufficient**: Clients cannot rely on identifying major stressors to know when they're at risk, because later episodes may not follow the stressor-symptom pattern of earlier episodes.

- **Early intervention is crucial**: By the time obvious warning signs appear, the depressive process may already be underway. The goal shifts toward recognizing subtler mood shifts and early-stage thought-affect patterns before they escalate.

- **Ongoing practice matters**: Preventive skills need to be maintained regularly, not just activated during crisis, because the depressive system can activate with minimal provocation.

This understanding bridges biological and psychological perspectives: neurobiological changes create conditions for easier psychological activation of depression-related thinking and feeling patterns.
