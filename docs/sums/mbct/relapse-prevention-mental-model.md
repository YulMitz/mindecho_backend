## How Depression Relapse Works: A Mental Model

Relapse in depression doesn't start with conscious choice—it emerges through automatic reactivation of thought and feeling patterns that were active during previous depressive episodes. Understanding this process is essential for helping clients recognize early warning signs and intervene skillfully.

### The Relapse Process

When mood begins to lower (even slightly), old patterns of negative thinking activate automatically, like well-worn grooves in the mind. This reactivation isn't deliberate; it happens as a mental habit "running off" on its own. Crucially, relapse isn't about a single negative thought—it's the reactivation of a whole integrated package: characteristic thoughts, feelings, and bodily sensations that interact and reinforce each other through feedback loops.

At the heart of this self-perpetuating state is a particular "view" or mental model: **the self is inadequate, worthless, and blameworthy, and negative thoughts are accurate reflections of reality.** This isn't merely a collection of intellectual ideas—it's the distilled essence of many past experiences, encoded at a level deeper than conscious concepts.

### What Keeps These Patterns Alive

These automatic mental habits persist because they're motivated by a powerful goal: **trying to escape or prevent depression itself.** A person who has experienced severe depression becomes deeply invested in avoiding it happening again, so the mind keeps attempting to "think its way out" of low moods.

However, this strategy backfires. The mind uses rumination—repeatedly dwelling on emotional states, past events, and future consequences—as an attempted solution. At the core is a "discrepancy monitor" that constantly compares current state against desired/expected/feared states. Once activated, this monitor finds mismatches (its job), which fuel more negative mood, which intensifies the attempts to solve the problem through further rumination. The person ends up digging themselves deeper into the very hole they're trying to escape.

### Clinical Implication

Clients need to understand this trap isn't a personal weakness—it's how the mind naturally operates under stress. The solution isn't stronger thinking or willpower, but a different approach entirely: disengaging from the relapse-generating state of mind itself rather than trying to fix it from within.
