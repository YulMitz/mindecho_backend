## Attachment and Aversion: The Automatic Reactions That Undermine Present-Moment Awareness

At the heart of the MBCT model lies a crucial psychological pattern: **the mind is most scattered when it tries to cling to some things and avoid others.** This is not about the content of experience—whether something is pleasant or unpleasant—but about the automatic *reaction* to that content.

### The Two Patterns

**Attachment (Clinging):**
When an experience is judged as pleasant, the automatic reaction is to hold on to it:
- "Don't let this end"
- "How can I have more of this?"
- "Why can't it always be like this?"

A common clinical example: a client has a good meditation session where they felt calm and peaceful. The next day, they sit to meditate and immediately think, "I want to feel like that again." The striving itself prevents the very state they're chasing. Before long, frustration arises—"Why isn't it working today?"—and what was pleasant becomes a source of suffering.

**Aversion (Pushing Away):**
When an experience is judged as unpleasant, the automatic reaction is to get rid of it:
- "I hate feeling like this"
- "Why does this always happen to me?"
- "This shouldn't be here"

This involves judgment layered on top of the original difficulty. A client experiences anxiety (the raw sensation) but then judges the anxiety itself as wrong, which creates a secondary emotional reaction: "I'm weak," "Something's wrong with me," "I can't even handle my own thoughts."

### Why This Matters in Depression Prevention

Both patterns take the person away from direct contact with what is actually happening. **Instead of experiencing the original feeling or thought, the person is now tangled up in the struggle against it or the desperate effort to recreate it.** This struggle itself becomes a driver of rumination and depressive cycles.

### Observing the Patterns in Practice

Meditation and mindfulness exercises naturally reveal these patterns:
- In sitting meditation, when the mind wanders, does the client notice self-judgment ("I'm a terrible meditator") rather than simple noticing and returning to breath? That's aversion at work.
- When pleasant sensations arise in practice, does the client tense up trying to hold them? That's attachment.

The practice isn't about eliminating these reactions. Rather, **the promise of mindfulness is that people will come to find it possible to extricate themselves from automatic reactions in a non-judgmental way when they do occur.**

### A Therapeutic Insight

It's worth reminding clients that **the skills necessary to deal with major life difficulties can be learned on neutral or trivial thoughts and impulses that arise during practice.** Learning to let go of a thought about what to eat for dinner is training for learning to release a rumination about whether one is capable of being a good parent. Both involve the same skill: noticing the thought has captured attention, and gently returning to the present-moment anchor.

### Clinical Conversations Around Attachment

A client who has become very attached to pleasant meditation experiences might say:
> "When I'm sitting, I feel in another world. When anything interrupts, I get really angry. It's like having my ice cream snatched from my hand."

The clinical response acknowledges the pleasure while gently examining the attachment:
- Yes, it's wonderful that something good is happening
- *But* pleasant experiences naturally come and go
- If the practice becomes about achieving that state, then every meditation without it feels like failure
- The deeper practice is to find something stable and trustworthy *regardless* of whether the experience feels good or bad

### Clinical Conversations Around Aversion

When a client hasn't done homework and approaches the session with dread and self-criticism, the aversion pattern is often visible:
- They avoided doing the practice (primary avoidance)
- They then built up fearful thoughts about coming to session (secondary rumination/aversion)
- They're now experiencing shame and self-judgment (tertiary aversion)

Rather than focusing on the homework itself, pause and name the pattern: *"I'm interested in what happened with the thoughts about not doing the homework. Can you notice the feeling in your body right now as you anticipate judgment from me?"* This shifts from content to process—from the failure to the aversion reaction itself.

### The Larger Pattern: "Wanting Things to Be Different"

The theme that connects both attachment and aversion is **wanting things to be different from how they are right now.** 

- Rumination involves judgment: "I don't want to feel like this," which moves away from direct experience into concept-based thinking
- Avoidance involves the same judgment: "This shouldn't be happening"

One of the first and most difficult steps in MBCT is learning that **one can actually stop struggling and be present with what is, and in that fuller space of awareness, clarity emerges about how to respond.**
