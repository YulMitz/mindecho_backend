## Thoughts Are Not Facts: Decentering from Thought Content

### The Core Problem

The human mind constantly generates interpretations, inferences, and running commentary on experience. We don't just perceive events; we immediately construct meaning around them. Usually, we're unaware we're doing this until we get "tricked"—as in the opening exercise about John and the math lesson, where our mental model had to be repeatedly updated.

For people vulnerable to depression, this interpretive machinery becomes a problem. Negative thoughts and self-critical narratives don't just accompany depression—they can trigger and maintain it. The depressed person treats their thoughts as facts: "I'm worthless," "If people knew the real me, they'd reject me," "I'll never get better." Once this propaganda stream starts, it becomes self-reinforcing: the mind notices confirming evidence and filters out contradictory information.

Until cognitive therapy in the 1970s, clinicians assumed negative thoughts were merely symptoms caused by depression. Cognitive therapy revealed that thoughts could *cause and maintain* depression, revolutionizing treatment. But cognitive therapy emphasized changing the *content* of thoughts—testing them against evidence, generating alternative viewpoints, "answering back" the negative thought.

MBCT builds on this but goes deeper: it teaches a different *relationship* to thoughts, whether negative or positive.

### The Shift from "From" to "To"

In traditional cognitive therapy, the goal is to change problematic thoughts: "I'm worthless" becomes "I have strengths and weaknesses, like everyone."

In MBCT, the added layer is learning to shift from relating *from* your thoughts (being caught in them, treating them as facts) to relating *to* your thoughts (observing them as mental events that arise and pass).

This shift happens implicitly during the early weeks of practice. When the client notices their mind wandering during breath meditation and gently brings attention back, they're learning (without being told): "That thought just went by. It doesn't define me. I can let it go." Over weeks of practice, this message sinks in.

### Why This Matters for Relapse Prevention

A client who has learned to change depressive thoughts through evidence-gathering may still be vulnerable. As soon as mood dips and negative thoughts return (which they will), the client may believe them again because the relationship hasn't fundamentally changed. The thought still carries the sense of "this is truth."

When a client can genuinely see thoughts as thoughts—temporary mental events with no special authority—mood dips are far less dangerous. The negative thought arises, but it's recognized: "Oh, here's that old 'I'm useless' tape again. I've heard this before. It's just a thought." The thought no longer triggers the cascade of depression.

### How to Recognize When This Theme Is Central

**Conversational cues:**
- The client describes being "taken over" by a thought or memory
- They speak of thoughts as commands or truths: "I know I'm a failure," "The thought is right—"
- They confuse interpretation with fact: "He didn't call me back, which means he doesn't care" (fact: no call; interpretation: he doesn't care)
- They're stuck in rumination loops despite understanding logically that the thoughts aren't helpful
- They express despair that "no matter how much I challenge these thoughts, they come back"
- They notice negative thoughts have returned and interpret this as meaning the therapy didn't work

### Practical Teaching Approach

**Clarify the Difference Between Content Work and Relationship Work**

You can teach this explicitly: "In the beginning of treatment, cognitive therapy taught us to examine our thoughts like a detective—gather evidence, find contradictions, answer back. That's powerful and valuable. But there's another layer: you can also learn to see thoughts as just thoughts, the way you might watch clouds moving across the sky. The cloud doesn't need to be challenged or changed; you just notice it's there and watch it move on."

**Use Metaphors**

The text offers several powerful ones:

- **Cinema screen**: Imagine sitting in a theater watching an empty screen. Thoughts are like images that appear and disappear. Some vanish as soon as you become aware of them. Some linger. You're the observer; the thoughts are what's being observed.

- **Sky and clouds**: Your mind is like the sky. Thoughts are clouds—some small, some dark and looming, some covering the whole sky. But the sky remains. Clouds move through. The sky isn't disturbed by them, even though they pass across it.

- **Standing behind the waterfall**: When thoughts are very negative and powerful, you can feel swept away by them—"a waterfall of ruminations." The invitation is to step behind the waterfall and watch its force rather than being dragged down by it. You can see the power of the thoughts without being in them.

- **The tape in the mind**: If you watch the same movie enough times, you stop caring about the plot. Recognition is key: "Oh, here's my 'boss doesn't appreciate me' tape again. This is the twelfth time this week." Once you recognize it as a familiar thought pattern (a tape), its grip loosens.

**Practice Observing Thoughts in Meditation**

In the sitting practice, have the client deliberately bring to mind a worry or concern, then practice observing the thoughts that arise without engaging with them. The instruction is to watch, like a cat at a mouse hole, for what exactly the thoughts are and what happens to them. Some vanish; some return; some morph into other thoughts. None of them require action in that moment.

**Address the "But My Thoughts Are True" Objection**

Some thoughts may have truth value (a real problem exists, a real loss occurred). The question isn't whether the thought is true, but whether being completely absorbed in it is helpful. The person can acknowledge "Yes, that's a real problem" while also choosing not to be dragged through rumination about it. The thought can be true AND just a thought to be observed.

**Connect to Body Sensations When Direct Thought Observation Is Difficult**

If the client can't easily "watch" negative thoughts (they're too powerful or the client doesn't think in words), pivot to the physical sensations that accompany the thought. Working with the body gives another foothold for stepping back from the thought. As you soften the physical tension, the thought often naturally loosens its grip.

### The Challenge of Authenticity

There's a subtle pitfall: Using "thoughts are not facts" as a technique to fix or escape from thoughts, rather than truly accepting them. If the client is silently thinking, "Once I realize this is just a thought, it will go away and I'll feel better," they're still operating in fix-it mode. The actual shift is more like: "This thought is here. It's not facts, but it's also not my enemy. I don't have to do anything about it."
