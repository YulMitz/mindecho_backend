## Non-Striving Attitude in Formal Practice

The body scan and breathing practices can feel paradoxical to clients who come to therapy expecting techniques to "fix" their problems. A core teaching challenge is conveying that **the intention is to be fully present with bodily sensations in each moment—not to achieve a special state or get somewhere else.**

### The Paradox Clients Face

Participants often think: *"Why spend 45 minutes a day doing this if I get nothing out of it?"* This reflects a problem-solving mindset where effort should produce measurable results. But mindfulness practice inverts this logic. Effortful striving to achieve outcomes *robs clients of their power for profound learning and changing.*

### How to Work With This in Session

**Reframe "progress" differently.** Acknowledge that progress is *possible* in mindfulness practice, and that a particular kind of effort is necessary—but this effort is not about achieving special states or pushing toward specific goals. The "effort" is the discipline of regular, frequent practice without attachment to particular results.

**Use the seed metaphor.** When a client plants a seed, constantly poking around and interfering with it prevents development. The practice works the same way: give it the right conditions (peace, quiet, regular practice), then *stop trying to make it produce results.* The more you try to influence what it will do, the less it will do.

**Normalize the restlessness.** When clients report that their minds wander or nothing happens, that *is* the practice working. Each time they notice the wandering and gently return attention, they are practicing exactly what they need—the ability to notice mental habits without being swept away by them.

**Address the "striving voice" in real-time.** If a client in session expresses frustration like "I'm not doing this right," pause and ask them to notice: Is that frustration itself a thought worth examining? Can they observe it with curiosity rather than act on it? This demonstrates the different "mode" of being the practice cultivates.
