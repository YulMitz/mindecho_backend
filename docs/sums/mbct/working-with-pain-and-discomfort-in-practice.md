## Working with Pain: Attention Instead of Avoidance

When clients encounter pain or strong discomfort during the body scan, their automatic response is often to think about it, worry about it, or try to make it go away. This mental activity—the commentary and resistance—often becomes more problematic than the sensation itself.

**The typical pattern:**

Louise notices tension in her chest and shoulders. Instead of simply observing the sensation, she begins an inner monologue: *Why am I so tense? Why can't I do anything right? What about stress at work? The tension with my husband and children?* The original sensation has become a hook for rumination and self-blame.

**The teaching opportunity:**

When a client reports this pattern, the instructor doesn't ask them to relax or dismiss the pain. Instead, the invitation is to notice the *difference* between sensation and the story about sensation.

"When you noticed the tension, what went through your mind?"

This question helps the client see that they have added conceptual layers—interpretation, judgment, worry—to a relatively simple physical experience.
