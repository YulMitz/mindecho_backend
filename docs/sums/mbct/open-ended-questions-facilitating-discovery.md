## Open-Ended Questions: Inviting Rather Than Suggesting

In MBCT, how the instructor asks about experience shapes what clients discover. The difference between closed and open-ended questions is subtle but consequential.

**Closed questions feel natural but limit discovery:**

"Did anyone feel tired?" "Did your mind wander?" "Did you feel relaxed?"

These questions naturally produce yes/no responses and can lead clients toward the answer the therapist expects. For someone learning to notice their own experience, closed questions can inadvertently reinforce the idea that there's a "right" experience to have.

**Open-ended questions evoke exploration:**

"Does anyone want to say anything about their experience?"
"What kind of thoughts went through your mind?"
"Where did those thoughts take you?"

These invitations allow clients to articulate what actually happened, including unexpected associations, surprising insights, and the wandering nature of mind. The therapist then deepens inquiry based on what the client has shared, rather than leading from assumptions.

**The pedagogical power:**

When a client says, "I was thinking about how strange it is that something dried up could taste so good. That made me think of hot sand, then holidays with my parents when I was small," the therapist can reflect: "So the task was to focus on the raisin, but the mind had its own agenda. It went from the raisin to reflections on taste, to hot sand, to your parents. That's a lovely example of how the mind has its own agenda."

This reflection is more powerful than the therapist saying it first, because the client has *discovered* it through their own attention.

**Why this matters for MBCT:**

The program aims to shift clients from automatic, reactive patterns to intentional awareness and choice. If the instructor directs clients toward predetermined insights, it subtly reinforces the "doing" mode—compliance, achievement, getting the answer right. Open-ended inquiry embodies the "being" mode: patient curiosity about what is actually present.
