## Dissolving Success/Failure in Practice

People who have experienced depression often bring perfectionistic, achievement-oriented attitudes to MBCT. They ask: "Am I doing this right?" "Is it working?" "Am I relaxed enough yet?" This mindset, while understandable, can undermine the very shift the practice aims to cultivate.

**The core tension:**

Depression involves entrenched patterns of self-judgment, comparison, and striving for approval. These habits naturally transfer to new activities, including meditation. A client thinks: *If I can't do the body scan perfectly, I'm failing. If the practice doesn't make me feel better, it's not working.*

The irony is that this goal-oriented, evaluative stance is part of the problem MBCT addresses. Paradoxically, trying hard to relax often produces more tension. Trying to empty the mind often produces more thoughts.

**The instruction:**

From the beginning, the teacher emphasizes:
- "It is important not to try too hard"
- "We are not trying to achieve any special state"
- "We are not even trying to relax"
- "You do not have to enjoy it; you just have to do it"

Later: "Sometimes the practice may bring up discomfort or boredom. If this happens, it does not mean you have failed."

**Working with judgment when it arises:**

When a client reports that they "couldn't keep still," or "felt anxious the whole time," or "kept doing it wrong," the instructor's response is crucial. Rather than reassuring them ("You're doing fine"), the instructor invites curiosity:

"That sense that something should be different than it is—that's very important to notice. Become aware of that feeling, that 'I should be doing this right.' Acknowledge it, and then let it go. Just deal with what actually is in this moment."

This models the shift from judgment to awareness, which is the heart of the work.

**Specific interventions:**

When a client reports physical fidgetiness or restlessness during practice:
- Acknowledge it: "That's your experience in that moment. It's not wrong."
- Invite noticing: "Can you feel the urge to move? Can you notice it as a sensation?"
- Offer choice: "With awareness, you may be able to feel the urge and then decide whether to act on it or not."

This turns a "failure" into an opportunity to practice disengaging from habitual reactions.

**Why this matters for depression prevention:**

One of the core patterns that perpetuates depression is rumination about performance and self-worth. By removing success/failure language from practice, MBCT interrupts that cycle. Clients learn that they can't "fail" at noticing what's present. Awareness itself is the goal, not a particular emotional state or outcome.
