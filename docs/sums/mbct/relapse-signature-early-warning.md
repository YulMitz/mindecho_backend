## Identifying Your Relapse Signature

After multiple episodes of depression, the brain develops patterns. The process of falling into depression can become increasingly autonomous—it gathers pace faster, seems to escalate without clear external triggers, and follows a recognizable (to that person) trajectory. This is actually useful information. Rather than wait until you're in full crisis, you can learn to recognize the early signs that depression is returning and intervene early.

Your "relapse signature" is the unique constellation of warning signs that *your mind and body* produce when depression is beginning to take hold. It's not a generic list of depression symptoms. It's your personal pattern—the early shifts in mood, behavior, sleep, energy, or thought that *you* notice, that signal to you that something is changing.

### Common relapse signatures

While each person's signature is unique, clients commonly report some or all of these early warning signs:

- **Mood/irritability shifts:** Becoming easily irritated or frustrated, or a subtle lowering of mood that's hard to articulate
- **Social withdrawal:** Decreasing contact with friends and family; just "not wanting to see people"; canceling plans
- **Sleep changes:** Sleeping too much, sleeping too little, or loss of the refreshing quality of sleep
- **Eating changes:** Loss of appetite, eating differently, changes in eating patterns
- **Energy crash:** Getting exhausted more easily, tiredness that feels qualitatively different from normal tiredness
- **Loss of activities:** Giving up exercise, dropping hobbies, stopping activities you normally enjoy
- **Task avoidance:** Not wanting to deal with practical matters (opening mail, paying bills, making appointments); postponing deadlines; letting things pile up
- **Thought patterns:** Noticing that familiar hopeless or self-critical thoughts are returning; that your mind is becoming more pessimistic
- **Physical sensations:** Tension returning to certain parts of the body; heaviness; feeling physically depleted

### Building your personal list

The exercise in the class asks people to make their own list: What changes do I notice in myself when depression is beginning? It's important to do this when mood is stable—when you have perspective. If you wait until depression has already taken hold, the hopelessness makes it hard to believe that early intervention is possible.

Working in pairs or small groups often helps. Partners can reflect back what they're hearing: "So when your mood starts to shift, you tend to isolate more. Is that right?" Sometimes hearing it reflected helps clarify the pattern. Family members or close friends can sometimes add insight: "I've noticed that when you stop going to yoga, it's usually a sign that things are shifting."

### The balance: Awareness without hypervigilance

There's an important balance to strike. You want to be aware of your relapse signatures so you can respond early. But you don't want to become hypervigilant—constantly monitoring yourself and panicking at every small change. Normal life includes fluctuations in sleep, appetite, mood, energy. Not every dip means depression is returning.

The goal is to know your patterns well enough that when *your particular constellation* of signs appears—when multiple things shift together, or when a particular sign that's meaningful for *you* shows up—you recognize it and can take action. It's like knowing your own baseline so well that deviations become noticeable without requiring constant surveillance.

### Obstacles to early detection

One major obstacle: hopelessness and denial. Even when you've identified your relapse signatures, when they actually start to appear and mood is beginning to lower, the depressed mind can whisper: *These signs don't really mean anything. I'm overreacting. There's no point trying the practices anyway; they didn't work before and they won't work now. I'm back to square one.*

This is why it's powerful to involve others. You might write down your early warning signs and share them with a partner, family member, or therapist. You ask them to watch for these signs and gently point them out when they notice them. When your own judgment is compromised by emerging depression, having someone outside say "Hey, I've noticed you haven't gone to yoga in three weeks, and you're being more withdrawn—remember what you said about that?" can break through the denial and hopelessness.

### From signature to action plan

Identifying relapse signatures is only the first half of the work. The second half is: *What will I do when I notice these signs?* This is the action plan—the subject of the next skill. But the premise is sound: the earlier you can catch the turning point, the easier it is to intervene and prevent a full relapse.
