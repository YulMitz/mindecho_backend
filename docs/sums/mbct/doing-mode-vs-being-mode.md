## Two Modes of Mind: Understanding Doing and Being

The mind operates in distinct modes, analogous to different gears in a car. Each mode has a characteristic function and activates different mental processes. Understanding these two primary modes—doing and being—is central to understanding why MBCT works.

### "Doing" Mode: The Goal-Driven Mind

**Trigger**: Doing mode activates whenever the mind registers a discrepancy between how things are (or will become) and how we wish them to be, or how they "should" be.

**Characteristics**:
- Goal-oriented: driven to reduce the gap between present/anticipated state and desired state
- Narrow focus: attention devoted to discrepancies between desired and actual states
- Constant monitoring: "How am I doing relative to my goal?"
- Emphasis on discrepancy-based processing: evaluating experiences primarily in terms of whether they move toward or away from goals
- Time-displaced: the mind travels backward to the past or forward to the future, analyzing previous failures or computing future consequences
- Result: the person is not actually "here" in the present moment

**Subjective experience**: A recurring sense of unsatisfactoriness caused by the focus on mismatches. The mind endlessly manipulates ideas about "how things are" versus "how they should be," hoping to find a way to reduce the gap. Thoughts feel "real" rather than being recognized as mental events.

**The central problem**: In doing mode, thoughts become the "currency" of problem-solving. When the problem cannot be directly solved (e.g., "I feel bad and want to feel better"), the mind keeps running the same loops, dwelling on past problems and feared futures. For unresolvable goals—like "be happy" or "never feel sad again"—this mode becomes self-perpetuating.

### "Being" Mode: Acceptance and Presence

**Characteristics**:
- Non-goal-oriented: not motivated to achieve particular goals
- Allowing and accepting: open to "what is" without pressure to change it
- No constant monitoring: no need to evaluate progress toward goals
- Full sensory presence: attention available for the full depth, width, and richness of present experience
- Time-focused: the mind has "nothing to do, nowhere to go"—processing dedicated to moment-by-moment experience
- Direct experience: intimate, unmediated contact with the present moment

**Relationship to thoughts and feelings**: Like sounds or other aspects of experience, thoughts and feelings are recognized as passing events in the mind that arise, become aware, and pass away. This represents a "decentered" perspective—thoughts and feelings are observed rather than taken as literal truths about reality.

**Critical feature—Disconnection from action**: Feelings don't automatically trigger action sequences. Unpleasant emotions don't immediately activate habitual patterns of escape or avoidance. Thoughts like "I'm a failure" don't necessarily link to corresponding behaviors. This requires greater tolerance for uncomfortable emotional states without immediately trying to fix them.

### Key Distinction: A Practical Example

**Doing mode example**: Washing dishes with the goal of finishing quickly to move to the next task. If interrupted, frustration arises because the goal is thwarted.

**Being mode example**: Accepting that dishes must be done and approaching the activity for its own sake, in its own time. An interruption simply presents a choice about what to do next—no frustration because there was no time-based goal.

Both modes can accompany any activity (or inactivity). The difference is the mind's relationship to the activity, not the activity itself.

### Why Doing Mode Perpetuates Depression

- Discrepancies automatically trigger negative feeling
- In depression, many discrepancies have no clear solution (internal states like mood)
- The mind loops endlessly, maintaining the very emotional discomfort it's trying to escape
- The ruminative processing that results consumes mental resources, preventing entry into other modes
- "Automatic pilot" keeps the person unaware of the process itself

### Why Being Mode Breaks the Cycle

- Non-discrepancy-based processing removes fuel from the negative feedback loop
- Awareness deployed to present-moment experience is incompatible with rumination about past/future
- Acceptance of "what is" undercuts the motivation driving automatic habits (which are fueled by aversion)
- The mind cannot simultaneously occupy both modes—they require exclusive access to the same mental resources

### Clinical Implication

The core therapeutic shift in MBCT is teaching clients to recognize when they're stuck in doing mode (especially the "driven" variant characterized by trying to escape depression) and to intentionally shift into being mode—not as a relaxation technique or special state, but as a fundamentally different way of processing experience. Regular practice makes this shift increasingly accessible and automatic.
