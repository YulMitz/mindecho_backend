## Session 8: Program Closure and Consolidation

The final session of MBCT requires deliberate attention to closure—marking the completion of 8 weeks of work, honoring what participants have learned, and preparing them for the transition to independent practice.

### Reflection on the Journey

Session 8 begins with a structured "looking back" exercise. Participants spend time—alone or in pairs—reflecting on questions such as:

- Why did you come originally? What were your expectations and why did you stay?
- What did you want/hope for?
- What did you get out of coming, if anything? What did you learn?
- What were the costs to you?
- What are your biggest blocks/obstacles to continuing?
- What strategies might help you not get stuck?

This retrospective exercise is more than nostalgic. It helps participants consolidate their learning by articulating it. When asked to reflect on costs as well as benefits, they develop realistic expectations going forward—this wasn't a magical cure, but a set of tools and insights.

### Written Self-Assessment

Participants are asked to rate, on a scale from 1 to 10, how important the program has been to them. More importantly, they write why they gave that rating.

The responses often reveal profound shifts. Common themes include:

- Discovery of an "inner, safe place" where one can be without criticism
- Learning that "I have a choice" about which thoughts to follow
- Recognition that acceptance of difficult feelings, rather than fighting them, opens possibilities
- Reduction of shame about having been depressed
- Discovery of inner strength
- Ability to stay present rather than being consumed by worry about future or guilt about past

Some participants report that meditation initially brought up "strange emotions" that worried them, but they came to understand these were long-repressed feelings that needed to be felt in order to move forward.

### The Memento Ritual

Many instructors give each participant a small object—a marble, stone, or bead—and lead the group in a final meditation examining it closely, similar to the mindful eating of a raisin in the first session.

This object serves multiple purposes:
- A concrete reminder that they have been in the group
- A symbol of the 8 weeks of effort and work
- A reminder of the people they shared the experience with
- A prompt to continue the process they started
- An invitation to "discover a way of living alongside the damaged aspects of themselves, and to hold before them the possibility of responding to their own fragility in an ever more gentle and more caring way"

### Linking to Future Practice

Before the session ends, there must be time to hear what type of practice each person has decided to continue. Recording these plans on a board helps participants see the range of approaches and know they have choices. Some commit to daily 30-minute sits; others plan to weave the breathing space throughout their day and do formal practice on weekends.

The instructor's role is not to prescribe but to ensure plans are realistic and rooted in positive reasons—why *they* want to continue, not why they *should*.
